## Summary

The Commercial Affairs Department's CAD Report 2024 describes Singapore's participation in Operation First Light 2024. It states that the INTERPOL-coordinated anti-fraud operation ran from March to May 2024 and links it to a Singapore-Malaysia investigation into a money-laundering cell that used Singapore bank accounts, including arrests in Johor and a later extradition to Singapore.

# and finance 1CAD REPORT 2024 COMMISSIONER’S MESSAGE

The Commercial Affairs Department (CAD) of the Singapore Police Force (SPF) operates at the

forefront of financial crime prevention, detection, and enforcement, thereby reinforcing Singapore’s

reputation as a trusted global business hub. Leveraging advanced investigative capabilities,

the CAD serves as the guardian of Singapore’s commercial integrity in the ever-evolving financial

crime landscape.

UNWAVERING COMMITMENT TO ENFORCEMENT

Notwithstanding the enhanced public awareness campaigns and

robust enforcement measures, scams continue to pose significant

challenges to our society. In February 2024, Singapore continued to strengthen our legal framework against scams and cyber threats, with the operationalisation of the Online Criminal Harms

Act. This empowered the Police to direct online platforms to

restrict Singapore users’ exposure to online criminal activities and implement upstream preventive measures.

With this enhanced framework, the CAD has continued to work closely with its partners to take comprehensive enforcement action

against financial criminals. The CAD had also further expanded its

partnership network to encompass more than 120 institutions, including

financial institutions, remittance service providers, cryptocurrency

exchanges, INTERPOL, foreign law enforcement agencies and

Financial Intelligence Units. Through these partnerships and direct communication channels, the department is better positioned to facilitate the swift freezing of accounts and recovery of funds to mitigate victim losses. A notable success was the swift recovery of over US$40 million in a Business Email Compromise Scam through rapid intervention and collaboration with Timor-Leste authorities in July 2024, which resulted in the arrest of seven suspects.

As part of these strengthened partnerships, the CAD, in collaboration

with six major financial institutions, prevented potential scam losses

exceeding S$420 million in 2024 and disabled more than 8,580

suspicious bank accounts through Project A.S.T.R.O., which leverages

Robotic Process Automation technology to proactively identify and

alert potential scam victims.

The CAD continues to conduct rigorous enforcement action and

secure stiff deterrent sentences against those who seek to abuse our

financial systems for illicit gain. This includes significant convictions

across a spectrum of financial crimes, including fraud involving public

and private institutions, securities fraud, and money laundering.

The CAD’s commitment to tackling complex financial crimes was

exemplified through several landmark cases. As further detailed

within the report, these included one of Singapore’s largest trade

financing fraud cases, which led to a significant conviction; our

largest money laundering case to date, resulting in billion-dollar

assets being surrendered to the State; and a sophisticated cross-

border gold smuggling operation, disrupted through collaboration with the Korean and Japanese authorities.

Through sustained enforcement operations and substantial deterrent sentences, the CAD has demonstrated its capability to combat

sophisticated financial crimes across different sectors and jurisdictions,

thereby underscoring our determination to ensure white-collar criminals face the full consequences of their actions.

ENHANCING GLOBAL PARTNERSHIPS

The financial crime landscape has transformed dramatically, with

criminal activities increasingly spanning multiple jurisdictions, enabled by global connectivity and digital innovation. Addressing such sophisticated threats requires collaborative approaches, as our success hinges on global partnerships. Only through coordinated

efforts with our foreign counterparts can we ensure that criminals

find no haven and that their ill-gotten gains are swiftly recovered.

Beyond Singapore, the CAD has forged close partnerships with

international law enforcement agencies. A significant milestone

was the launch of the cross-border Anti-Scam collaboration platform, FRONTIER+, in October 2024, bringing together law enforcement agencies from 11 jurisdictions, including Singapore, Hong Kong, Malaysia, Thailand, the Maldives, South Korea, Australia, Macau SAR, Canada, Indonesia, and Brunei. This CAD-led initiative positions Singapore as a regional centre of excellence for coordinating cross-border investigations, forming a key pillar of the

SPF’s strategy to combat scams and transnational financial crimes.

In 2024, the CAD spearheaded coordinated enforcement operations with multiple agencies and partners, both domestically and internationally. Such operations have strengthened the CAD’s partnerships with its counterparts and fostered closer collaboration internationally.

The CAD remains committed to supporting foreign law enforcement

agencies. In 2024, we facilitated the first successful extradition

request from Hong Kong’s Securities and Futures Commission to Singapore, demonstrating Singapore’s strong commitment to

international cooperation in combating financial crimes.

The CAD’s extensive network of international partnerships forms the

cornerstone of its approach to combating transnational financial

crime. By fostering these relationships through active participation in global dialogues and knowledge sharing, the CAD continues to enhance its collective expertise and capabilities.

PARTNERING THE COMMUNITY AND STAKEHOLDERS

Safeguarding Singapore requires collective effort and vigilance

from all sectors of society. Through extensive public education and community outreach initiatives, the CAD works tirelessly to

empower our community with knowledge about emerging financial

crimes and evolving scam typologies. The expansion of the Inter-Agency Forum on Countering Financial Fraud to 40 participating government agencies has strengthened our collective

prevention efforts.

Beyond government partnerships, the CAD maintains its collaboration with key partners and stakeholders. The Suspicious

Transaction Reporting Office also remains committed to supporting

local agencies by providing crucial financial intelligence, and in

doing so, strengthening our nation’s collective ability to combat

financial crime and money laundering.

Through these targeted engagements and partnerships, we continue to build a more resilient community that is better equipped to

identify and prevent financial crimes. The whole-of-society approach,

combining prevention through education with strong enforcement

actions, remains fundamental to safeguarding Singapore’s financial

integrity for generations to come.

THE ROAD AHEAD

Through unwavering dedication and robust capabilities, the CAD

continues to excel in its mission to combat financial crimes at

every level. Looking forward, the development of NAVIGATE

(National AML Verification Interface for Government Agencies

Threat Evaluation) demonstrates our commitment to enhancing

our detection capabilities through improved data sharing.

The exceptional commitment and dedication demonstrated by the

CAD officers position us strongly to address emerging challenges

in the evolving financial crime landscape. Their persistent efforts

and expertise will remain instrumental in maintaining Singapore’s

standing as a trusted and secure financial and business hub.

# DIRECTOR’S REPORT

In 2024, the Commercial Affairs Department (CAD) dealt with numerous complex financial crime threats

arising from Singapore’s position as an open economy and its status as a global financial centre.

These threats – particularly in the areas of scams, money laundering and complex fraud – evolved

significantly through the convergence of traditional and digital criminal methodologies. The sophisticated

deployment of technology by criminal syndicates required us to continuously adapt and enhance our enforcement strategies. Through intelligence-led operations, strengthened international partnerships

and advanced analytical capabilities, we stayed true to our mission to prevent, deter and detect financial

crimes while safeguarding Singapore’s position as a trusted financial and business hub.

DISRUPTING SCAM SYNDICATES THROUGH COORDINATED ENFORCEMENT AND INTERNATIONAL COLLABORATION

Scams remained a key threat to Singapore’s financial and

digital landscape in 2024, with increasingly coordinated and sophisticated syndicates targeting the public through phishing, malware and impersonation tactics. The CAD

intensified our efforts to detect and disrupt these operations

through targeted enforcement and cross-border coordination.

Our Project A.S.T.R.O. initiative demonstrated the

effectiveness of public-private partnerships in combating

scams. Through collaboration with six major financial

institutions, this initiative achieved significant results in

2024 – preventing potential scam losses exceeding S$420 million and disabling more than 8,580 suspicious

bank accounts. Project A.S.T.R.O.’s success underscores

the importance of leveraging technological solutions and

strategic partnerships in our fight against scams.

Strengthening Cross-border Enforcement and Asset Recovery

Robust enforcement and international cooperation through several major operations ensured that criminals and their illicit proceeds found no safe haven here or overseas.

We conducted a joint operation with the Royal Malaysia

Police in August 2024, resulting in the arrest of 12 Malaysian

nationals in Johor Bahru, Malaysia, for laundering scam proceeds through Singapore bank accounts. One of the suspects was extradited and charged in Singapore.

In another case, our swift intervention led to the seizure of

approximately €11 million (S$15.4 million) linked to a cross-

border investment fraud. The case began when the Suspicious

Transaction Reporting Office (STRO) detected suspicious

transfers totalling €29 million to a Singapore-based payment account between July and August 2024. Through rapid

collaboration with foreign Financial Intelligence Units (FIUs),

we secured crucial intelligence that enabled investigators to decisively freeze the remaining funds in November 2024. The funds were traced to two companies whose owners were involved in operating an unauthorised online investment platform that had allegedly defrauded victims of approximately €100 million.

Our enhanced capabilities in rapid cross-border asset recovery were demonstrated in July 2024, when swift action by the

Anti-Scam Centre (ASC) led to the recovery of US$39 million

in a Business Email Compromise (BEC) scam involving a

multi-national commodity firm in Singapore. By rapidly deploying

multiple international cooperation channels including the

INTERPOL-Global Rapid Intervention of Payments

mechanism, Asset Recovery Interagency Network – Asia

Pacific, Egmont Group of FIUs, and collaboration with

Timor-Leste authorities, we successfully intercepted the funds within 24 hours. The joint investigation also led to the arrest of seven suspects and recovery of an additional US$2.2 million in cash in Timor-Leste.

Further successes in cross-border asset recovery included the interception of US$5.4 million in September 2024 through collaboration with US authorities in another BEC case,

and the recovery of US$132,500 in August 2024 through

coordination with UAE authorities. These cases demonstrate our strengthened international partnerships and enhanced capabilities in rapid cross-border asset recovery.

These outcomes were made possible through proactive

financial intelligence from the STRO, operational agility, and

close coordination with financial institutions and international

partners to freeze accounts and prevent further losses to victims.

A notable breakthrough in our international operations came through our investigation of South Korean national Kim Taek Hoon, uncovering a complex gold smuggling operation worth at least S$1.5 billion. This case, achieved through close

collaboration with the Korean National Police Agency,

Japanese authorities and INTERPOL, demonstrated

Singapore’s commitment to preventing the abuse of its precious metals sector.

Enhancing Regional Collaboration Through FRONTIER+

A significant milestone in our cross-border anti-scam efforts

was the formalisation of FRONTIER+ (Funds Recovery

Operations & Networks Team, Inspiring Effective Resolution

Plus) in October 2024. This CAD-led initiative brings

together law enforcement agencies from 11 countries including Singapore, Hong Kong, Malaysia, Thailand, the Maldives, South Korea, Australia, Macau SAR, Canada, Indonesia and Brunei, enabling timely cross-jurisdictional cooperation

in dismantling syndicates and disrupting scam flows

through these partner countries.

The impact of this collaboration was powerfully demonstrated between April and May 2025, when Operation FRONTIER+ resulted in the arrest of over 1,800 suspects across Asia. In Singapore alone, 106 individuals were arrested, with enforcement actions leading to the seizure of S$7.7 million

and the freezing of 714 bank accounts linked to over 1,300

scam cases. The operation exemplified the impact of

timely intervention across multiple countries, at speed and at scale.

These efforts demonstrate Singapore’s leadership in

regional anti-scam enforcement while reinforcing the

resilience of our financial ecosystem through prevention

and deterrence. DISMANTLING MONEY LAUNDERING NETWORKS AND HOLDING ENABLERS ACCOUNTABLE THROUGH STRONGER LAWS

While strengthening our fight against scams in 2024,

we also intensified efforts against another critical threat –

sophisticated money laundering networks. Our comprehensive approach focused on dismantling criminal networks, prosecuting professional enablers, and strengthening legislative frameworks.

Major Money Laundering Operations and Enforcement Actions

The year marked a significant breakthrough with the

dismantling of a S$3 billion money laundering operation.

Following the 2023 raid and arrest of 10 suspects, the CAD

led SPF’s investigations into the remaining 17 suspects who

were out of Singapore. Our investigations for 15 suspects were concluded by November 2024, resulting in them being barred from returning to Singapore and a forfeiture of their Singapore assets.

Through decisive enforcement action, approximately S$2.8 billion in assets from these 25 criminals were ordered

to be surrendered to the State as of 31 December 2024,

comprising monies in bank accounts, real estate properties, vehicles, and other luxury items.

Targeting Enablers

The S$3 billion money laundering case also demonstrated the

CAD’s comprehensive approach in targeting both principal

offenders and their professional enablers. The CAD also charged

two former bank relationship managers who had forged documents to conceal the true nature of their client’s assets

and money laundering offences. Additionally, a former

personal driver received a jail sentence for lying to conceal and dispose of S$8 million worth of luxury vehicles linked to a fugitive in this case.

This approach of targeting enablers was also evident in

other significant cases. In one instance, we secured the

conviction of a corporate director for facilitating the laundering of nearly S$4.8 million in scam proceeds through companies under her control. By knowingly registering entities and opening bank accounts that were used to move illicit funds from a U.S.-based phishing operation, she had allowed her corporate vehicle to be exploited for criminal purposes.

These outcomes reinforce the CAD’s commitment to take

down the entire ecosystem behind financial crime, not just

those who handle illicit funds but also those who enabled,

concealed, or benefited from them. We will spare no effort to

investigate criminals who seek to exploit our financial system.

Strengthening Legislative Framework

To strengthen our operational capabilities and enforcement outcomes, 2024 saw the implementation of several key legislative reforms. The Anti-Money Laundering and Other

Matters Act, which came into effect in November 2024,

significantly improved our ability to pursue cross-border

money laundering cases linked to foreign predicate offences.

This was complemented by the Online Criminal Harms Act implemented in February 2024, which provided new tools

to combat online financial crimes. Further strengthening our

regulatory framework, two new Codes of Practice were

implemented in June 2024, requiring designated online service providers to implement upstream measures for preventing and disrupting scams.

These legislative changes align with Singapore’s National Anti-Money Laundering Strategy, published in October 2024. The strategy outlines seven key national priorities to

strengthen the country’s defences against illicit finance,

focusing on enhanced supervision, intelligence sharing, and

public-private collaboration to combat financial crime

more effectively.

Enhanced International Collaboration

International cooperation reached new heights with the

successful implementation of the INTERPOL Silver Notice/ Diffusion tool. This innovative mechanism enhances member

countries’ ability to coordinate cross-border asset seizure

and confiscation efforts, representing a crucial step forward

in combating transnational financial crimes.

Notable engagements included the United Kingdom’s National Crime Agency delegation visit and expanded collaboration with regional partners. These partnerships proved crucial in

several cross-border operations and asset recovery efforts. Together, these measures support the CAD’s ongoing efforts

to maintain Singapore’s position as a trusted and resilient

global financial hub, ensuring the country’s Anti-Money

Laundering and Countering the Financing of Terrorism

(AML/CFT) framework remains adaptive and forward-looking

in a rapidly evolving threat landscape.

PROSECUTING HIGH-IMPACT COMPLEX FINANCIAL CRIMES

The CAD’s efforts to tackle complex financial fraud in 2024

yielded significant outcomes across various sectors, reinforcing

safeguards for market integrity and investor confidence.

Major Corporate Fraud

We secured a landmark conviction against Lim Oon Kuin,

founder of Hin Leong Trading (Pte) Ltd, who was

sentenced to 17.5 years imprisonment on 18 November 2024. Following a trial of more than 60 days, Lim was convicted of two charges of cheating and one charge of instigating forgery for deceiving a bank into disbursing approximately US$111 million through fraudulent discounting applications. The investigation revealed that Lim had directed his employees to fabricate two oil sales contracts

with China Aviation Oil (Singapore) Corporation Ltd and

Unipec Singapore Pte Ltd, supported by forged

documentation. The case, widely regarded as one of

Singapore’s most serious trade financing frauds,

demonstrated the CAD’s resolve to address large-scale corporate wrongdoing at the highest levels.

Investment Fraud Schemes

Another significant prosecution involved Ng Yu Zhi (Ng),

director of Envy Asset Management and former director of Envy Global Trading. Ng orchestrated a S$1.46 billion

fraudulent nickel trading scheme that affected nearly

1,000 investors. The investigation revealed a classic Ponzi

scam where funds raised were not used for nickel trades as claimed but instead diverted to pay earlier investors and

Strengthening Prevention Through Stakeholder Engagement

While maintaining strong enforcement actions, the CAD recognised that prevention through education and

engagement was equally crucial to combat financial

crime. We maintained a strong focus on prevention through enhanced stakeholder engagement across multiple sectors.

The Inter-Agency Forum on Countering Financial Fraud

against Government Assistance Schemes saw significant

expansion, growing from 25 to 40 participating government agencies. This expansion enabled broader sharing of risk management strategies and investigation insights across the public sector.

Our engagement with the charity sector yielded notable results through targeted initiatives. Working with Sport Singapore, we delivered a targeted webinar reaching over 90 leaders of National Sports Associations, focusing on fraud risk mitigation measures. We also engaged with religious organisations through the National Steering Committee on Racial and Religious Harmony, addressing

specific fraud risks faced by religious institutions.

Through focused outreach sessions with individual agencies, including the Building and Construction Authority, National Environment Agency, and Agency for Integrated Care, we

addressed sector-specific vulnerabilities and enhanced

fraud detection capabilities. These engagements demonstrate our commitment to a preventive approach in protecting

Singapore’s financial integrity.

These preventive measures, combined with our robust enforcement actions, have strengthened Singapore’s

defences against financial crime. However, as criminal

methodologies continue to evolve, we must remain forward-looking in our approach.

As financial crimes continue to evolve, particularly at the

intersection of traditional and digital channels, the CAD remains committed to strengthening our capabilities. We are

developing NAVIGATE (National AML Verification Interface

for Government Agencies Threat Evaluation), a platform that

will strengthen our detection capabilities through enhanced data sharing. By enabling law enforcement and relevant agencies to cross-reference their databases seamlessly, NAVIGATE will help identify suspicious entities more

swiftly and efficiently.

The successful implementation of the Law Enforcement Agencies Anti-Money Laundering Strategy 2024, combined

with significant operational achievements and legislative

enhancements, positions the CAD to address future

challenges in the fight against financial crime. Through

our sustained investigation and prosecution of financial

global financial centre. Working closely with our partners,

# COMPLEX COMMERCIAL AND FINANCIAL CRIMES 7CADREPORT2024

The commercial and financial crime landscape, to borrow a trite phrase, is constantly evolving and

continues to present significant challenges. Digital assets, once seen as novel, are gaining mainstream

acceptance, and thus also creating new pathways for fraud and the channelling of illicit monies. The pervasive use of social media and messaging platforms has enabled criminals to reach and defraud vastly larger audiences in double-quick time. The increasing sophistication of commercial and

financial fraud constantly tests the boundaries of existing criminal law frameworks, and our ability to

protect the integrity of Singapore as a global financial centre and commercial hub.

Despite the challenges, we have investigated and resolved several high-profile cases in the past year.

We remain committed to investigating and preventing similar crimes, through a combination of rigorous investigative work, international collaboration and stakeholder engagement. We also continuously work to strengthen our investigative capabilities.

ENFORCEMENT ACTION HIGHLIGHTS

The perpetrators of two relatively large investment frauds were convicted during the year. In 2020 and 2021, Kam Boon Hee Kenneth was charged for cheating more than S$16 million from “benefactors” who thought they were lending money to someone with an excellent forex trading

record. After an 18-day trial involving 63 out of 340 of

such cheating charges, he was convicted and sentenced to 10 years imprisonment.

Ong Kai Min (Ong) was already touting artificial intelligence (AI)

before ChatGPT made AI mainstream. Through his company,

Singapore Index Trading Institute Pte Ltd, Ong cheated

investors of more than S$4 million, by claiming AI would

conduct trades in financial instruments on their behalf. On

27 August 2025, Ong pleaded guilty and was convicted on

eight amalgamated charges of cheating involving US$537,000

and more than S$2,700,000. Sixteen other charges will be taken into consideration for sentencing. On 19 September

2025, Ong was sentenced to 93 months imprisonment. The conviction of Pang Yuheng, a former Wealth Planning

Manager with DBS Bank Ltd, for operating a fictitious fixed

deposit scheme that defrauded clients of S$348,000,

highlighted the continued need for vigilance against internal fraud. In a similar vein, Charn Sze Choong, a former claims

assessor at Prudential, received seven years and four

months imprisonment for submitting fraudulent insurance claims totalling S$1.95 million, which demonstrated the serious consequences of breaching professional trust.

The mortgage industry saw sophisticated fraud schemes involving multiple parties, including sellers, buyers, property agents and a conveyancing lawyer who had acted in

conspiracy to inflate the sale prices of landed properties.

The prosecution of Sufandi Bin Ahmad and his co-conspirators revealed an elaborate mortgage fraud operation that resulted in bank losses exceeding S$1.79 million. The High Court’s decision to increase sentences for two masterminds to nine years and 11 years imprisonment

respectively reflected the gravity of such orchestrated

deceptions in Singapore’s property market.

We continue to take a strong stance towards the protection of public funds and strive to take strong enforcement action on fraud involving such funds. This year, a total of 14 persons were charged for their involvement in defrauding the government. We also secured the conviction of Tan Chee Boon for defrauding government grant schemes administered by the then Agri-Food and Veterinary Authority and the conviction of Choo Chiang Wei for procurement fraud against the Ministry of National Development.

We remain committed to taking firm and decisive action against

those who compromise the integrity of our capital markets. This year, current and former members of the board of directors

of Raffles Education Corporation Limited (now known as Raffles

Education Limited) were prosecuted for disclosure breaches.

Their charges were in relation to the SGX Mainboard-listed company’s failure to disclose material information on repayment demands of more than RM400 million.

Our earlier investigative efforts into securities market misconduct

also yielded positive enforcement outcomes. One such outcome is the nine month imprisonment term meted out

to former dealer Oh Wee Hian (Oh) for defrauding Contracts

for Difference (CFD) provider, IG Asia Pte Ltd on a total of

215 occasions. Oh entered fictitious or “spoofed” share

orders for six SGX-listed counters, with the aim of artificially

influencing the prices of CFDs linked to these counters. By

doing so, Oh was able to buy CFDs low from, and sell them high to, IG Asia. Within just eight months, he had reaped more than S$250,000, which, by far, is the largest amount

of illicit profits involved in analogous cases. We also launched several high-profile investigations this year.

In August 2024, we commenced a probe into Evergreen Group

Holdings’ Chief Executive Officer Yong Khung Lin concerning

the company’s fund-raising practices through promissory notes. In September 2024, we commenced investigation into

the collapse of a major e-commerce platform Qoo10 Pte Ltd

over allegations of delayed payments to merchants who had sold goods on the Qoo10 platform. In February 2025, we conducted a joint operation with Singapore Customs, during which we raided 22 locations. The operation resulted in the charging of three men in court the following day for fraud by false representations to server suppliers Dell and SuperMicro. In July 2025, we also commenced

investigation into AmazingTech Pte Ltd (AmazingTech),

operating as Tokenize Xchange, a cryptocurrency trading platform. The platform had ceased its Singapore operations

after the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) rejected

its application for a Major Payments Institution licence in July.

We commenced an investigation pursuant to information

from the MAS that AmazingTech did not have sufficient

assets to meet its customers’ claims and that it might not have segregated its customers’ assets from AmazingTech’s assets. The director of AmazingTech was charged in Court

on 31 July 2025 for fraudulent trading.

## • PP v Oh Wee Hian

A former dealer, Oh Wee Hian (Oh), devised a fraudulent

very small quantities to minimise any potential losses,

should the orders be fulfilled.

largest among similar past prosecutions for defrauding CFD providers through price manipulation.

convicted on two counts of offences under Section 201(a)

Legislative Framework Strengthening

The years 2024 to 2025 marked a watershed period in

Singapore’s legislative landscape, with several significant

enhancements to strengthen the Anti-Money Laundering

(AML) / Counter-Financing of Terrorism (CFT) framework.

In February 2024, crucial amendments to the Corruption,

Benefits) Act and the Computer Misuse Act were implemented,

and financial crimes.

The Online Criminal Harms Act, progressively implemented from February 2024, provided authorities with enhanced

powers to combat online financial crimes. A notable

milestone was achieved in June 2024 with two

comprehensive Codes of Practice that established

clear legal obligations for designated online service providers, strengthening platform accountability against digital scams and fraudulent schemes.

Building on this foundation, four major pieces of legislation were introduced to further strengthen Singapore’s capabilities

in combating financial crimes and scams: • The Law Enforcement and Other Matters Act, effective

from 1 January 2025, introduced new offences to strengthen

our enforcement capabilities against the misuse of local

SIM cards in scams. The Act criminalises the fraudulent

actions of irresponsible subscribers, middlemen and

scammers, and errant retailers, with penalties of up to

S$20,000 and five years’ imprisonment for repeat offenders.

• The Corporate Service Providers (CSP) Act, effective on

9 June 2025, significantly enhanced the anti-money

laundering framework by mandating ACRA registration

for all CSPs and expanding their obligations to include

countering proliferation financing. The Act introduced

substantial penalties of up to S$100,000 per breach for

both CSPs and their senior management, while establishing

comprehensive customer due diligence requirements and

mandatory internal controls for detecting and preventing

financial crimes. • The Protection from Scams Act, effective from 1 July 2025,

introduced innovative preventive measures through

Restriction Orders, empowering Police officers to restrict

banking and credit facilities of individuals at risk of being

scammed. The Act included careful safeguards such as

30-day duration limits, provisions for legitimate expenses,

and robust appeal mechanisms, representing a shift from

reactive to proactive protection of potential victims.

• The Anti-Money Laundering and Other Matters Act,

passed in August 2024, strengthens Singapore’s AML/

CFT framework through phased implementation. The

first phase, effective from 14 November 2024, streamlines

prosecution of cross-border money laundering cases,

enables enhanced cross-agency data sharing, and aligns

casino operators’ compliance requirements with Financial

Action Task Force (FATF) standards. Additional provisions

will be implemented progressively.

These legislative enhancements collectively represent a

comprehensive upgrade to Singapore’s financial crime-fighting capabilities, providing prosecutors and investigators

with robust legal tools to combat increasingly sophisticated

financial crimes while maintaining appropriate safeguards

evolving financial crime threats, particularly in the digital

In June 2019, the Singapore Police Force (SPF) established

Operation SENTINEL (Strategic Enforcement for Neutralising Transactions Involving Nefarious

Entities and Laundering)

Since May 2024, the Police observed an uptick in the

suspicious activity on Singapore bank accounts which had been opened by foreigners. These bank accounts were being used as a conduit to receive and layer scam proceeds, which served to obscure the actual sources of funds,

thereby facilitating illicit financial activities such as money

laundering by scam syndicates. This made tracking and

regulating the flow of funds a challenging task for the

authorities.

In response to this surge, the ASC collaborated with OCBC to leverage their in-house data analytic models and network detection capabilities to identify transactions and bank accounts exhibiting potential money mule behaviour. Stemming from the joint operation and protracted information

sharing, both the ASC and OCBC identified and froze over

300 bank accounts that exhibited suspicious transaction

patterns and activities. The joint operation resulted in the successful seizure of more than S$1.8 million believed to be illicit funds moved in and out of the accounts by scammers.

Operation TARGUS HAWK

Between January and July 2024, the Singapore Police Force

(SPF) received 185 reports pertaining to Technical Support

Scams (TSS), resulting in total losses of at least S$17.9 million.

In this scam variant, the victims would receive screen pop-ups on their laptops or desktops, claiming that their electronic devices had been compromised by malware and instructing them to seek immediate help from Microsoft or Apple technical support. The pop-up alerts would contain

contact number(s) for victims to call for technical support. When

the victims called the number(s), scammers impersonating

Microsoft or Apple technical support employees would claim that the victims’ devices had been compromised by “hackers” and were used for illegal activities. Following this, the

scammers would instruct victims to access websites and/or

download software that would grant scammers remote access to their devices.

Subsequently, the scammers would direct the victims to log into their internet banking accounts on the pretext of helping to apprehend the “hackers”. When victims did so, the scammers would capture the victim’s internet banking IDs and passwords. Thereafter, the scammers would use the remote access function to perform unauthorised transactions using the victims’ bank accounts. In some cases, the scammers would guide victims to create cryptocurrency

accounts or provide One-Time Passwords (OTPs) to authorise

internet banking transactions. Victims would only realise that they had been scammed when they discovered unauthorised transfers or deductions from their bank accounts.

Between 9 and 12 August 2024, following extensive information sharing and joint investigations by the CAD

of the SPF and Hong Kong Police Force (HKPF),

officers from the HKPF raided three premises in Hong Kong

and arrested one mastermind of the syndicate, three syndicate members and three money mules, aged between

33 and 43. Cash amounting to HK$300,000 and over 60

bank cards were recovered and seized as case exhibits.

Through further follow-up investigations, another HK$1.3 million

in crime proceeds was successfully recovered. Through

intelligence sharing from the HKPF, the SPF arrested

a 23-year-old male Singaporean for his involvement in

money laundering activities. Investigations revealed that he had relinquished his bank account to a criminal syndicate associated with the money-laundering syndicate which was

busted by the HKPF. Investigations are ongoing.

“Instrumental Player” in Money Laundering Syndicate Convicted

In July 2023, the accused, Goh Soon Lee (Goh), was arrested

when the CAD intercepted the delivery of the four ATM cards from Goh to another “ATM runner”.

investigations and operations, targeting syndicated and transnational scam cases, and executing strategic measures to combat scams.

The ASCom spearheaded the monthly joint operations with the SSTs in seven Land Divisions and CAD branches, targeting money mules and scammers. Operation Quicksand was conducted on 2 April 2019. Since its inception, the CAD and the seven Land Divisions have hauled up
