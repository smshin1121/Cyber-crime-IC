Mexico: ICITAP and DEA Strengthen Mexico’s Forensic Capacity With First Opioid Identification Pilot In Baja California Sur

Blog Post

Friday, September 5, 2025

Share

Facebook

X

LinkedIn

Email

During the week of September 1–5, the ICITAP‑Mexico Opioid Identification Program conducted its first pilot course at the Chemistry Laboratory in La Paz, Baja California Sur (BCS), training four local forensic chemistry experts in advanced methodologies for opioid detection. ICITAP forensic chemistry advisors led the training on “Drug Analysis Method Design Using Gas Chromatography Coupled to Mass Spectrometry (GC‑MS) for Opioid Identification,” in collaboration with Dr. Sandra Rodríguez‑Cruz, Senior Research Chemist at the DEA Special Testing and Research Laboratory and current president of the Scientific Working Group on Seized Drugs (SWGDRUG). The course provided participants with hands-on instruction in designing GC‑MS analytical methods for opioids, precursors, and related substances, including critical parameters required to align testing protocols with the DEA’s Global Uniform Method of Analysis and Reporting of Drugs and Related Substances (GUARDS). As a result, the La Paz laboratory updated its drug library, integrated certified reference materials, and strengthened its analytical methodology, positioning it at the forefront of synthetic drug identification in Latin America. The lab is now equipped to seamlessly share analytical data and findings with the DEA, enhancing regional intelligence and investigative coordination. ICITAP advisors will incorporate feedback from the pilot to refine the curriculum for rollout across other laboratories participating in the Opioid Identification Project throughout northern Mexico. This initiative supports Executive Order 14157, “Designating Cartels and Other Organizations as Foreign Terrorist Organizations,” and demonstrates ICITAP’s commitment to strengthening forensic capacity and transnational law enforcement collaboration. In Mexico, ICITAP implements these efforts in coordination with the U.S. Department of State’s Bureau of International Narcotics and Law Enforcement Affairs (INL) and the U.S. Embassy in Mexico City.
