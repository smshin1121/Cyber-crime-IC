Background

The U.S. Transnational and High-Tech Crime Global Law Enforcement Network (GLEN) program is the result of a partnership between the U.S. Department of State’s Bureau of International Narcotics and Law Enforcement Affairs and the U.S. Department of Justice’s (DOJ) Computer Crime and Intellectual Property Section (CCIPS) and Office of Overseas Prosecutorial Development, Assistance and Training (OPDAT).

The GLEN is a worldwide law enforcement network of International Computer Hacking and Intellectual Property (ICHIP) attorney advisors, computer forensic analysts (Global Cyber Forensics Advisors or GCFAs), and federal law enforcement agents who together promote America First priorities by developing the ability of foreign law enforcement, prosecutorial, and judicial partners to combat intellectual property and cybercrime activity, as well as to assist in the collection and use of electronic evidence to combat all types of crime, including transnational organized crime. The ICHIPs also provide strategic guidance to key justice sector partners in the region to hold criminals fully accountable, particularly on those crimes with a U.S. nexus. The ICHIPs work closely with OPDAT and CCIPS headquarters staff and U.S. interagency partners to ensure lines of effort meet Departmental and U.S. Government objectives.

The objective of the GLEN is to protect Americans from criminal threats emanating from abroad by delivering targeted technical assistance to encourage both immediate improvements as well as long-term institutional change. This assistance includes skills development workshops, case-based mentoring, legislative review, and promoting institutional reform such as the formation of specialized units to address these criminal threats. The ICHIPs also encourage countries to join the Council of Europe’s Convention against Cybercrime (Budapest Convention), as well as the G7 24-7 High Tech Crimes Digital Evidence Sharing Network, an informal network that provides high-tech expert contacts to assist in preserving and sharing digital evidence.

Currently, ICHIPs are posted to regional positions in Africa (Tanzania and Ethiopia), Asia (Hong Kong S.A.R. and Malaysia), Europe (Romania, Croatia, and the Netherlands), and Western Hemisphere (Brazil and Panama). Two subject-matter expert ICHIPs—the ICHIP for Global Internet Fraud and Public Health (IFPH) and the ICHIP for Global Dark Web and Cryptocurrency (DWC)—as well as DOJ computer forensic analysts and law enforcement officials with intellectual property and cybercrime expertise, also form part of the GLEN.

IPR-Focused ICHIP

ICHIP Bucharest supports the needs in partner nations throughout Central and Eastern Europe and Central Asia by assessing their ability to combat criminal activity in violation of intellectual property rights (IPR); designing and implementing technical assistance to improve their ability to enforce IP crime, particularly that committed by criminal groups involved in large-scale digital piracy operations targeting American movies and tv programs or counterfeiting of American products infiltrating European markets; assisting in developing or strengthening institutions dedicated to IPR enforcement; monitoring regional trends in IPR protection; and furnishing expert assistance in support of U.S. intellectual property in the region. The ICHIP also assists foreign partners with IPR-related legislative drafting and preparing country submissions for the U.S. Trade Representative’s annual Special 301 Report. Finally, the ICHIP acts as a liaison among foreign law enforcement partners, U.S. law enforcement, and American rightsholders, whose support is critical for IPR investigations and prosecutions that protect U.S. interests.

Cyber-Focused ICHIPs

ICHIP The Hague and ICHIP Zagreb help to improve the ability of partners in Central and Eastern Europe and Central Asia to combat transnational cybercrime and cyber-enabled crime by assessing the ability of law enforcement and prosecutorial and judicial authorities to detect, investigate, prosecute, and adjudicate cybercrime; providing technical assistance; assisting in developing or strengthening institutions dedicated to cybercrime enforcement; and monitoring regional cybercrime trends and providing expert assistance. They also review and comment on draft legislation that seeks to address cybercrime and related digital evidence issues as well as cryptocurrency. ICHIP Hague is also the Cybercrime Liaison Prosecutor at Eurojust, and in that capacity, advances U.S. interests in multinational cybercrime investigations and disruptions by leading coordination between U.S. DOJ prosecution teams and prosecution teams from other countries represented at Eurojust and Europol.

IPR-Focused ICHIP Recent Activities

March 2026: ICHIP-trained Ukrainian investigators launched a criminal case into an organized criminal group composed of three Ukrainian citizens running an illegal counterfeit cigarette black market. These cigarettes were sold in Ukraine under the name of various trademarks of well-known brands. Officers seized a full-cycle cigarette production line, finished counterfeit products, tobacco-like raw materials, over one million suspected counterfeit foreign excise tax stamps, and materials for manufacturing and packaging tobacco products. The estimated value of the seized items exceeds $340,000.

March 2026: ICHIP-trained Romanian economic police officers executed 15 home search warrants and 5 arrest warrants in connection with the Red Dragon Shopping Complex - a known source of wholesale distribution of counterfeit goods originating from China. Through their investigation, police discovered a criminal network trafficking in counterfeit perfumes and cosmetics, including Tom Ford, Tommy Hilfinger, Calvin Klein, Ralph Lauren, Marc Jacobs, Estée Lauder, and Donna Karan (DKNY). The case revealed that the targets of the investigation illegally obtained almost $461,000 from sales of these products.

January 2026 : Bulgarian authorities, in close coordination with ICHIP Bucharest, HSI, and U.S. prosecutors, executed search warrants at 30 locations and dismantled three of the largest internet piracy platforms in the European Union: zamunda.net, arenabg.com, and zelka.org, which were used by millions of users monthly and responsible for substantial losses to U.S. copyright holders. U.S. authorities simultaneously seized the associated domains in the United States. Bulgarian authorities arrested and prosecuted site administrators under a new criminal statute that criminalizes operating piracy platforms, the first law of its kind in Europe, drafted with OPDAT and CCIPS assistance. Three individuals have been charged with online piracy and money laundering.

January 2026: ICHIP-mentored Romanian economic police officers conducted searches and issued a subpoena for interrogation in three Romanian cities in a joint case with Italian authorities in Operation Switch Off to gather evidence against a large transnational criminal organization involved in illegal streaming of copyright-protected movies and television shows. The total damage reported by the rightsholders, including U.S companies like Amazon Prime, Netflix, Paramount, and Disney+, amounted to $3.5 billion USD. In Italy, three illegal IPTV platforms were seized. An international arrest warrant was issued for a Romanian target who distributed illegal content through six servers operated out of Romania and an African country.

January 2026: ICHIP-supported Ukrainian Economic Security Bureau (ESBU) officers conducted a takedown of an organized criminal group involved in a counterfeit Marlboro cigarette scheme. ESBU officers conducted ten searches of the suspects' residences and other premises and uncovered nearly 250,000 packs of illegal cigarettes and 39 boxes of bulk tobacco without factory packaging.

Cyber-Focused ICHIP Recent Activities

March 2026: ICHIP The Hague assisted in hosting a coordination center at Eurojust for law enforcement and prosecutors working on the takedown of SocksEscort, a criminal proxy service. The SocksEscort investigation and takedown involved representatives from the U.S., France, Austria, the Netherlands, Hungary, Bulgaria, Romania, Germany, Eurojust, and Europol.

February 2026: ICHIP The Hague provided a presentation to the African Cryptocurrency Working Group (ACWG) about the ICHIP network, as well as Eurojust and Europol’s SIRIUS Network, which, among other things, is devoted to assisting law enforcement with obtaining digital evidence from Internet service providers and cryptocurrency platforms around the world.

January 2026: ICHIP The Hague provided a virtual presentation to Ukrainian law enforcement regarding cybercrime trends, with a focus on the dark web, network intrusions, and ransomware. ICHIP The Hague also discussed best practices in working with international partners to tackle online/cyber transnational organized crime groups and how to trace, seize, and forfeit cryptocurrency. The webinar was organized by the Resident Legal Advisor to Ukraine.

January 2026: ICHIP The Hague, along with representatives from DOJ’s Office of International Affairs, presented to over 800 attendees from the SIRIUS Network regarding the U.S. mutual legal assistance process, with a focus on collecting electronic evidence in cybercrime cases.

December 2025: ICHIP The Hague hosted a Eurojust-wide meeting regarding Mexican and Colombian cartel members infiltrating Europe and setting up laboratories for making crystal methamphetamine. During the meeting, U.S. law enforcement discussed the ways cartels are laundering the proceeds of drug trafficking via cryptocurrencies, including stablecoins like USDT (Tether), which is pegged to the U.S. dollar.

October 2025: ICHIP-mentored Croatian police officers, with assistance from Europol and Australia’s Queensland Police, helped dismantle a global 250-member encrypted network dedicated to exchanging child sexual abuse material. Croatian police arrested the Croatian national running the network. ICHIP had previously trained these investigators to track offenders across encrypted platforms and preserve digital evidence for prosecution.

September 2025: ICHIP The Hague assisted a DOJ prosecution team investigating and then indicting an administrator of the LockerGoga, MegaCortex, and Nefilim ransomware schemes that extorted more than 250 U.S. companies.
