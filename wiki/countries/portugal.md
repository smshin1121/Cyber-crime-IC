## Summary

Portugal is an EU member state and **[[budapest-convention|Budapest Convention]] party**. Its 2009 Cybercrime Law (*Lei do Cibercrime*, Law 109/2009) is widely cited as a close implementation of the Budapest Convention. The **Polícia Judiciária** (PJ) — Portugal's criminal investigation police — hosts the **Unidade Nacional de Combate ao Cibercrime e Criminalidade Tecnológica (UNC3T)**, which handles most cybercrime investigations including international cooperation.

## Legal Framework for Cybercrime

### Substantive Law

**Lei 109/2009 — Lei do Cibercrime** [1] criminalizes:
- Unauthorized access (Art. 6)
- System interference (Art. 5)
- Data interference
- Computer-related fraud and forgery

The law integrates with the Portuguese Penal Code.

### Procedural Powers

Law 109/2009 also contains procedural provisions including expedited preservation of data, production orders, and interception of content data. *Likely* compliant with Budapest Convention Chapter II procedural requirements.

### Mutual Legal Assistance

Central authority for MLAT requests is the **Procuradoria-Geral da República** (Attorney General's Office). Portugal is a party to EU mutual assistance instruments as well.

## Cooperation Track Record

Portugal cooperates through Europol EC3, Eurojust, and the Budapest Convention 24/7 Network. *Likely* participates in major EU joint operations.

## Capacity Assessment

Portugal's PJ and UNC3T represent consolidated institutional cybercrime capacity. The 2009 Cybercrime Law provides a solid legal framework. EU integration supports strong IC.
