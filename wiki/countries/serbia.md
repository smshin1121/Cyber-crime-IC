## Summary

Serbia is a **[[budapest-convention|Budapest Convention]] party** since 2009 and is *almost certainly* one of the first states to ratify the **Second Additional Protocol (CETS 224)** [1]. Serbia has institutionalized cybercrime enforcement through a **Special Prosecutor's Office for High-Tech Crime** and a specialized department within the Belgrade Higher Court.

## Legal Framework for Cybercrime

### Substantive Law

Serbia's 2005 **Law on Organization and Competence of Government Authorities for Combating High-Tech Crime** [2] established the institutional framework. Cybercrime offences are defined in the Serbian **Criminal Code** (Arts. 298-304a covering unauthorized access, data damage, and related conduct).

### Procedural Powers

Standard procedural tools including search, seizure, preservation, and production orders.

### Mutual Legal Assistance

Central authority is the **Ministry of Justice**.

## Treaty Memberships

- **[[budapest-convention|Budapest Convention]]** — party (ratified 2009-04-14) [1]
- **[[second-additional-protocol|Second Additional Protocol (CETS 224)]]** — ratified 2023-11-02 (among the earliest ratifiers) [1]

## Key Agencies

- **Special Prosecutor's Office for High-Tech Crime** (Tužilaštvo za visokotehnološki kriminal)
- **Ministry of Interior Cybercrime Department**

## Cooperation Track Record

Serbia cooperates with neighboring Balkans states, EU Member States, and via Europol and Interpol channels. *Likely* strong regional cooperation role.

## Capacity Assessment

Medium capacity; institutional specialization provides focused cybercrime prosecution capability. EU accession process has driven alignment with EU instruments.

## Contradictions & Open Questions
