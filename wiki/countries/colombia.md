## Summary

Colombia deposited its instrument of accession to the [[budapest-convention|Budapest Convention on Cybercrime]] on **16 March 2020**, becoming the 65th Party [1][2]. Its substantive cybercrime law is **Ley 1273 de 2009 "De la protección de la información y de los datos"**, which amended the Criminal Code to create a new protected legal good ("protection of information and data") and established comprehensive substantive offences [3][4].

Operationally, the **Policía Nacional de Colombia** (through DIJIN) and the **Fiscalía General de la Nación** jointly operate the **CAI Virtual** online reporting platform and the "A Denunciar" national virtual reporting system [3][4]. Colombia has been a key Council of Europe GLACY+ and C-PROC capacity-building beneficiary, and its accession in 2020 was the culmination of a multi-year process.

## Legal Framework for Cybercrime

### Substantive Law
Law 1273/2009 created new offences in the Criminal Code:
- Abusive access to computer systems (Acceso abusivo a un sistema informático)
- Unlawful obstruction of computer systems or telecommunications networks
- Interception of computer data
- Computer damage (Daño informático)
- Use of malicious software
- Computer-related theft (Hurto por medios informáticos)
- Violation of personal data
- Phishing / capture of personal data
- Unauthorized transfer of assets

Maximum penalty: 120 months' imprisonment and fines up to 1,500 minimum legal monthly wages [3][4].

### Procedural Powers
The Code of Criminal Procedure provides for search and seizure, production orders, and interception under judicial authorization. Specific procedural tools for electronic evidence have been developed through prosecutorial practice and Fiscalía guidance [5].

### Mutual Legal Assistance
The **Fiscalía General de la Nación** serves as central authority for MLAT requests. Since 2020, Colombia can invoke Budapest Convention Chapter III (Articles 23-35) with other parties. Colombia has bilateral MLATs with the United States and Spain (inter alia).

## Treaty Memberships

- **[[budapest-convention|Budapest Convention]]** — Party, acceded 2020-03-16 [1][2]
- Bilateral MLATs with US, Spain, and other partners

## Key Agencies

### Policía Nacional de Colombia — DIJIN
The **Dirección de Investigación Criminal e INTERPOL (DIJIN)** houses the Centro Cibernético Policial, which investigates cybercrime and operates the CAI Virtual public-facing reporting platform [3].

### Fiscalía General de la Nación
National public prosecutor; central authority for MLA; houses specialized cybercrime prosecutors and operates the "A Denunciar" platform jointly with DIJIN [5].

### ColCERT
National CERT under the Ministry of ICT; coordinates incident response.

## Cooperation Track Record

Colombia is *likely* an active participant in Interpol's cybercrime operations in Latin America. Its accession to the Budapest Convention in 2020 has *likely* increased its formal MLA cooperation with European parties. Colombia is a long-standing US partner with extensive MLAT traffic.

Capacity is *likely* medium to medium-high by regional standards. Colombia benefits from substantial US and EU capacity-building investment. Spanish is the working language; English proficiency in specialized units is *likely* medium.
