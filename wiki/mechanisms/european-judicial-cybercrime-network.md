## Summary

The **European Judicial Cybercrime Network (EJCN)** is a semi-formal liaison and information-sharing platform of judicial practitioners — prosecutors and investigating judges — specialising in cybercrime, cyber-enabled crime, and electronic evidence. It was established by Council Conclusions in June 2016 and is **administered by [[eurojust|Eurojust]]** at the agency's headquarters in The Hague.

The EJCN is *almost certainly* the EU's primary recurring forum for cybercrime prosecutorial practitioners to exchange experience on emerging legal questions (data retention, AI in evidence, encryption, jurisdiction over cloud data) and to align approaches across national prosecution services. It complements rather than replaces [[eurojust|Eurojust]]'s operational casework, [[europol-jit|Joint Investigation Teams]], and [[empact|EMPACT]] policy-cycle activities.

## Legal Basis

The EJCN was established by **Council Conclusions of the Council of the European Union (9 June 2016)** on the establishment of a European Judicial Cybercrime Network. These conclusions:

- Recognised the need for a dedicated judicial network for cybercrime, parallel to existing networks for organised crime and counter-terrorism
- Designated [[eurojust|Eurojust]] as the host and administrative anchor
- Tasked the network with strengthening contacts among practitioners, identifying obstacles to cross-border judicial cooperation, and contributing to the development of common approaches

EJCN is not established by treaty or regulation; it operates as a **practitioner network** under Eurojust's institutional umbrella.

## How It Works

### Membership

Each participating EU member state (and a small number of partner states) designates one or more **national contact points** — typically senior prosecutors or investigating judges with cybercrime expertise — to represent them in the network.

### Plenary Meetings

The EJCN holds **plenary meetings** approximately twice per year at Eurojust headquarters in The Hague. These plenaries:

- Bring together national contact points and invited experts
- Cover thematic agendas (e.g., cyberattacks on critical infrastructure, AI, data retention, encryption, cloud evidence, anonymisation services)
- Produce working conclusions and inform subsequent best-practice guidance

The **19th plenary**, held on **4-5 December 2025**, brought together **62 experts from 26 countries** to discuss cyberattacks on critical infrastructure, artificial intelligence, and data retention [1].

### Working Groups

Between plenaries, the network operates issue-specific working groups that drill down on technical or legal sub-topics (e.g., e-evidence, encryption-related procedural challenges).

### Outputs

EJCN outputs include:

- **Position papers and reports** on emerging legal and procedural questions
- **Best-practice guidance** for national prosecution services
- **Input into EU legislative debates** (e.g., e-evidence regulation, data retention)
- **Informal practitioner-to-practitioner contacts** facilitating cross-border cooperation outside formal channels

## Scope and Capabilities

The EJCN is a **discussion and practice-sharing network**, not an operational casework body. It does not:

- Open or coordinate cases
- Transmit MLA requests
- Coordinate arrests
- Form Joint Investigation Teams

Those operational functions remain with [[eurojust|Eurojust]]'s casework, the [[europol-jit|JIT mechanism]], and national authorities. The EJCN's value-add is *likely* in **upstream alignment**: ensuring that when cross-border cases arise, prosecutors across the EU share a common analytical vocabulary and procedural toolkit.

## Practical Usage

In practice, the EJCN supports:

1. **Common positions on emerging issues** — e.g., how to handle encrypted communication services, AI-generated CSAM, or cloud data extraterritoriality
2. **Cross-pollination of national experience** — prosecutors in one country learn from another's recent case law or procedural innovations
3. **Pre-positioning for legislative reforms** — feeding practitioner input into EU legislative processes (e-evidence regulation, AI Act enforcement)
4. **Trust-building among national contact points** — informal trust developed in plenaries *likely* accelerates subsequent formal cooperation

## Advantages

- **Low overhead**: As a network rather than an operational unit, it requires modest resources
- **Practitioner-driven**: Outputs reflect frontline prosecutorial experience rather than top-down policy
- **Eurojust integration**: Direct line to Eurojust's casework function ensures EJCN insights inform live cases
- **Recurring cadence**: Twice-yearly plenaries maintain continuity

## Limitations

- **Not operational**: Cannot directly trigger or coordinate enforcement
- **EU-centric**: Primarily for EU member states; non-EU practitioner participation is limited
- **Soft outputs**: Best-practice guidance is non-binding
- **Variable national implementation**: Adoption of EJCN guidance varies by member state

## Notable Uses

The EJCN has held **19 plenary meetings** as of December 2025. Specific operations or cases coordinated *through* the EJCN are not publicly catalogued, consistent with its role as a practitioner network rather than a casework body.

## Comparison with Alternatives

| Mechanism | Function | Operational? |
|-----------|----------|--------------|
| EJCN | Prosecutorial practitioner network | No (information sharing only) |
| [[eurojust]] casework | Cross-border judicial coordination | Yes |
| [[europol-jit|JIT]] | Joint investigation team | Yes |
| [[empact]] | EU policy cycle for serious organised crime | Mixed (policy + operational) |

The EJCN's distinctive niche is **judicial practitioner alignment** — neither an operational unit nor a policy-making body, but the connective tissue between them.

## Korean Usage (한국의 활용)

[[south-korea|South Korea]] is not a member of the EJCN, which is *almost certainly* limited to EU member states and select EU-adjacent partners. Korean cybercrime prosecutors do not have a direct equivalent forum; *likely* the closest analogue is the periodic **Asia-Pacific Working Group on Cybercrime** or bilateral exchanges with national prosecution services. Korea's 2024 accession to the [[budapest-convention|Budapest Convention]] could *likely* open opportunities for observer-style participation in EJCN plenaries on a topic-specific basis, though this is speculative.

## Contradictions & Open Questions

- **Exact membership scope**: Does the EJCN include any non-EU contact points, or is it strictly EU? The 19th plenary description references "26 countries" — the EU has 27 member states, so the configuration is unclear.
- **Working group output transparency**: Are EJCN working group reports publicly available, or only shared internally with national contact points?
- **Influence on EU legislation**: How directly do EJCN positions feed into Commission and Council proposals on cybercrime legislation?
- **Korean / Asia-Pacific engagement**: Is there any pathway for non-EU practitioner observation?
