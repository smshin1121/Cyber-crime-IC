## Summary

The **Cybercrime Programme Office (C-PROC)** is the capacity-building arm of the Council of Europe dedicated to cybercrime and electronic evidence. Based in **Bucharest, Romania**, it became operational on **7 April 2014** following a Memorandum of Understanding between the Council of Europe and the Government of Romania signed in October 2013. C-PROC marked its 10th anniversary in 2024.

C-PROC is *almost certainly* the most active dedicated capacity-building actor supporting implementation of the [[budapest-convention|Budapest Convention on Cybercrime]]. Since 2014, it has supported over 2,000 activities for more than 130 countries worldwide, making it the principal delivery channel for the Council of Europe's global cybercrime cooperation agenda.

## Mandate and Authority

C-PROC operates under the authority of the Council of Europe's Directorate General of Human Rights and Rule of Law (DGI). Its mandate is to **assist countries worldwide in strengthening their legal and institutional capacity** to respond to the challenges of cybercrime and electronic evidence, using the [[budapest-convention|Budapest Convention]] as the normative framework.

Key functions:
- Design and delivery of multi-year capacity building projects jointly funded by the EU and the Council of Europe
- Legislative assistance to countries seeking to align domestic law with the Budapest Convention
- Training of judges, prosecutors, investigators, and 24/7 Network points of contact
- Support to the [[budapest-convention|Cybercrime Convention Committee (T-CY)]] and its working groups
- Technical guidance on digital forensics, MLA drafting, and provider cooperation

## Structure Relevant to Cybercrime IC

C-PROC is organized around **joint projects** rather than a rigid hierarchical structure. Major programmes include:

| Programme | Scope | Funding |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| **GLACY+** (Global Action on Cybercrime Extended) | Global capacity building, ~15 priority countries | EU + CoE |
| **GLACY-e** | Successor programme, 35+ countries (2024-) | EU + CoE |
| **iPROCEEDS-2** | South-Eastern Europe and Turkey — focus on financial cybercrime | EU + CoE |
| **CyberEast+** | Eastern Partnership countries (Armenia, Azerbaijan, Belarus, Georgia, Moldova, Ukraine) | EU + CoE |
| **CyberSouth+** | Southern Neighbourhood (Algeria, Jordan, Lebanon, Morocco, Tunisia) | EU + CoE |
| **Octopus Project** | Global coordination and Octopus Conference series | CoE core |

## IC Capabilities

- **Standard-setting support:** Drafting templates, guidance notes, and model legislation
- **Peer-to-peer exchanges:** Facilitating exchanges between practitioners across jurisdictions
- **24/7 Network support:** Training and evaluation of national contact points under Art. 35 of the Budapest Convention
- **MLA streamlining:** Country-level assistance on central authority efficiency
- **Second Additional Protocol promotion:** Awareness raising and legal gap analysis for parties considering ratification of [[second-additional-protocol|CETS 224]]

## Key Operations and Cases

C-PROC is **not an operational agency** and does not conduct investigations. Its impact is measured through:
- Number of countries receiving sustained assistance (130+)
- Number of activities delivered (2,000+ since 2014)
- Legislative reforms achieved in partner countries
- Budapest Convention accessions supported (notably supporting the accession processes of states across Africa, Latin America, and Asia-Pacific)

## Cooperation Track Record

C-PROC maintains active working relationships with:
- **[[europol-ec3|Europol / EC3]]** — capacity-building synergies, especially for EU neighborhood
- **[[interpol-igci|INTERPOL / IGCI]]** — joint training activities
- **[[unodc|UNODC]]** — coordination on cybercrime capacity building (sometimes with tension over normative leadership)
- **OSCE** — confidence-building measures on cybersecurity
- **Private sector** — provider engagement in the T-CY context

> [!note] Coexistence with UNODC
> With the adoption of the [[un-cybercrime-convention-2024|UN Convention against Cybercrime]] in 2024, C-PROC's capacity-building role now operates alongside an expanded [[unodc|UNODC]] Cybercrime Programme. The two organizations *likely* will need to coordinate to avoid duplication for developing-country recipients.

## Capacity Building

C-PROC's capacity building is organized around five pillars:
1. **Legislation and policies** — aligning domestic law with Budapest standards
2. **Criminal justice capacities** — training of investigators, prosecutors, judges
3. **International cooperation** — MLA, 24/7 Network, joint investigations
4. **Public/private cooperation** — provider engagement
5. **Strategy, policy, and statistics**

## Korean Interactions (한국과의 협력)

South Korea's 2024 accession to the [[budapest-convention|Budapest Convention]] was *likely* supported by engagement with C-PROC during the preceding observer period at the T-CY. Korean representatives have attended the Octopus Conference series and C-PROC-organized capacity building events. Korea is a potential donor and expert-contributor country for C-PROC programmes targeting Asia-Pacific.

> [!warning] Legal status check needed
> Verify Korea's specific participation in C-PROC-led programmes and whether Korean experts have been deployed as trainers in GLACY+ or GLACY-e activities.
