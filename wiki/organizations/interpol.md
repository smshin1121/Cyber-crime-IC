## Current Status (as of 2026-04-10)

**Status:** Active. INTERPOL remains headquartered in **Lyon, France**, with **196 member countries** and its Constitution unchanged. No structural reorganization reported.

**Secretary General:** **Valdecy Urquiza** (Brazil) — took office in 2024 as INTERPOL's first Latin American Secretary General, succeeding **Jürgen Stock** (Germany, 2014–2024) whose 10-year maximum term expired. Urquiza previously served as INTERPOL's Executive Director of Police Services [10].

**President:** **Ahmed Naser Al Raisi** (United Arab Emirates) — elected at the 89th General Assembly in November 2021 for a four-year term (still in office as of April 2026; next presidential election expected at the 2025/2026 General Assembly cycle) [10].

**Structure:** General Secretariat (Lyon) + [[interpol-igci|INTERPOL Global Complex for Innovation (IGCI)]] in Singapore + Regional Bureaus (Buenos Aires, Abidjan, Nairobi, Yaoundé, San Salvador, Singapore, Bangkok, Harare) + 196 National Central Bureaus. The IGCI in Singapore celebrated its **10-year anniversary in June 2025** at a ceremony attended by President Al Raisi and SG Urquiza, reaffirming Singapore as INTERPOL's primary cyber and innovation hub [11].

**Recent priorities (2024–2026):** Global financial-cybercrime operations (HAECHI V/VI led by Korea, First Light 2024, Operation Synergia II and **Synergia III** in March 2026), African regional operations (Serengeti, Sentinel Africa), BEC/Black Axe (Jackal series), CSAM (Cyber Guardian 2025), AI-enabled crime, silver notices for asset tracing.

> [!note] Organizational continuity
> INTERPOL's mandate and structure are unchanged. The material change since this page was created is the **Secretary General transition from Stock to Urquiza (2024)** and the continued centrality of the Singapore IGCI after its 10-year anniversary celebration in June 2025.

## Summary

**INTERPOL** (International Criminal Police Organization) is the world's largest international police organization, with **196 member countries**. Headquartered in **Lyon, France**, INTERPOL facilitates cross-border police cooperation through its secure global communications network (**I-24/7**), international notice system (**Red Notices**, Blue Notices, Purple Notices), criminal databases, and operational support for multinational law enforcement operations.

For cybercrime, INTERPOL's primary operational hub is the **INTERPOL Global Complex for Innovation (IGCI)** ([[interpol-igci]]) in Singapore, which houses the **Cyber Fusion Centre** and coordinates major operations including the **HAECHI** series (led by [[south-korea]]), **Jackal** (targeting Black Axe), **Serengeti**, **Synergia**, and **First Light**.

INTERPOL is *almost certainly* the most globally inclusive platform for cybercrime cooperation, reaching regions (Africa, Southeast Asia, Pacific Islands) where the [[budapest-convention]] has limited membership.

## Mandate and Authority

INTERPOL operates under its **Constitution** (adopted 1956, amended). Key principles:
- **Art. 2:** Ensure and promote mutual assistance between criminal police authorities
- **Art. 3:** Prohibition of intervention in matters of political, military, religious, or racial character
- **Sovereignty principle:** INTERPOL facilitates cooperation but has no arrest or investigative authority; all operational actions are taken by national police

INTERPOL does **not** have its own investigators or powers of arrest. It serves as a coordination, communication, and intelligence platform.

## Structure Relevant to Cybercrime IC

### General Secretariat (Lyon)
- Executive Committee and Secretary General
- Operational coordination and policy
- I-24/7 network administration
- Notices issuance
- Criminal databases management

### INTERPOL Global Complex for Innovation (IGCI) — Singapore
See [[interpol-igci]] for full profile.
- **Cyber Fusion Centre:** Real-time cyber threat intelligence and operational coordination
- **Innovation Centre:** Research and development for law enforcement technology
- **Capacity Building Directorate:** Training programs for member countries
- **ASEAN Desk:** Regional coordination for Southeast Asia

### National Central Bureaus (NCBs)
- Each of 196 member countries maintains an NCB
- NCBs are the primary interface between national police and INTERPOL
- NCBs access I-24/7, submit and receive notices, and coordinate operations
- For cybercrime: [[knpa-cyber-bureau]] (Korea NCB), FBI (US NCB), BKA (Germany NCB), etc.

### Regional Bureaus
- Buenos Aires (Americas)
- Abidjan (West Africa)
- Nairobi (East Africa)
- Singapore (Asia-Pacific, co-located with IGCI)

## IC Capabilities

### I-24/7 Network
- Secure global police communications network
- Connects all 196 NCBs
- Enables real-time information exchange, database queries, and operational coordination
- *Almost certainly* the most widely accessible international law enforcement communications platform

### Notice System
| Notice Type | Purpose | Cybercrime Relevance |
|-------------|---------|---------------------|
| Red Notice | Seek location/arrest of wanted person | Fugitive cybercriminals |
| Blue Notice | Collect information on identity/activities | Intelligence on suspects |
| Purple Notice | Provide information on modus operandi | New cybercrime techniques (e.g., AI voice cloning alert via HAECHI IV) |
| Orange Notice | Warn of objects/events posing threat | Malware, exploits |
| Black Notice | Seek information on unidentified bodies | — |
| Silver Notice | Locate assets | Cryptocurrency, proceeds of cybercrime |

### I-GRIP (INTERPOL Global Rapid Intervention of Payments)
- Mechanism for rapidly freezing fraudulent financial transactions
- Used extensively in HAECHI operations
- Enables cross-border payment interception within hours
- *Likely* the fastest international mechanism for intercepting cybercrime proceeds

### Databases
- Stolen and Lost Travel Documents (SLTD)
- Facial recognition, fingerprints, DNA
- Foreign fighters
- I-24/7 messaging archive
- Cybercrime-specific threat intelligence feeds

## Cooperation Track Record

### Global Reach

INTERPOL's 196-member reach makes it *almost certainly* the only platform for cybercrime cooperation that includes:
- Non-Budapest Convention countries (China, Russia, most African/Asian states)
- Countries without bilateral MLATs with Western nations
- Developing countries with limited IC capacity

### Operational Model

INTERPOL's cybercrime operations typically follow this model:
1. **Intelligence phase:** IGCI Cyber Fusion Centre aggregates threat intelligence
2. **Planning phase:** Participating NCBs coordinate through I-24/7
3. **Action phase:** Coordinated action days across participating countries
4. **Results phase:** Arrests, seizures, asset recovery reported through NCBs
5. **Follow-up:** Purple Notices, capacity building, subsequent operation phases

### Limitations

- **No arrest authority:** INTERPOL relies entirely on national police to execute actions
- **Varying capacity:** Member country capabilities range from highly advanced to very limited
- **Political dynamics:** General Assembly politics; Art. 3 restrictions on political cases
- **Non-binding:** Red Notices are requests, not arrest warrants; countries may ignore them
- **Russia/China tensions:** Geopolitical tensions affect cooperation on state-linked cases

## Capacity Building

INTERPOL's capacity building for cybercrime includes:
- **GLACY+** program: Global Action on Cybercrime Extended (with Council of Europe)
- **INTERPOL Cyber Academy:** Online and in-person training for NCB officers
- **Regional workshops:** Targeted training for Africa, Southeast Asia, Pacific Islands
- **IGCI Innovation Centre:** Research and development of new tools for member countries
- **Cyber hygiene campaigns:** Public awareness in member countries

## Korean Interactions (한국과의 협력)

Korea's relationship with INTERPOL is *almost certainly* among the most active and significant of any member country for cybercrime:

- **HAECHI series:** Korea leads this flagship INTERPOL operation series through [[knpa-cyber-bureau]], making Korea the operational driving force behind INTERPOL's largest cybercrime operations
- **NCB Korea:** [[knpa-cyber-bureau]] serves as NCB Korea for cyber matters, with direct I-24/7 access
- **IGCI proximity:** The Singapore-based IGCI facilitates Asia-Pacific regional coordination, and Korea is a key partner
- **Financial contribution:** Korea is *likely* a significant financial contributor to INTERPOL cybercrime programs, given its HAECHI leadership role
- **I-GRIP usage:** Korean authorities have used I-GRIP to recover fraudulent transfers (e.g., [[operation-haechi-vi]] Korea-UAE sub-operation, $3.91M recovery)
