## Current Status (as of 2026-04-10)

**Status:** Active. Eurojust remains headquartered in **The Hague, Netherlands**, co-located with [[europol-ec3|Europol]], operating under **EU Regulation 2018/1727** (the Eurojust Regulation). No structural reorganization since the 2018 Regulation [6].

**President:** **Michael Schmid** (National Member for Austria), elected in 2025 and took office on **15 November 2025** for a four-year term. He succeeded **Ladislav Hamran**, who completed his second and final term as President (first elected 2017, re-elected 2021) [7][8].

**Structure:** The Eurojust College (27 National Members, one per EU member state) remains the decision-making body. Coordination Centres are activated for major cross-border operations. National Members continue to hold the authority to transmit MLA requests and authorize Joint Investigation Team (JIT) establishment on behalf of their national authorities.

**Recent mandate developments:** The European Commission has proposed amendments to the Eurojust Regulation to strengthen digital cooperation and the handling of core international crimes (e.g., war-crimes evidence related to Ukraine). Eurojust has also received an expanded mandate to negotiate **cooperation agreements with third countries** through the European Commission [9].

**Recent priorities (2024–2026):** Judicial coordination for the Operation Cronos (LockBit), Operation Endgame (Phase 1 & 2), and Phobos/8Base cybercrime takedowns; continued JIT support; expanded third-country cooperation (notably Ukraine on war-crimes and with potential new agreements under negotiation).

> [!note] Organizational continuity
> Eurojust's institutional structure is unchanged. The material change since this page was created is the **presidency transition from Hamran to Schmid in November 2025**. The operational relationships with [[europol-ec3|Europol EC3]], national prosecution authorities, and third-country partners are unchanged.

## Summary

Eurojust (유로저스트) is the European Union Agency for Criminal Justice Cooperation, headquartered in The Hague alongside [[europol-ec3|Europol EC3]]. Established in 2002, Eurojust provides the **judicial coordination** dimension of cross-border criminal investigations. While [[europol-ec3|Europol EC3]] provides operational and analytical support (the police side), Eurojust handles the prosecutorial and judicial side: ensuring that evidence is admissible, mutual legal assistance requests are properly channeled, Joint Investigation Teams have legal foundations, and European Arrest Warrants are coordinated across jurisdictions.

Eurojust is *almost certainly* indispensable to major EU cybercrime operations. It provided judicial coordination for all 5 operations covered by currently ingested sources, hosting coordination meetings, facilitating JITs, and ensuring that the multi-jurisdictional legal requirements were met for arrests, extraditions, and evidence sharing.

## Mandate and Authority

Eurojust's current mandate is governed by **EU Regulation 2018/1727** (the Eurojust Regulation), which replaced the original 2002 Council Decision and significantly strengthened the agency's powers and operational capacity. Key aspects of the mandate include:

- **Coordination of investigations and prosecutions:** Eurojust coordinates between national prosecution authorities of EU member states (and partner countries) when cases involve two or more countries or require a coordinated EU-level response.
- **Facilitating mutual legal assistance (MLA):** Eurojust assists in the transmission and execution of MLA requests, European Investigation Orders (EIOs), and other judicial cooperation instruments.
- **Joint Investigation Teams:** Eurojust facilitates the establishment of JITs, providing legal advice, logistical support, and financial funding for joint investigation activities.
- **Conflict of jurisdiction:** When multiple member states have jurisdiction over the same criminal activity, Eurojust mediates to determine the most appropriate forum for prosecution.
- **European Arrest Warrants (EAWs):** Eurojust coordinates the execution of EAWs across member states, resolving practical and legal issues that arise in cross-border surrenders.

Eurojust is composed of **National Members** — one senior prosecutor or judge from each of the 27 EU member states — who are permanently based at the agency's headquarters in The Hague. National Members have the authority to act on behalf of their national judicial authorities in urgent cases.

## Structure Relevant to Cybercrime IC

### College and National Members

The Eurojust College consists of all 27 National Members and is the agency's decision-making body for operational matters. Each National Member maintains direct links with their national prosecution service and can exercise specific powers conferred by their member state, including:

- Issuing and executing MLA requests on behalf of their national authority
- Agreeing to JIT establishment
- Authorizing coordination of European Investigation Orders

### Case Management System

Eurojust maintains a sophisticated case management system that tracks cross-border criminal cases and enables National Members to identify links between investigations in different member states. This is *likely* critical for detecting overlapping cybercrime investigations that should be coordinated.

### Coordination Centres

For major operations (such as the cybercrime takedowns in which Eurojust participated), Eurojust activates **Coordination Centres** at its headquarters. These centres bring together prosecutors and judges from multiple countries to coordinate action day activities in real time, ensuring that:

- Arrest warrants are executed simultaneously across jurisdictions
- Evidence preservation orders are issued in sync
- Search warrants comply with national procedural requirements
- Judicial authorizations are in place before operational actions

### Cooperation with Europol

Eurojust and [[europol-ec3|Europol EC3]] are co-located in The Hague and maintain a close institutional relationship. Europol provides the operational-police coordination while Eurojust provides the judicial coordination. This complementary relationship is formalized through cooperation agreements and joint action day protocols. In major cybercrime operations, both agencies typically participate, with Europol managing the operational coordination centre and Eurojust managing the judicial coordination centre.

## IC Capabilities

Eurojust's specific capabilities for international cybercrime cooperation include:

- **JIT facilitation and funding:** Eurojust provides financial support for JIT meetings, travel, translation, and operational costs. JITs are *almost certainly* the most effective EU mechanism for complex cross-border cybercrime investigations, and Eurojust's financial and legal support is essential to their functioning.
- **Coordination meetings:** Eurojust organizes and hosts coordination meetings between national prosecution authorities, facilitating agreement on investigative strategies, evidence sharing, and prosecution priorities. For the [[phobos-8base-crackdown|Phobos/8Base crackdown]], Eurojust hosted 2 coordination meetings.
- **MLA facilitation:** Eurojust assists in drafting, transmitting, and following up on MLA requests, reducing the delays and misunderstandings that frequently impede cross-border evidence gathering.
- **European Investigation Order (EIO) support:** For intra-EU evidence requests, Eurojust facilitates the use of EIOs, which are *likely* faster than traditional MLA processes.
- **Conflict of jurisdiction resolution:** When multiple countries wish to prosecute the same cybercriminals, Eurojust mediates to determine the most effective forum, avoiding duplicative prosecutions.
- **Third-country cooperation:** Eurojust has cooperation agreements with non-EU countries (including the US, Norway, Switzerland, and others), enabling judicial coordination beyond the EU's borders.

### Role in Major Cybercrime Operations

Eurojust's role in these operations illustrates a consistent pattern:

1. **Pre-operational phase:** Eurojust hosts coordination meetings where prosecutors from participating countries agree on investigative strategies, evidence-sharing protocols, and prosecution priorities.
2. **JIT establishment:** Eurojust facilitates the legal formation of Joint Investigation Teams, providing the framework for real-time evidence sharing between participating countries.
3. **Action day coordination:** During takedown operations, Eurojust activates a Coordination Centre to manage the judicial dimensions in real time — ensuring warrants are executed, evidence is preserved according to national procedural rules, and arrests are synchronized.
4. **Post-operational phase:** Eurojust coordinates follow-up judicial actions including extradition requests, additional evidence collection, and prosecution coordination to avoid conflicts of jurisdiction.

This pattern of involvement makes Eurojust *almost certainly* a participant in all significant Europol-coordinated cybercrime operations, even where its role may not be prominently featured in press releases (which tend to emphasize operational/police actions over judicial coordination).

## Cooperation Track Record

Eurojust's cooperation on cybercrime has expanded significantly since 2020. Key metrics (publicly reported) include:

- **Annual casework:** Eurojust handles thousands of cases annually across all crime types, with cybercrime representing a growing share.
- **JIT support:** Eurojust supports hundreds of JITs, with cybercrime-related JITs increasing in number and complexity.
- **Coordination meetings:** Eurojust hosts hundreds of coordination meetings per year, with cybercrime cases increasingly featuring in these meetings.

## Capacity Building

Eurojust contributes to judicial capacity building through:

- **Judicial training:** Cooperation with the European Judicial Training Network (EJTN) on cross-border investigation skills.
- **Best practice dissemination:** Publication of guidance on JIT formation, EIO use, and cybercrime prosecution coordination.
- **Third-country engagement:** Eurojust's cooperation agreements with non-EU countries often include capacity building components, particularly for countries aspiring to EU accession.

## Korean Interactions (한국과의 협력)

Direct Eurojust-Korea judicial cooperation on cybercrime is *likely* limited but developing:

- **Cooperation agreement status:** As of the knowledge available, South Korea does not have a formal cooperation agreement with Eurojust (unlike the US, Switzerland, Norway, and several other non-EU countries). Such an agreement would facilitate direct judicial cooperation on cross-border cases.
- **Indirect cooperation:** Korean participation in Europol-coordinated operations (such as the [[phobos-8base-crackdown|Phobos/8Base crackdown]]) may involve indirect coordination with Eurojust through the operational framework, even without a direct bilateral agreement.
- **Potential for closer ties:** Given South Korea's increasing participation in international cybercrime operations and its interest in cooperating with EU agencies, a formal Eurojust-Korea cooperation agreement *likely* represents a gap that could be addressed to strengthen judicial cooperation.

> [!note] Information gap
> The current status of any Eurojust-South Korea cooperation agreement negotiations requires further sourcing. Eurojust has been expanding its network of third-country agreements, and South Korea could be a candidate given growing operational cooperation.

## Contradictions & Open Questions

- What is the precise division of responsibility between Eurojust and Europol during cybercrime action days? Press releases often blur these roles.
- How effectively does Eurojust's conflict of jurisdiction function work in practice for cybercrime cases where victims and infrastructure span many countries?
- Does Eurojust have the resources to handle the growing volume and complexity of cybercrime cases, or is there a capacity gap?
- What is the status of Eurojust's cooperation with non-EU countries (particularly in Asia-Pacific) for cybercrime judicial coordination?
- How does post-Brexit cooperation between Eurojust and the [[united-kingdom|UK]] function in practice for cybercrime operations? The UK-EU TCA established a framework, but practical effectiveness is uncertain.
