## Source Summary

DOJ USAO-WDNC press release announcing the first federal court appearance of Romanian national Gavril Sandu, 53, after his extradition from Romania to the United States on 2026-04-30. Sandu had been indicted on 2017-11-14 by a federal grand jury in Charlotte, North Carolina, on one count of conspiracy to commit bank fraud (18 U.S.C. § 1349) and one count of bank fraud (18 U.S.C. § 1344), and was arrested by Romanian authorities in Romania on 2026-01-09 pursuant to a U.S. provisional arrest request. The underlying conduct — May 2009 to October 2010 — involved Sandu and co-conspirators hacking small-business VoIP (Voice-over-IP) systems in the United States and using them to conduct automated mass "vishing" (voice-phishing) campaigns against U.S. bank customers; victims surrendered debit card and PIN credentials, which Sandu then cloned onto fake magnetic-stripe cards and used to withdraw cash from ATMs as a money mule for the broader Romanian-resident hacker ring.

## Relevance to IC

This source documents a clean bilateral **US–Romania extradition** cooperation pathway running through the formal MLAT/extradition channels (DOJ OIA ↔ Romanian Ministry of Justice, under the 1924 US-Romania extradition treaty as supplemented by the 2003 EU-US extradition agreement), supported on the ground by the **FBI Bucharest Legal Attaché (LEAT) Office** liaising with **Romanian authorities** (Romanian National Police executing the arrest, Romanian Court of Appeal authorizing surrender, Romanian Ministry of Justice signing the surrender order). The case illustrates: (1) the patience of OIA extradition workflow (17-year cycle from underlying conduct to surrender; 9-year cycle from sealed indictment to surrender), (2) the value of in-country FBI LEATs as continuous liaison nodes for cold-case cybercrime fugitive recovery, and (3) the operational durability of pre-Budapest bilateral extradition treaties for cybercrime where the underlying offense is mapped to traditional bank-fraud / wire-fraud statutes.
