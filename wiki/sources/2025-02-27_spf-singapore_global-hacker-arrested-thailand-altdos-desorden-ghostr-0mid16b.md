## Source Summary

Singapore Police Force press release dated **27 February 2025** announcing the **26 February 2025 arrest** in Thailand of a **39-year-old man** suspected of being the threat actor operating under aliases **ALTDOS, DESORDEN, GHOSTR, and 0mid16B** — collectively responsible for **at least 75 international data-breach-extortion cases** since 2020. The arrest was made by the **Royal Thai Police (RTP) Central Investigation Bureau (CIB)** under the Thai Criminal Code and Computer-Related Crime Act, with assets valued at **over 10 million THB** seized (laptops, mobile phones, luxury vehicles, branded bags).

The investigation was a **~5-year cross-border collaboration** between SPF Cybercrime Command (CCC) and RTP CIB, supported by the **Cyber Security Agency (CSA) of Singapore** and (per the corresponding Group-IB release) **Group-IB's Digital Crime Resistance Centers in Thailand and Singapore**. The case originated in **2020 from reports by 11 Singapore victims** and expanded to encompass victims across Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia, India, the United Kingdom, the United States, Canada, and the Middle East.

The release establishes:

1. **SPF-RTP bilateral cybercrime cooperation track** as a documented pattern with a successful 5-year multi-alias data-breach-extortion case as a reference outcome.
2. **Cross-jurisdictional alias-correlation pattern** — the same threat actor operating under multiple aliases (ALTDOS, DESORDEN, GHOSTR, 0mid16B) over 5 years, requiring sustained writing-style + targeting + posting-pattern analysis to attribute. This is the analytical-tradecraft layer enabling the eventual arrest.
3. **Extortion-not-ransomware pattern** — the threat actor exfiltrated databases and extorted victims for non-disclosure rather than encrypting (with rare exceptions) — a structurally distinct attack pattern from ransomware actors. Reputational pressure escalation via customer notifications + media + DP regulators.
4. **Public-private partnership pattern** — Group-IB DCRCs (Singapore + Thailand) as the long-run private-sector tracking layer; SPF + RTP + CSA as the public-sector arrest-and-prosecution layer.
