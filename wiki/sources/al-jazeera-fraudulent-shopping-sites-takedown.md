## Summary

Al Jazeera reported that Indonesian immigration officials raided a villa in Tabanan district, Bali on 26 June 2024, detaining 103 individuals — 91 men and 12 women — on suspicion of operating cybercrime activities and violating their visas and residency permits. Authorities seized hundreds of mobile phones and electronic devices.

Indonesian Immigration Director Saffar Muhammad Godam stated that the detainees would be deported rather than prosecuted in Indonesia because the alleged cybercrimes targeted victims outside Indonesian jurisdiction — a *likely* reference to the substantive-law jurisdictional limitation that affects many countries' ability to prosecute foreign scam-compound operations.

Taiwan's Ministry of Foreign Affairs subsequently clarified that only 14 of the 103 detainees were confirmed as Taiwanese citizens, despite the initial framing of the story. The remaining nationalities were not identified in the report.

## Why this source matters

For cybercrime IC research this Al Jazeera article illustrates:

1. **Jurisdictional limitation on substantive law** — Indonesia can detain and deport but cannot prosecute where victims are foreign. This is a *recurring* challenge in Southeast Asian cybercrime IC.
2. **Southeast Asian cybercrime ecosystem** — the story fits a broader pattern (also seen in [[korea-cambodia-scam-repatriation]]) of scam operations physically hosted in one country but victimizing others.
3. **Administrative-deportation vs. criminal-prosecution divergence** — many cross-border cybercrime cases resolve through immigration action rather than formal prosecution + extradition.
4. **Nationality mis-reporting** — the headline's "103 Taiwanese" claim was quickly contradicted by Taiwan's foreign ministry (only 14 confirmed), showing the *unreliability of initial national-identity claims* in fast-moving multi-national detention cases.
