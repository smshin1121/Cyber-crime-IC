## Source Summary

Official press release published on 5 February 2025 by the Spanish National
Police (Policía Nacional) on its institutional press-release portal
(policia.es Sala de prensa, comunicado ID 16448), authored by the Dirección
General de la Policía - Gabinete de Prensa. Announces the arrest in Calpe
(Alicante) of a hacker described as responsible for more than 40 cyberattacks
against strategic Spanish and international public and private entities,
charged with "descubrimiento y revelación de secretos", "acceso ilícito a
sistemas informáticos", "daños informáticos" and "blanqueo de capitales".

The suspect is not named in the public source. The release reports more than
50 cryptocurrency accounts held by the suspect and the seizure of diverse IT
equipment under judicial search.

The release is published in Spanish on the Policía Nacional's own
institutional domain and constitutes a tier-1 primary source under
LESSONS L24.

## Relevance to IC

This source matters for the cyber-IC wiki in three ways:

1. **Explicit Spain–United States cyber-LE cooperation** — the release names
   "Homeland Security Investigations (HSI) de los EE.UU." as an international
   collaborator alongside EUROPOL. This is a tier-1 acknowledgment of
   bilateral cyber-LE cooperation for a hacking / unauthorised access case,
   not via FBI but via HSI (DHS), which is a distinct U.S. cyber-LE channel
   relative to most prior wiki Spain–U.S. operations.
2. **Multiple Spanish national-security actors** — the operation visibly
   combines judicial police (Policía Nacional + Guardia Civil) with national
   cryptologic / signals-intelligence services (CCN / CNI), illustrating how
   high-profile cyber-attack cases on Spanish strategic targets are handled
   on the Spanish side. The investigation trigger came from the Guardia
   Civil UCO after the suspect's late-2024 attack on Guardia Civil and
   Ministry of Defence databases.
3. **Documented attack surface** — the release lists named institutional
   victims including NATO, U.S. Army, UN, and ICAO databases. This is one of
   the few publicly available primary sources from a Spanish LE agency that
   names specific intergovernmental victims of a single hacker, which makes
   it useful for downstream analysis of cyber-attack patterns against
   strategic targets.
