## Summary

VOA's article documents one of the most unusual cybercrime-cooperation cases on record: **ethnic rebel militias in Myanmar physically dismantling Chinese-targeting cyber-scam compounds** in coordination with (or at least with tacit support from) the Chinese state. Key facts:

1. **Operation 1027**: Coordinated offensive launched 2023-10-27 by the 'Three Brotherhood Alliance' (MNDAA, TNLA, AA) against Myanmar military junta forces
2. **Kokang SAZ target**: Ethnic Chinese-majority area along the Myanmar-China border; hub of cyber-scam compounds targeting Chinese victims since ~2017
3. **October 2023 catalyst**: Mass killing of Chinese nationals at a scam compound by local BGF militia as captives attempted escape — triggering Beijing's tacit endorsement of the rebel action
4. **Outcomes**: Thousands of Chinese captives released; subsequent OCCRP reporting indicates Myanmar handed ~31,000 telecom scammers to Chinese custody
5. **Targets**: BGF and pro-junta militias that had administered the Kokang SAZ since 2009
6. **Beneficiary**: The Chinese government's cybercrime-crackdown objectives were advanced by a military offensive not conducted by Chinese forces

## Why this source matters

For a future `myanmar-kokang-scam-compound-crackdown` page VOA provides:

- Strategic framing of the 2023 Kokang operation as a **tacit China–rebel cybercrime cooperation**, a case that *defies standard IC taxonomies* (not LE-to-LE, not MLAT-based, not treaty-based)
- A rare concrete example of **authoritarian informal cooperation** through proxy armed groups — relevant to the wiki's [[informal-cooperation]] and challenge pages
- Contextualises the Southeast Asian scam-compound problem (pig butchering, online investment fraud) at an industrial scale
- Demonstrates that 'IC on cybercrime' can include outcomes produced by *conflict*, not only by formal cooperation

This is a valuable contrarian data point for any analysis arguing that formal IC mechanisms are the dominant pathway.
