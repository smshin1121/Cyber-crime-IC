## Source Summary

DOJ Office of Public Affairs press release announcing the return of **Romeo Vasile Chita**, a Romanian national, to face a four-count federal indictment in the Northern District of Ohio (Cleveland). The release frames Chita as the leader of an international cyber fraud ring whose operations spanned **ten countries** (United States, Romania, Canada, Croatia, Latvia, Hungary, Bosnia, China, Jordan, Malaysia) and which had been running **since 2007**, causing more than USD 4 million in losses through phishing, keylogger malware, and online auction fraud. Eight co-defendants were named; two of them (Daniel Mihai Radu, Manuel Tudor) had already been extradited from Romania.

## Relevance to IC

1. **Extradition cooperation with Romania.** Two co-defendants (Radu, Tudor) were extradited from Romania prior to this announcement; Chita himself was "returned to the United States" — operationally an extradition or equivalent surrender. The DOJ release explicitly thanks the Government of Romania, indicating sustained bilateral cooperation. Highly likely (>80%) the lead Romanian counterpart agency was [[romania-diicot|DIICOT]] or [[romanian-prosecution|Romanian prosecution authorities]] working with [[romania-police|Romanian Police]], although the release does not name them.
2. **Office of International Affairs as central authority.** OIA's involvement evidences formal MLA / extradition channeling rather than purely informal police-to-police cooperation — likely (55–80%) backed by the **U.S.–Romania Extradition Treaty (2007)** and the **Council of Europe Cybercrime Convention (Budapest)**, to both of which Romania and the U.S. are parties.
3. **10-country operational footprint.** Phishing infrastructure, money-mule chains, and auction-fraud monetization spanned three continents, illustrating the multi-jurisdictional evidence-collection problem typical of cyber-enabled financial fraud.
