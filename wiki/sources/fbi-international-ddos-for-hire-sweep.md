## Summary

The FBI press release covers **Operation Tarpit**, the 5-9 December 2016 international law enforcement operation targeting **customers of DDoS-for-hire (booter/stresser) services**. The operation was coordinated from **Europol EC3 headquarters in The Hague** with simultaneous action in **13 countries**, resulting in **34 arrests** and **101 "knock-and-talk" interviews** of suspects.

Key characteristics:

1. **Demand-side targeting** — the operation went after customers/users of booter services rather than the service operators themselves. This is a *deterrence* strategy aimed at reducing demand for attack-as-a-service offerings.
2. **Young-offender demographic** — the FBI and Krebs reporting both emphasize that most suspects were under 20 years old, which raised policy questions about whether prosecution or youth-diversion programs were more appropriate.
3. **Europol-centered coordination** — the operation was run out of The Hague, with the FBI as one of 13 national agencies rather than as the lead. This is an early example of symmetric US-EU cybercrime cooperation.
4. **Tarpit methodology** — 101 of the 135 contacted suspects received "knock-and-talk" warnings rather than formal arrest, a lighter-touch approach than standard criminal prosecution.

## Operational significance for cybercrime IC

Operation Tarpit is a reference case for:

1. **Demand-side deterrence** rather than supply-side dismantlement — a distinct enforcement theory
2. **Knock-and-talk lighter-touch model** for youth offenders — a prosecutorial policy choice with implications for recidivism and deterrence theory
3. **Europol-centered multi-country coordination** without a single national lead — contrasts with the more common US-DOJ-led model
