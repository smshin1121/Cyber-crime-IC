## Source Summary

On 27 September 2024, the U.S. DOJ Office of Public Affairs, the U.S. Attorney's Office for the District of Columbia, the DOJ National Security Division, and the FBI jointly announced an unsealed indictment in the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia charging three IRGC cyber operatives — Masoud Jalili (36), Seyyed Ali Aghamiri (34), and Yaser Balaghi (37), all Iranian nationals — with a multi-year spearphishing and social-engineering campaign that culminated in a June 2024 hack-and-leak operation against U.S. Presidential Campaign 1, with stolen material disseminated to media and to individuals associated with U.S. Presidential Campaign 2. The DOJ release was coordinated the same day with a Department of State Rewards for Justice offer of up to USD 10 million for information on the subjects or on associated IRGC election-interference activity, and with a Department of the Treasury (OFAC) sanctions designation of defendant Jalili under Executive Order 13694 (as amended) and Executive Order 13848. The charged conduct includes conspiracy, computer intrusion (CFAA), wire fraud (including a domain-name false-registration variant), aggravated identity theft (eight counts), and conspiracy to provide material support to a designated foreign terrorist organization (the IRGC). The release places the conduct within Iran's broader campaign to erode confidence in the U.S. electoral process and to acquire information useful for IRGC retaliation following the killing of Qasem Soleimani. Investigating offices include the FBI Washington Field Office (lead), FBI Cyber Division, FBI Springfield Field Office, and FBI Minneapolis Field Office.

## Relevance to IC

The release is a tier-1 instance of a U.S. coordinated denial-and-deterrence action against state-directed election-interference cyber actors. Three IC-relevant features stand out. First, the indictment is announced jointly with State Department Rewards for Justice and Treasury OFAC sanctions, illustrating the U.S. "stack" of legal actions deployed when defendants are sheltered by an adversary state. Second, the case relies on no formal mutual legal assistance partner — Iran is not a Budapest Convention party and is *highly unlikely* to extradite IRGC personnel — so the cooperation logic is informational and deterrent rather than custodial. Third, the explicit charging of material support to a designated foreign terrorist organization (IRGC) overlays counterterrorism statutory authority on cybercrime conduct, which is *likely* to influence how analogous future state-actor election-interference cases are framed.
