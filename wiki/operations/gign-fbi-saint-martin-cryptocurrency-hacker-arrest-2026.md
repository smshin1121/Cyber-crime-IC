## Summary

On **4 March 2026**, around the evening hours, a joint operation conducted by the **Antenne GIGN de Guadeloupe** (about ten GIGN operators), three investigators from the **Section de recherches (S.R.) de Saint-Martin / Saint-Barthélemy**, and three agents of the **U.S. Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI)** arrested a 24-year-old U.S. national in a luxury rented villa on the French portion of the island of **Saint-Martin** (Caribbean overseas collectivity of France). The suspect is described in the Gendarmerie nationale's official press release as a "hacker" suspected of having stolen the equivalent of approximately **USD 46 million in cryptocurrency** before leaving the United States.

The operation was executed on the basis of an **international arrest warrant** (mandat d'arrêt international). The full reported stolen amount — USD 46 million in cryptocurrency — was recovered at the scene, alongside high-end IT equipment, cryptocurrency-wallet credentials, multiple phones, approximately EUR 250,000 in cash, and a loaded Glock pistol. The suspect was placed in retenue judiciaire and then in pre-trial detention pending the procédure d'extradition to the United States.

The press release was published on **6 March 2026** by the Gendarmerie nationale on its institutional website, authored by commandant Céline Morin.

## Background

Approximately one month before the action day — i.e., **early February 2026** — the **Commandement de la gendarmerie de Saint-Barthélemy et Saint-Martin (COMGEND)** was contacted by the FBI. U.S. authorities reported that one of their nationals, identified as a "target principale" (principal target), was believed to have taken refuge on the island of Saint-Martin after fleeing the United States.

The Section de recherches (S.R.) de Saint-Martin opened the local investigation under the supervision of the **parquet général de Guadeloupe**. The international leg was driven by the S.R.'s dedicated "cellule internationale" in direct liaison with the FBI. Investigators rapidly confirmed the suspect's presence on the island and located him in a rented luxury villa. FBI agents were then dispatched to Saint-Martin and an international arrest warrant was issued.

The Gendarmerie press release describes the suspect's profile as that of an individual potentially armed and willing to use a weapon, with a personal bodyguard, and notes that the **COMGEND** of Saint-Barthélemy and Saint-Martin is equipped only with a peloton d'intervention de gendarmerie mobile — not a specialised intervention unit. For this reason the **Antenne GIGN de Guadeloupe** was tasked with the action, with logistical support from gendarmerie air assets in Martinique to transport specialised equipment.

- **International arrest warrant (mandat d'arrêt international)** — operative legal instrument for the apprehension on French sovereign territory.
- **Joint action-day model** — FBI agents physically present alongside French gendarmes for the arrest and the subsequent search of the villa under the warrant.

## Challenges and Friction Points

- **Armed-suspect risk profile** — the suspect was reported by U.S. authorities as potentially armed and willing to use a firearm, with a bodyguard. The COMGEND's organic response capability (a peloton d'intervention de gendarmerie mobile) was insufficient, requiring deployment of a specialised intervention unit (Antenne GIGN de Guadeloupe) and aerial logistics from Martinique. This is a recurring operational pattern when cyber-fugitives are reported armed: the cyber-investigation is led by judicial-police but the arrest must be subcontracted to a specialised unit.
- **Identification opacity in public source** — the suspect is not named in the press release. The U.S. predicate offence, the U.S. district that issued the underlying warrant, the specific cryptocurrency / token, and the original victim platform are not disclosed.
- **British / Dutch role ambiguity** — the press release names the British and the Dutch as local partners but provides no specific agency, role, or contribution. Their inclusion appears to be environmental rather than operational.
- **Extradition treaty not invoked by name** — the 1996 U.S.–France extradition treaty is the natural bilateral instrument for the planned extradition but is not invoked by name in the source.

## Lessons Learned

- **Caribbean-overseas-collectivity venue model**: U.S. cyber-fugitive cases can land on French Caribbean territory and be resolved through territorial gendarmerie infrastructure (COMGEND → S.R. → Antenne GIGN), with regional prosecutorial supervision (parquet général de Guadeloupe) rather than central-Parisian judicial channels. This expands the de facto perimeter of FBI-led cyber-IC beyond U.S.–metropolitan-France cooperation.
- **Pre-arrest forensic localisation of crypto wallet credentials** can convert a fugitive arrest into a near-total asset-recovery operation within a single action day.
- **Specialised intervention unit deployment for cyber-fugitive cases** — when the suspect is reported armed, the cyber-investigation is naturally led by judicial police but the action day is subcontracted to a tactical unit (here GIGN). The GIGN's presence is not because the offence is cyber, but because the suspect is dangerous; this is a useful structural distinction when comparing arrest patterns across jurisdictions.
- **Tri-national island geography matters**: the press release's express naming of the British and the Dutch as local partners — even without operational role here — flags Saint-Martin / Sint Maarten as a structurally tri-national operating environment for any future cyber-IC action on the island.
