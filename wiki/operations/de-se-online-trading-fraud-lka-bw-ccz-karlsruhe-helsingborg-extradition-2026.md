# Summary

On 2026-03-26, the **Cybercrime-Zentrum (CCZ)** at the **Generalstaatsanwaltschaft Karlsruhe** and the **Landeskriminalamt Baden-Württemberg (LKA-BW)** announced that they had identified a 61-year-old Swedish national resident in Helsingborg, Sweden, as an alleged enabler of an internationally organized online-trading fraud network. A European Arrest Warrant (EAW) issued by CCZ Karlsruhe was executed by Swedish judicial authorities, and the defendant was extradited from Sweden to Germany in March 2026. She is in pretrial detention pending trial.

Investigative findings reported in the primary source: the criminal group has been operating since **at least 2018**, running professionally designed Internet platforms offering fake derivative investment products to German retail investors. The Swedish suspect allegedly set up the corporate-account and ledger structures used to receive and forward victim deposits, receiving approximately EUR 280,000 for her role over several years.

**Quantitative scope** (per the primary release):
- 24 German victims, across multiple federal states (mehrere Bundesländer)
- More than **EUR 1.17 million** in investor deposits received and forwarded through accounts the suspect helped establish
- Investigation continuing to identify further perpetrators

This is one of several recent Baden-Württemberg CCZ-led extraditions of online-trading-fraud enablers (compare to the September 2024 Ukrainian suspect from Slovakia case referenced in the LKA-BW backstory) and reflects the increasing use of the **[[european-arrest-warrant|European Arrest Warrant]]** as the dominant judicial cooperation tool for intra-EU prosecutions of cross-border online investment fraud.

> [!note] Confidence
> The 1.17M EUR / 24-victim / EUR 280K compensation figures and the March 2026 extradition fact are *almost certainly* correct as reported by LKA-BW (tier-1 primary). The identification of additional unnamed co-perpetrators is *likely* still pending. Whether the Swedish defendant was the principal organizer or a facilitator is *not asserted* in the primary source — she is charged with aiding (Beihilfe), suggesting a facilitator role.

# Background

The CCZ Karlsruhe (Cybercrime-Zentrum at the Generalstaatsanwaltschaft Karlsruhe) operates as Baden-Württemberg's specialized cybercrime prosecution unit, working in joint investigation with the LKA-BW. Online-trading fraud (Online-Trading-Betrug / Cybertrading-Betrug) has been one of the LKA-BW's tracked priority typologies since 2018, with the LKA publishing investor-warning advisories on its public-facing pages alerting to "traumhafte Gewinne — echte Verluste" (dream profits → real losses).

The release explicitly references the typology: criminal groups operate professionally designed platforms that present themselves as legitimate trading exchanges (often impersonating well-known brokerages or branding themselves under invented derivative product names), then use boiler-room call-center pressure to extract progressively larger "deposits" from victims before the platform becomes unreachable.

The Swedish defendant's role per the primary source: setting up corporate account structures and bank-receivable infrastructure that received victim deposits — i.e., the *money-mule organizer* / *infrastructure facilitator* role rather than the customer-facing fraud-execution role.
