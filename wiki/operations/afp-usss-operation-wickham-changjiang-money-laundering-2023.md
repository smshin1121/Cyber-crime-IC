## Summary

**Operation Wickham** is a joint investigation between the [[australia-afp|Australian Federal Police (AFP)]] and the [[us-secret-service|United States Secret Service (USSS)]]. AFP describes Wickham as having opened on **22 August 2022**, after USSS notified the AFP-led Joint Policing Cybercrime Coordination Centre (JPC3) that funds generated through investment-fraud schemes in the [[united-states|United States]] were being transferred into an Australian business bank account. AFP took the Australian-side investigation forward as Operation Wickham.

The 26 October 2023 AFP media release announces the **seventh** alleged offender charged under Operation Wickham — a 37-year-old Chinese national, resident in Melbourne — who is alleged to have laundered more than **AUD 100 million** through accounts associated with the **Changjiang Currency Exchange** and a network of fake [[australia|Australian]] businesses controlled by the wider Long River money-laundering organisation. AFP estimates more than AUD 160 million was stolen worldwide through the underlying investment scams, and reports more than AUD 21 million has been restrained in Australian bank accounts believed to be scam proceeds.

The investigation is described as ongoing; the page treats Wickham as a still-open joint investigation with continuing money-laundering charges layered on top of the Long River / Changjiang network disruption.

> [!warning] Source-side asymmetry
> Public reporting in this wiki is anchored on the AFP statement. Numerical claims (AUD 160 million stolen worldwide; AUD 100 million laundered by the seventh-charged accused; AUD 21 million restrained) are AFP-attributed, not independently verified here.

## Background

The wider Long River money-laundering organisation and its alleged operating arm, the Changjiang Currency Exchange, are the subject of a parallel and larger AFP money-laundering takedown announced separately on 25 October 2023 — the day before this Operation Wickham release — under the headline "Seven syndicate members charged for allegedly laundering almost AUD 229 million." That parallel takedown surrounded the Changjiang / Long River infrastructure with arrests, restraining orders and parallel charges. Operation Wickham is the AFP-USSS *cybercrime joint-investigation* lens onto the same network: it focuses specifically on the laundering of scam-derived proceeds — particularly from US-victim investment fraud routed through manipulated MetaTrader and cryptocurrency trading platforms — that flowed through that Australian remittance corridor.

The Australian leg targets domestic [[money-laundering-ic|money-laundering]] and unregistered-remittance offences, while the US side concerns the underlying [[online-fraud-ic|investment-fraud]] victimisation in the [[united-states|United States]]. Together, the two legs illustrate how AU–US bilateral cybercrime cooperation can begin with a US-side proactive notification on outbound scam proceeds rather than an Australian intelligence push.

## Participating Parties

- **Lead Australian agency / requesting partner on Australian arrests:** [[australia-afp|Australian Federal Police (AFP)]] through the AFP-led JPC3.
- **Co-lead US agency / originating notifier:** [[us-secret-service|United States Secret Service (USSS)]] — referred the cross-border money flow that originated the case in August 2022.
- **Australian states' contribution:** Search-warrant execution at residences in Sydney and Melbourne on 25 October 2023 was supported under Australian domestic policing arrangements; specific state-police partner agencies are not separately named in the AFP release ingested here.

## Legal Framework

The AFP release does not name a specific MLAT or treaty basis for the joint investigation. Cooperation is described in terms of a USSS-to-AFP JPC3 referral followed by a joint AFP–USSS investigation; charges and restraining-order proceedings are executed under domestic Australian law. The two charges laid against the seventh-charged accused are:

1. Recklessly engaging in conduct that resulted in dealing with proceeds of general crime (value exceeding AUD 10 million) — maximum 15 years' imprisonment.
2. Providing unregistered remittance services — maximum 2 years' imprisonment.

Any formal mutual legal assistance traffic between Australia and the United States that supports Wickham (e.g., for receipt of US victim-side evidence by AFP, or for AFP financial-records material flowing back to USSS / DOJ) is not addressed in the public release.

## Operational Timeline

- **22 August 2022 — operation opened:** USSS advises AFP JPC3 that millions of dollars in US-investment-fraud proceeds are being transferred into an Australian business bank account; AFP opens Operation Wickham as the Australian-side joint investigation.
- **22 August 2022 – 25 October 2023 — investigation:** AFP works the Australian-side money-laundering layer; six earlier defendants are charged across this period (not separately indexed in this wiki at time of ingest).
- **25 October 2023 — search warrants:** AFP executes search warrants at residences in Sydney and Melbourne; the seventh defendant is taken into custody locally (no extradition).
- **26 October 2023 — public release:** AFP announces charges against the seventh alleged offender — a 37-year-old Chinese national resident in Melbourne — for laundering AUD 100 million+ through the Changjiang Currency Exchange and Australian shell-company accounts.
- **Post-26 October 2023 — ongoing:** Operation Wickham continues; further charges and restraining-order proceedings are not detailed in the release.

## Cooperation Mechanisms Used

- **Police-to-police joint investigation:** AFP–USSS, channelled via the AFP-led JPC3 cybercrime liaison structure. Categorised here as [[informal-cooperation|informal-cooperation]] in the absence of a cited treaty basis in the public release.
- **Domestic-charging vehicle:** Australian proceeds-of-crime and unregistered-remittance offences as the disruption tool for the Australian-side laundering layer.

## Challenges and Friction Points

- **Treaty-citation opacity:** The public release does not reveal whether US–Australia MLAT mechanisms (or the [[budapest-convention]]'s evidence-cooperation provisions) supported any evidentiary transfer between USSS and AFP. Wickham, in its public form, illustrates how high-trust bilateral cybercrime cooperation can run on liaison channels and joint-investigation framing without a public MLAT citation, leaving the formal-mechanism layer invisible to outside observers — a recurring [[jurisdictional-conflicts|cross-border-investigation transparency]] gap.
- **Embedded-laundering corridor:** Wickham targets a laundering layer hosted inside a nominally legitimate AU currency-exchange / remittance corridor (Changjiang Currency Exchange / Long River), requiring AML and unregistered-remittance charges rather than treaty-based extradition or production orders to make disruption stick.

## Lessons Learned

- USSS-to-AFP proactive notification on outbound scam-proceeds flows is, on the Wickham record, sufficient on its own to open a high-value, multi-defendant Australian-side money-laundering investigation — strengthening the case for institutionalising standing trust channels (here JPC3) between cybercrime liaison units rather than relying solely on case-by-case MLAT requests for follow-the-money work across the AU–US corridor.
- Major scam-money laundering layers can sit in nominally legitimate currency-exchange and remittance corridors inside the requested-state economy. Wickham illustrates that domestic AML and unregistered-remittance offences may be the practical disruption tool for those layers, even where the underlying [[online-fraud-ic|fraud]] victimisation is mainly extra-territorial.
