# Operation Chargeback — Koblenz-BKA-led 10-jurisdiction takedown of payment-processor-compromise card-fraud and money-laundering networks (Nov 2025)

## Summary

On **4 November 2025**, the Landeszentralstelle Cybercrime (LZC) of the **Generalstaatsanwaltschaft Koblenz** (Rhineland-Palatinate, Germany) and the [[germany-bka|Bundeskriminalamt (BKA)]] led a coordinated action day across ten jurisdictions, dismantling three transnational organised-crime networks that had been compromising regulated German payment-service-providers since 2016 and using the access to draw more than 19 million fake recurring-subscription charges from approximately 4.3 million cardholders in 193 countries. Eighteen arrest warrants were executed (five in Germany), 44 suspects were identified, more than 60 search locations were searched worldwide (29 in Germany across eight federal states), and approximately EUR 35 million in assets were seized in Germany alone. Total estimated damage exceeds EUR 300 million.

[[eurojust|Eurojust]] coordinated the multi-jurisdictional judicial action and [[europol-ec3|Europol EC3]] coordinated the law-enforcement operational picture. Germany was supported by the cooperating jurisdictions explicitly named by the Koblenz prosecutor — **United Kingdom, Italy, Canada, Luxembourg, Netherlands, Singapore, Spain, United States, Cyprus**.

> [!info] Confidence
> The cooperating-jurisdiction roster and action-day metrics rest on the Koblenz prosecutor own-domain release, cross-confirmed by BKA, Europol, Eurojust and Zoll-FIU within 24 hours. Confidence: **almost certainly** accurate as published.

## Background

The investigation began at the Koblenz LZC in 2020 after suspicious chargeback patterns were reported by acquiring banks and payment-service-providers regulated by BaFin (Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht). Forensic analysis showed that malicious software had been implanted in the providers' processing systems, allowing the criminals to silently route fraudulent charges through legitimate-looking merchant accounts and disguise them as ordinary subscription billing. The criminal-business model layered:

1. **Stolen-card supply** — credit-card data from approximately 4.3 million victims across 193 countries acquired through unrelated breaches and dark-web markets (2016–2021).
2. **Fake-merchant infrastructure** — more than 2,000 professionally-built websites mimicking streaming, dating and entertainment services, each operating through approximately 500 shell companies registered in low-scrutiny jurisdictions, designed solely to receive small recurring charges that fall below cardholder-attention thresholds.
3. **Payment-processor compromise** — malware-enabled abuse of regulated German payment-service-providers to give the fake-merchants a "legitimate" channel into the card networks.
4. **Laundering** — > 100,000 individual laundering acts moving the proceeds through corporate accounts in multiple jurisdictions.

## Legal Framework

- **Charges (Germany)**: gewerbs- und bandenmäßiger Computerbetrug (gang- and commercially-organised computer fraud), Geldwäsche (money laundering), Missbrauch von Zahlungsdienstleistern.
- **Coordination instrument**: Eurojust coordination meetings between German, Italian, Canadian, Luxembourgish, Dutch, Singaporean, Spanish, US and Cypriot judicial authorities, supported by Europol's operational task-forcing.
- **Cross-border evidence**: MLAT requests across the nine foreign partner jurisdictions for parallel search-and-seizure on the action day.

## Cooperation Mechanisms Used

- **[[eurojust-coordination-meeting|Eurojust coordination meetings]]** between the 10 partner-jurisdictions' prosecutors, allowing parallel judicial action on a single action day.
- **[[europol-jit|Europol operational coordination]]** through EC3 / European Cybercrime Centre, providing the cross-border operational picture and connecting the German payment-processor evidence to the foreign-jurisdiction merchant-side activity.
- **MLAT** requests for foreign searches, seizures and arrests in UK, Italy, Canada, Luxembourg, Netherlands, Singapore, Spain, US and Cyprus.
- **Financial-supervisor / FIU integration** — BaFin (regulator of compromised payment-service-providers) and FIU Deutschland (money-laundering analysis) integrated directly into the investigation, illustrating supervisory-financial-LE convergence.
- **[[asset-freezing]]** and **[[asset-recovery]]** — EUR 35M secured in Germany on day one.

## Challenges and Friction Points

- Five-year investigation horizon (2020–2025) reflects the difficulty of attributing payment-processor compromise to specific individuals when the criminal-side websites are professionally separated from the laundering shell-companies via multiple jurisdictions.
- 193 victim-countries make full victim-notification impossible at action-day scale; restitution will depend on per-jurisdiction follow-on civil and criminal proceedings.
- The cooperating-jurisdiction list (10 countries) does not enumerate the specific foreign LE counterparts by name in the Koblenz release — only the partner jurisdictions are named. (BKA, Europol and Eurojust mirror releases provide partial agency-level naming for follow-on enrichment.)

## Lessons Learned

- Long-running prosecutor-led cybercrime investigations (Koblenz LZC, since 2020) **can** survive multi-year evidence collection across 10 jurisdictions and produce simultaneous action-day arrests when Eurojust and Europol provide structural coordination.
- Integration of the financial supervisor (BaFin) and FIU into a cybercrime investigation — not as a downstream notification recipient but as part of the operational team — provides direct visibility into the payment-processor compromise that is otherwise invisible to LE-only investigations.

## Follow-Up Actions

- Suspects remanded to pre-trial detention in Germany pending indictment by Koblenz prosecutor.
- Asset-recovery proceedings ongoing in Germany; mutual-recognition asset-freezing requests in foreign partner jurisdictions ongoing.
- Cardholder-notification handled through respective issuing banks across the 193 affected jurisdictions.
