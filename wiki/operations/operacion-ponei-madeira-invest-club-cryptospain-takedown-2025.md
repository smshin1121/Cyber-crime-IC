## Summary

On **2025-11-06**, the [[spain-guardia-civil|Spanish Guardia Civil]] (Departamento contra el Cibercrimen / UCO) announced the arrest of Spanish national **A.R.**, publicly known on social media as **"CryptoSpain"**, as the alleged leader and ultimate person responsible for an international criminal organisation operating the **Madeira Invest Club (MIC)**. The Guardia Civil identifies the action as **"Operación PONEI."**

The investigation attributes to the MIC network **more than 3,000 victims** and **more than EUR 260 million** in global fraud proceeds. The release identifies the scheme as a classical **pyramid/ponzi** structure, dressed as a "private investment club" offering fixed-yield buy-back contracts on real estate, luxury vehicles, yachts, whisky, gold, and cryptocurrencies. Investments were formalised through digital-art purchase contracts the club promised to repurchase within fixed terms with preset profits — but, per Guardia Civil findings, no real underlying economic activity existed.

> [!note] Confidence
> The operation, scale figures (3,000+ victims, EUR 260M+), and foreign-LE-counterpart roster are taken directly from the Guardia Civil's official press release and are highly likely accurate as published; downstream charging counts, asset-freeze totals, and crypto-seizure figures are not specified in the primary release and are recorded as missing fields.

## Background

MIC began operating in early 2023. The Guardia Civil release describes the scheme's external face — a "club privado de inversión" with promises of fixed returns and guaranteed buy-back via digital-art purchase contracts — and the underlying reality — a pyramid structure paying earlier participants with funds from later participants. The principal arrest, A.R. ("CryptoSpain"), is presented as the alleged ultimate director of the criminal organisation.

The corporate-and-banking layer of the network is mapped across multiple jurisdictions: **Spain, Portugal, United Kingdom, Albania, Dominican Republic, United States, Malaysia, Belgium, Thailand, and Hong Kong**. Per L24, only those jurisdictions whose LE/prosecutorial agencies are **explicitly named in the tier-1 primary source as cooperating counterparts** appear in `participating_countries` above; the remaining jurisdictions are recorded here as money-flow / corporate-banking layer geography.

## Legal Framework

Spanish criminal proceedings:
- Investigating court: **Juzgado Central de Instrucción nº 4 de la Audiencia Nacional**
- Prosecuting authority: **Fiscalía contra la Corrupción y la Criminalidad Organizada**
- Predicate offences (per Guardia Civil framing): organised criminal-organisation membership (estafa, blanqueo de capitales) — fraud and money-laundering

International cooperation framework:
- EUROPOL coordination of multi-country information-sharing among the named foreign LE counterparts.
- The primary release **does not** name a formal Joint Investigation Team (JIT) or any specific MLAT instrument; the cooperation is described as collaboration ("colaboración") rather than a treaty-based instrument.

## Lessons Learned

- **5-jurisdiction Spain-led pattern with explicit Southeast-Asian LE triad:** Operación PONEI is the first wiki-recorded Spain-led crypto investment fraud cooperation that explicitly names Singapore Police Force, Royal Malaysia Police, and Royal Thai Police together with US HSI under EUROPOL coordination — broadening the SNA pattern beyond the EU + US axis typical of EFECC-coordinated crypto fraud takedowns.
- **L24 split between cooperating LE jurisdictions and money-flow-only jurisdictions:** Portugal, UK, Albania, Dominican Republic, Belgium, and Hong Kong are mentioned in the primary release as corporate-and-banking-layer jurisdictions but **not** as cooperating LE counterparts, and are therefore not included in `participating_countries`. This is the canonical L24 split for multi-layer transnational fraud takedowns where the laundering footprint is wider than the cooperating LE roster.
- **Digital-art-wrapped ponzi typology:** the MIC scheme uses digital-art purchase-and-buy-back contracts as the formal vehicle for investor inflows — a typology variant on classical ponzi structures that overlaps with NFT / digital-asset narratives without an underlying tokenised-art technical layer per the primary release.
