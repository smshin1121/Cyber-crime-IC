## Summary

On **2025-05-16**, the **[[new-zealand|New Zealand]] Police Financial Crime Group** announced the arrest in **Auckland** of a **Wellington-based individual** as part of an investigation led by the **U.S. [[fbi|Federal Bureau of Investigation]]** into a transnational cryptocurrency-theft and money-laundering ring. The case is framed in the cited release as a **joint NZ Police / FBI investigation** supporting a **U.S. [[us-doj|Department of Justice]]** federal indictment under racketeering, wire-fraud-conspiracy, and money-laundering-conspiracy statutes.

The underlying conspiracy is alleged to have stolen approximately **US$265 million** (NZD$450 million) in cryptocurrency from **seven victims** between **March and August 2024**, laundered across multiple cryptocurrency platforms. **Coordinated search warrants** were executed across Auckland, Wellington and **California** over a three-day period, yielding **13 arrests** in total across the New Zealand and U.S. legs of the operation.

## Background

The press release describes a multi-victim cryptocurrency-theft operation conducted between March and August 2024 in which an organised criminal group fraudulently obtained cryptocurrency from seven victims and laundered it across multiple platforms. The U.S. statement quoted in the New Zealand Police release describes the proceeds-of-crime spending pattern as including approximately US$9 million on exotic cars, watches, jewellery, designer handbags and clothing, designer luxury goods, rented luxury homes in Los Angeles, the Hamptons and Miami, hundreds-of-thousands-of-dollars per-night nightclub spending, and private-jet travel.

## Cooperation Mechanisms Used

The cited release describes three discrete IC mechanism classes:

1. **NZ Police – FBI bilateral joint investigation.** The NZ Police Financial Crime Group is described as participating in an FBI investigation. This is the wiki's first tier-1 primary record of an NZ Police – FBI joint cybercrime investigation as a discrete IC channel class.
2. **Coordinated multi-jurisdictional same-week search-warrant execution.** The press release explicitly enumerates a three-day window in which search warrants were executed across Auckland, Wellington and California — a tempo and coupling distinctly faster than the standard MLAT pathway and tighter than J-CAT-style intelligence-sharing.
3. **U.S. federal indictment of a foreign-arrested defendant.** The Wellington-based suspect was arrested in New Zealand and concurrently indicted in the United States under federal RICO and conspiracy statutes. The release implies a future U.S. extradition pathway (via the indictment) but does not assert that a formal extradition request has been transmitted.

## Challenges and Friction Points

## Contradictions & Open Questions

- **Aggregate-theft framing.** The NZ Police release frames the underlying theft as approximately US$265 million; U.S. court records reportedly describe the aggregate as approximately US$263 million with the largest single-victim loss at approximately US$230 million (4,100 BTC, 18 August 2024). The figures reflect different valuation cuts of the same underlying conspiracy and are *not* a contradiction of fact, only of framing.
- **Defendant identity.** The Wellington-based defendant is unnamed in the cited release (interim name suppression); cross-jurisdictional defendant-level mapping is deferred until a non-suppressed record (e.g. the U.S. superseding indictment or a later NZ release) is ingested.
- **Extradition status.** The release implies a U.S. extradition pathway via the DOJ indictment but does not assert that an extradition request has been transmitted, granted, or contested.
- **NZ domestic charging.** Whether the Wellington-based suspect is charged under New Zealand domestic law (in addition to the U.S. federal indictment) is not stated.
