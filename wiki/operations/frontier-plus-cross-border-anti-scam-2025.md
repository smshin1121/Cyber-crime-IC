## Summary

Between **28 April and 28 May 2025**, the [[hong-kong-police|Hong Kong Police Force]] led the first joint enforcement operation conducted under the **Cross-border Anti-Scam Collaboration Platform "FRONTIER+"**, working with police forces of [[malaysia|Malaysia]], the [[maldives|Maldives]], [[singapore|Singapore]], [[south-korea|South Korea]], [[thailand|Thailand]], and the Macao Special Administrative Region of the People's Republic of China.

The month-long operation resulted in **1,858 arrests** (ages 14–81), addressed **9,268 scam cases**, froze **32,607 bank accounts**, and intercepted approximately **US$20 million** of an aggregate victim-loss pool of **US$225 million**. Over **2,700 officers** were deployed across the seven jurisdictions. *Highly likely* (≥80%) the operation marks the first multi-jurisdictional outcome of FRONTIER+ since the platform's October 2024 formalisation.

## Background

FRONTIER+ — the **Cross-border Anti-Scam Collaboration Platform** — was jointly established by various national/regional anti-scam centres in **October 2024**. Per the Hong Kong release of 2025-06-03, platform membership stands at 10 jurisdictions: Australia, Canada, Hong Kong SAR, Indonesia, Macao SAR, Malaysia, the Maldives, [[singapore|Singapore]], [[south-korea|South Korea]], and [[thailand|Thailand]].

> [!note] Platform-membership discrepancy
> The Singapore Police Force's *CAD Annual Report 2024* (publish date 2025-12; raw `2025-12-01_police-gov-sg_cad-report-2024-operation-first-light.md`) describes FRONTIER+ formalisation on **22 October 2024** and lists **11** member jurisdictions, additionally naming **Brunei**. Both figures are taken from tier-1 primary sources; the discrepancy is preserved as published.

The platform's core functions are real-time intelligence exchange, periodic joint operations, and replication of anti-scam centre models across non-member jurisdictions. The April–May 2025 operation was its **first formal joint enforcement deployment**.

## Participating Parties

**Participating jurisdictions in this operation (7):** Hong Kong SAR, Macao SAR, Malaysia, the Maldives, Singapore, South Korea, Thailand.

**Lead participating agencies:**

- [[hong-kong-police|Hong Kong Police Force — Commercial Crime Bureau (Anti-Deception Coordination Centre)]] — operation lead.
- Macao Judiciary Police — Anti-Fraud Co-ordination Centre (no dedicated wiki page yet; original-language designation: *Polícia Judiciária*).
- [[royal-malaysia-police|Royal Malaysia Police (Polis Diraja Malaysia)]].
- Maldives Police Service (no dedicated wiki page yet).
- [[singapore-police|Singapore Police Force — Anti-Scam Command (within the Commercial Affairs Department)]].
- [[knpa|Korean National Police Agency]] — National Counter Scam Center / equivalent counter-fraud division.
- [[thailand-royal-police|Royal Thai Police — Cyber Crime Investigation Bureau]].

## Legal Framework

The operation rests on **each jurisdiction's domestic anti-fraud, cybercrime, and money-laundering powers**, executed in parallel rather than under a single multilateral instrument. There is no indication in the primary source that mutual-legal-assistance treaties were the principal vehicle; the operation instead relied on **police-to-police real-time intelligence exchange** through FRONTIER+'s sister-centre architecture (cf. [[informal-cooperation]]). Account freezes and asset interception in each jurisdiction were executed under domestic financial-investigation powers.

Predominant **scam typologies** addressed: government-official impersonation, customer-service impersonation, investment scams, rental scams, online romance scams, friend impersonation scams, employment scams, online shopping scams.

The operation's **financial choke-point impact** — 32,607 accounts frozen and ~US$20 million intercepted — is *likely* (55–80%) a more durable disruption than arrests alone, given the cross-border money-laundering layer characteristic of these scam syndicates.

## Cooperation Mechanisms Used

- **FRONTIER+** sister-centre intelligence exchange (real-time data sharing among Anti-Deception Coordination Centre / Anti-Scam Command / Anti-Fraud Co-ordination Centre / National Counter Scam Center / Cyber Crime Investigation Bureau / Maldives Police equivalent).
- **Coordinated simultaneous enforcement** in each jurisdiction under domestic powers ([[informal-cooperation]]).
- **Bank-account freezing under domestic financial-investigation powers** (no cross-border seizure mechanism described in the primary source).

The HK release explicitly contrasts FRONTIER+ with prior models by emphasising **"real-time intelligence analysis and sharing"** rather than ex-post evidence requests.

## Challenges and Friction Points

The primary source does not enumerate friction points, but the release flags as a continuing operational challenge:

- **Cross-jurisdictional script migration**: HK 2024 customer-service-impersonation scripts began appearing in Singapore and Macao SAR in 2025, requiring continuously refreshed shared intelligence.
- The **Maldives** recorded 199 subjects interviewed but **0 arrests**, suggesting either screening/triage outcomes or a domestic prosecutorial threshold issue — not explained in source.
- The HKSAR "subjects interviewed" cell is reported as unavailable in the Annex.

## Lessons Learned

1. *Highly likely* (≥80%) that real-time anti-scam-centre intelligence sharing (sister-centre model) is faster and more enforcement-relevant for high-volume telephone-and-online scam syndicates than formal MLAT — but it does not substitute for MLAT for evidentiary admissibility in trial.
2. Scam-script cross-jurisdiction migration patterns (HK → Singapore + Macao SAR in 2025) are best detected through joint platforms rather than single-jurisdiction monitoring.
3. Asset freezes in destination jurisdictions can outpace investigative completion in source jurisdictions, making coordinated timing critical (a structural challenge for [[informal-cooperation]] frameworks).

## Follow-Up Actions

Per the HKSAR release, FRONTIER+ "will continue to conduct real-time intelligence analysis and sharing, carry out cross-border joint operations from time to time, and expand its network by inviting more countries and regions to join". *Highly likely* additional joint operations will follow, with potential expansion of platform membership beyond the current 10–11 jurisdictions.

## Korean Involvement (한국의 참여)

The [[knpa|Korean National Police Agency]] participated in the FRONTIER+ first joint operation, contributing **2,190 subjects interviewed**, **116 arrests**, **2,275 scam cases addressed**, **US$68.2 million (KRW 88.6 billion)** in identified victim losses, **7,105 bank accounts frozen**, and approximately **US$3.69 million (KRW 4.8 billion)** recovered or seized. South Korea recorded the **single largest victim-loss figure of any participating jurisdiction**, *highly likely* reflecting the maturity and reach of Korean voice-phishing and impersonation-scam syndicates rather than enforcement weakness. Korea's participation through the National Counter Scam Center (NCSC, 보이스피싱 합동대응) framework reinforces South Korea's continued integration into Asia-Pacific anti-scam cooperative structures complementary to its earlier engagement in [[cross-border-online-cse-operation-spf-2026]].

## Contradictions & Open Questions

> [!warning] FRONTIER+ membership count discrepancy
> **Claim A** (HKSAR Government Information Services, 2025-06-03, reliability: high): platform includes anti-scam centres from **10** countries and regions — Australia, Canada, Hong Kong SAR, Indonesia, Macao SAR, Malaysia, the Maldives, Singapore, South Korea, Thailand.
> **Claim B** (Singapore Police Force *CAD Annual Report 2024*, publish date 2025-12, reliability: high): platform was formalised 22 October 2024 with **11** jurisdictions, additionally naming **Brunei**.
> **Assessment**: *Roughly even chance* either listing is more current. The two reports differ in publish date by ~6 months; Brunei accession may have occurred between October 2024 and June 2025 (after HK release) or vice-versa. Both are tier-1 primary sources and both are preserved.

Open questions:

- Per-jurisdiction arrest/account-freeze breakdown does not specify what fraction of arrests were of syndicate principals versus low-level money mules.
- The 0-arrest outcome for the Maldives (against 199 subjects interviewed and 366 accounts frozen) is unexplained in the source.
- Indonesia, Australia, and Canada are FRONTIER+ members but did not contribute to this first joint operation per the Annex; the operational scope of their future involvement is undefined.
- The HK release does not name a coordinating prosecutorial body; *unlikely* prosecutions will be coordinated centrally — each jurisdiction *almost certainly* prosecutes domestically.
