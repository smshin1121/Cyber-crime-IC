## Summary

This operation is the Canadian-side downstream prosecution of two Toronto residents alleged to have been among the top 50 worldwide subscribers of iSpoof.cc, a caller-ID spoofing-as-a-service platform that the UK Metropolitan Police took down in November 2022 under Operation Elaborate with Europol and Eurojust coordination. On 2025-02-19 the Ontario RCMP Cybercrime Investigative Team (CIT) Toronto, part of [[canada-rcmp|Royal Canadian Mounted Police]] Central Region (Ontario), executed a search warrant on the suspects' residence and arrested **Chakib Mansouri (29)** and **Majdouline Alouah (31)**. The release was issued the next day, 2025-02-20, by RCMP – Central Region.

The investigation identified at least 570 Canadian victims of spoofing, phishing and smishing schemes in which the suspects impersonated banks, government employees and police using the iSpoof.cc service to display falsified caller IDs. The release states "millions of dollars" in Canadian losses and explicitly anticipates that "the number of identified victims will increase as a result of the execution of search warrants."

## Background

iSpoof.cc was a subscription-based caller-ID spoofing platform that, at peak, had as many as 38,000 worldwide subscribers. Subscribers paid for the ability to display a falsified caller ID — typically the genuine number of a bank, government department, or police force — to deceive victims into surrendering account credentials, one-time passcodes, or to induce wire transfers. The platform was taken offline in November 2022 in a UK Metropolitan Police-led operation conducted with Europol and Eurojust support; that takedown produced an intelligence package on the platform's most active subscribers worldwide, with prosecutions of high-volume subscribers expected to follow in their respective home jurisdictions.

The Ontario RCMP arrest of Mansouri and Alouah is one such follow-on prosecution: the Canadian release explicitly identifies them as "among the top 50 most active subscribers in the world." The intelligence chain implied by the partner list — UK Metropolitan Police → Dutch National Police → EUROPOL → EUROJUST → RCMP — is consistent with a Europol-mediated package being operationalised by a non-EU partner agency to support domestic Canadian charges.

## Legal Framework

Charges laid against both Mansouri and Alouah under the Canadian Criminal Code:

- s.380(1) — Fraud
- s.342.1 — Unauthorized use of Computer
- s.462.31 — Laundering Proceeds of Crime
- s.430(1.1) — Unauthorized Possession of Credit Card Data
- s.354 — Possessing the Proceeds of Crime

The release does not specify whether a formal Mutual Legal Assistance request, [[budapest-convention|Budapest Convention]] Article 26 spontaneous information channel, or only informal Europol/Eurojust-mediated intelligence sharing was used to obtain the underlying iSpoof.cc subscriber data from European partners. As of the publication date (2025-02-20), Canada is a party to the Budapest Convention (ratified 2015-07-08) but had not signed the [[second-additional-protocol|Second Additional Protocol]] on enhanced cooperation and disclosure of electronic evidence.

## Results and Impact

- 2 arrests (Chakib Mansouri, 29; Majdouline Alouah, 31).
- 570 confirmed Canadian victims at time of announcement; final scope after device analysis revised to "literally thousands" per [[2025-10-30_rcmp-ca-gazette_how-rcmp-took-down-prolific-cyber-fraudsters|RCMP Gazette 2025-10-30]].
- "Millions of dollars" in aggregate Canadian victim losses (no precise figure stated by RCMP in either source).
- Search warrant yielded **40 mobile phones** plus other electronic devices (per Gazette 2nd source).
- Five Criminal Code charges per suspect.

### Court Outcome (per 2nd source, RCMP Gazette 2025-10-30)

- **August 2025**: both defendants plead guilty to all 5 charges (fraud / unauthorized use of computer / laundering proceeds of crime / unauthorized possession of credit card data / possessing proceeds of crime).
- **Mansouri**: 4 years 9 months custodial sentence.
- **Alouah**: 2 years 9 months custodial sentence.
- **Pre-trial-detention precedent**: Alouah remanded into custody on primary, secondary and tertiary grounds (Mansouri did not seek bail) — Cpl Erin Power notes the Justice of the Peace's reasoning can now be cited by prosecutors in future Canadian cybercrime bail hearings.

The 1st-source press release does not state whether any cryptocurrency, fiat currency, or specific dollar value of assets was seized at the residence, nor whether any victim notification programme was launched; the 2nd source confirms laundering channels were online casinos and cryptocurrency accounts but does not quantify the value seized.

## Cooperation Mechanisms Used

- **Informal international cooperation** through Europol's [[europol-ec3|European Cybercrime Centre (EC3)]] and [[eurojust|Eurojust]] — the standard non-EU partner model for Europol-mediated intelligence packages.
- **Bilateral agency-to-agency cooperation** between [[canada-rcmp|RCMP]] and [[uk-metropolitan-police|London Metropolitan Police]] and the [[netherlands-politie|Dutch National Police]].
- **Domestic interagency cooperation** within Canada (RCMP, municipal police, regulator, victim-facing centre, federal cybercrime coordination).

The 1st-source release frames the case explicitly as an exemplar of cross-border cybercrime collaboration. Inspector Lina Dabit:

> "This investigation underscores the critical importance of international cooperation in the global fight against cybercrime. In our interconnected world where cyber threats transcend borders, collaboration between law enforcement agencies, both domestically and internationally, is essential."

The 2nd source ([[2025-10-30_rcmp-ca-gazette_how-rcmp-took-down-prolific-cyber-fraudsters|RCMP Gazette 2025-10-30]]) supplies the verbatim L24 ≥2-LE cooperation passage — primary-source attestation of the UK-Canada information-sharing event that founded the Canadian investigation:

> "Canadian law enforcement first got wind of the case in late 2022, when police in the U.K. notified the RCMP about an account on a platform called iSpoof.cc being used to defraud victims in Canada. ... Doing so required the Ontario RCMP Cybercrime Unit to cooperate with several domestic and international partners including the London Metropolitan Police, the Dutch National Police, EUROPOL, EUROJUST, Toronto Police, Peel Regional Police, FINTRAC, the Canadian Anti-Fraud Centre, and the National Cybercrime Coordination Centre." — RCMP Gazette, 2025-10-30

This passage names ≥2 distinct LE jurisdictions cooperating with the RCMP (UK Metropolitan Police + Dutch National Police), plus 2 EU intergovernmental judicial-cooperation agencies (Europol, Eurojust), satisfying L24 strict ≥2-LE primary-source cooperation requirement.

## Challenges and Friction Points

The release does not document explicit operational friction. Inferable challenges based on the case profile:

- **Time gap between source takedown and downstream prosecution.** The iSpoof.cc takedown occurred in November 2022; the Canadian arrest is February 2025 — roughly a 27-month interval, during which the Canadian-side investigation built domestic evidence sufficient for charges under five Criminal Code sections.
- **Subscriber-list operationalisation.** Translating an intelligence package about subscriber identities (provided through Europol channels) into Canadian-evidence-rule-compliant search warrants and charges requires either a parallel domestic investigation generating independent evidence, or formal MLAT to convert intelligence to admissible evidence. The release does not specify which path was used.

## Contradictions & Open Questions

- The exact aggregate Canadian victim loss is stated only as "millions of dollars" across both RCMP sources; the 2nd source (Gazette 2025-10-30) revises the victim-count estimate from ~600 to "literally thousands" but does not quantify the aggregate dollar figure. A confirming UK / Europol or Dutch source quantifying the iSpoof.cc Canadian-subscriber footprint would resolve the aggregate-loss question.
- Neither RCMP source states whether Mansouri and Alouah are also subject to UK or other foreign charges (the iSpoof.cc UK prosecution focused on the platform operator, Tejay Fletcher, sentenced 2023-05-18 in the UK; subscriber-level prosecutions worldwide were left to home jurisdictions).
- Whether [[budapest-convention|Budapest Convention]] Article 26 spontaneous information sharing or only informal Europol-mediated channels were used to transmit the original UK Met intelligence package to RCMP is not disclosed. The 2nd source's "police in the U.K. notified the RCMP" wording does not distinguish formal Budapest Article 26 from informal LE-to-LE channel.
- Subsequent procedural history (plea, sentence) was resolved by the 2nd source (August 2025 guilty pleas; Mansouri 4y9m; Alouah 2y9m).
