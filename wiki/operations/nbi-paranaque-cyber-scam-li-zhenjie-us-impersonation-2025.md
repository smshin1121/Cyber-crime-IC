## Summary

On 2025-11-21 the **Philippine National Bureau of Investigation — Cybercrime Division (NBI-CCD)** executed a **Warrant to Search, Seize, and Examine Computer Data (WSSECD)**, granted by the Regional Trial Court of Makati on 2025-11-20, against a cyber-scam cell operating out of a condominium unit in **Parañaque City, Metro Manila, Philippines**. The single principal arrested on-site is **Li Zhenjie**, a Chinese national. The substantive Philippine charges are §4(b)(1) (Social Engineering Schemes) of **Republic Act No. 12010** ("Anti-Financial Account Scamming Act," AFASA) in relation to **Republic Act No. 10175** ("Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012").

The cell was using **spoofed contact numbers** to impersonate agents of (i) the **New Haven Police Department in Connecticut, USA** and (ii) the **FBI in Washington**, and threatened victims **located in the United States** with a fictitious nationwide online arrest warrant unless they transferred **cryptocurrency, typically USD 3,000–5,000**, to an account under the alias **"GENERAL."** Investigation was triggered by a confidential informant. NBI-CCD agents executed the warrant with assistance of condominium security personnel and seized devices "containing critical social engineering scripts." OIC Director Atty. **Angelito DLP Magno** is the named NBI authority on the release.

## Background

The Anti-Financial Account Scamming Act (Republic Act No. 12010) was enacted in the Philippines in 2024 and is the primary domestic substantive-law instrument used in this case (§4(b)(1) — Social Engineering Schemes). It is invoked in relation to the older Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012 (Republic Act No. 10175), which provides the underlying cybercrime-offence framework and the operating environment for the Rule on Cybercrime Warrants (A.M. No. 17-11-03-SC, 2018).

The WSSECD is one of the four cybercrime warrants under that Rule (alongside the WDCD, WICD, and WECD) and authorises Philippine law enforcement to search, seize, and examine computer data on identified physical premises. Its use here — granted 2025-11-20 by the RTC of Makati and executed the next day — is consistent with the Rule's fast-issuance design.

## Cooperation Mechanisms Used

The public release names only Philippine domestic mechanisms:

1. **NBI-CCD investigative authority** under Republic Act No. 10175 and AFASA.
2. **WSSECD (Warrant to Search, Seize, and Examine Computer Data)** under the 2018 Rule on Cybercrime Warrants, supporting [[search-seizure|search-and-seizure]] of [[electronic-evidence|electronic evidence]].
3. **Condominium-management cooperation** at the point of warrant execution (assistance of condominium security personnel).

> [!warning] Cooperation-chain attestation gap
> The NBI press release does not name a US, Chinese, or third-country LE counterpart. Cross-border elements present in the conduct itself — US victims, US-agency impersonation, Chinese-national suspect — are not matched by any named bilateral or multilateral cooperation instrument in the public release. This operation is therefore best characterised, on the present source, as a **host-state takedown of an outbound transnational scam cell**, with international-cooperation links pending second-source confirmation.

## Challenges and Friction Points

- **Host-state-only attestation.** The NBI release does not document US-side victim notification, FBI cooperation, or Chinese-side handover/extradition consideration for the suspect's nationality. Whether any of those exist in non-public channels cannot be inferred from this source.
- **Single principal.** The release names only Li Zhenjie as arrested, while using plural language ("perpetrators," "they") about the cell's operations. The number and identity of co-conspirators, and whether any escaped prior to 2025-11-21, are not reported.
- **Modus-operandi attribution.** The "fictitious nationwide online arrest warrant" pretext combined with US-LE-impersonation has been observed in multiple Southeast-Asia-based scam-cell operations targeting US victims; this release is one Philippine attestation but does not link the cell to a wider syndicate or to a named US-side investigation.

## Lessons Learned

> [!note] Confidence
> Likely (55-80%): the lessons below are drawn from a single tier-1 primary source (the NBI press release). They are consistent with the broader wiki picture of Philippine scam-hub takedowns but are not yet corroborated by a second independent tier-1 attestation.

1. **AFASA (RA 12010) is being used in practice for outbound scam infrastructure.** The 2024 Anti-Financial Account Scamming Act §4(b)(1) (Social Engineering Schemes) is invoked as the lead substantive charge for a cell whose victims are entirely in the United States. This evidences host-state criminalisation as an alternative to extradition or international referral in Philippine cybercrime enforcement.
2. **WSSECD enables a 1-day warrant-to-execution turnaround.** Issuance 2025-11-20 → execution 2025-11-21 demonstrates the operational tempo achievable under the 2018 Rule on Cybercrime Warrants for cybercrime evidence preservation in the Philippines.
3. **Cross-border conduct without named cross-border cooperation chain.** The release should be retained as a baseline document of how a Philippine takedown of an outbound US-targeting scam cell can be publicly framed without any US-cooperation attestation. The wiki should track whether subsequent US filings (FBI, DOJ) reference the same cell or victims.
