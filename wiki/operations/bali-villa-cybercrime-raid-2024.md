## Summary

On June 26, 2024, Indonesian immigration officials raided a villa in the Marga subdistrict of Tabanan regency, Bali, detaining 103 foreign nationals — 91 men and 12 women — suspected of conducting cybercrimes targeting victims in other countries, including Malaysia. The operation was led by the Bali Immigration Directorate and resulted in the seizure of approximately 450 mobile phones and dozens of laptops, printers, routers, and identity cards. Because the alleged victims were located outside Indonesia, authorities determined they could not pursue criminal charges under Indonesian jurisdiction and instead opted for deportation on immigration violations (visa misuse). The raid was described as the largest immigration arrest in Indonesia in 2024.

This case is notable from an international cooperation perspective because it starkly illustrates the **jurisdictional gap** in cross-border cybercrime enforcement: a host country may detect criminal activity on its soil but lack jurisdiction to prosecute when all victims are abroad.

## Background

Southeast Asia has become a major hub for cyber-fraud operations, with criminal syndicates establishing scam centers in countries such as Cambodia, Myanmar, Laos, the Philippines, and Indonesia. These operations frequently target victims in other Asian countries through voice phishing (vishing), romance scams, investment fraud, and online gambling schemes.

Taiwan, China, and several Southeast Asian countries have faced growing challenges with their nationals being recruited — sometimes voluntarily, sometimes through trafficking — into these scam compounds. The Bali villa operation reportedly targeted individuals in Malaysia at the time of the raid.

- Taiwan's Ministry of Foreign Affairs initially disputed the characterization, stating that of the 103 arrested, only 14 were suspected of being Taiwanese; the remainder were likely Chinese nationals or others.

> [!note] Nationality Dispute
> Multiple sources (Al Jazeera, Reuters, AP) reported "103 Taiwanese" based on Indonesian immigration officials' statements. However, Taiwan's MOFA stated its de facto embassy was told only 14 of the 103 were suspected Taiwanese. This discrepancy was not fully resolved in public reporting.

## Legal Framework

No international legal framework was formally invoked. Indonesian authorities acted under domestic **immigration law** (visa misuse provisions) rather than criminal law, because:

1. The alleged cybercrimes targeted victims outside Indonesia.
2. Under Indonesian jurisdiction principles, crimes whose effects occur entirely abroad are difficult to prosecute domestically.
3. Deportation for immigration violations (misusing tourist or business visas for illegal activities) was the available enforcement pathway.

> [!warning] Jurisdictional Gap
> This case illustrates a critical challenge in cybercrime international cooperation: when suspects operate from Country A but victimize people in Country B, Country A may lack jurisdiction to prosecute. Without a bilateral or multilateral cooperation mechanism to transfer the case, deportation becomes the default — effectively allowing suspects to avoid criminal accountability.

## Results and Impact

**Immediate Results:**
- 103 foreign nationals detained
- ~450 mobile phones and dozens of electronic devices seized
- No criminal charges filed (deportation only)

**Limitations:**
- Suspects were not prosecuted for the underlying cybercrimes
- Seized evidence was likely not shared with victim countries for prosecution
- Deportation does not prevent suspects from resuming operations elsewhere
- No reported follow-up investigation by Malaysian authorities (the reported victim country)

**Broader Context:**
Bali deported 378 foreigners in Q3 2024 alone, and subsequently launched a dedicated **Cybercrime Unit Targeting Tourists** to address the growing pattern of foreigners using Bali as a base for cyber-fraud operations.

## Cooperation Mechanisms Used

No formal international cooperation mechanisms were employed. This is a notable gap — the operation was handled entirely through immigration enforcement, without:
- [[mlat-process|MLAT]] requests to victim countries
- [[informal-cooperation|Informal police-to-police cooperation]] channels
- Evidence sharing with Malaysian or Taiwanese law enforcement
- [[interpol|INTERPOL]] involvement

This represents a missed opportunity for cooperative enforcement: had Indonesian authorities coordinated with Malaysian law enforcement (the reported victim jurisdiction), evidence gathered during the raid could have supported prosecutions in Malaysia.

## Challenges and Friction Points

1. **Jurisdictional gap** — Indonesia lacked jurisdiction because all alleged victims were abroad. This is a fundamental challenge under traditional territorial jurisdiction principles. See [[jurisdictional-conflicts|Jurisdictional Conflicts]].

2. **Nationality verification** — Discrepancy between Indonesian immigration claims (103 Taiwanese) and Taiwan MOFA's position (only 14 Taiwanese). Distinguishing between PRC and ROC passport holders presents diplomatic sensitivities given cross-strait relations.

3. **No criminal prosecution** — Deportation means suspects face no criminal accountability for the underlying fraud. They can potentially resume operations from another country.

4. **Absence of international cooperation** — No bilateral or multilateral mechanism was activated, despite clear cross-border elements. This may reflect capacity limitations, diplomatic considerations around Taiwan's political status, or the speed at which immigration decisions were made.

5. **Evidence preservation** — It is unclear whether digital forensic evidence from seized devices was preserved in a manner suitable for potential future prosecution by victim-country authorities.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Immigration enforcement is a poor substitute for criminal prosecution** in cybercrime cases. While it provides immediate disruption, it offers no deterrence and no justice for victims.

2. **Pre-established cooperation channels** with likely victim countries would enable rapid case referral when a scam center is discovered.

3. **Digital forensic capacity** at the point of arrest is critical — evidence from seized devices must be preserved even if the arresting country cannot prosecute.

4. **Regional cooperation frameworks** specifically addressing scam compound operations are needed in Southeast Asia.

## Follow-Up Actions

- Bali launched a dedicated cybercrime unit targeting foreign nationals in late 2024.
- Indonesia continued large-scale deportations of foreign nationals involved in cyber-fraud throughout Q3-Q4 2024.
- No confirmed criminal prosecution in any jurisdiction stemming from this raid has been publicly reported.

## Korean Involvement (한국의 참여)

No known Korean involvement in this operation. However, the case is relevant to Korean interests because:
- Korean nationals have been identified as victims of similar Southeast Asian cyber-fraud operations.
- South Korea has faced its own challenges with nationals being trafficked to scam compounds in Southeast Asia (particularly Cambodia and Myanmar).
- The jurisdictional gap highlighted by this case is a challenge Korea also faces when its nationals are victimized by operations based abroad.

## Contradictions & Open Questions

> [!warning] Nationality Contradiction
> **Claim A** (Source: Indonesian immigration officials, Al Jazeera [1]): All 103 detained were Taiwanese nationals.
> **Claim B** (Source: Taiwan MOFA, Taipei Times [3]): Only 14 of the 103 were suspected of being Taiwanese.
> **Assessment**: The discrepancy is *likely* due to Indonesian officials treating all ROC travel document holders as "Taiwanese" without verifying whether some were PRC nationals using different documentation, or due to the political sensitivity of distinguishing between Chinese and Taiwanese nationals.

**Open Questions:**
1. Were the seized electronic devices subjected to forensic analysis? If so, was evidence shared with Malaysian authorities?
2. Were any of the deported individuals subsequently prosecuted in their home jurisdictions?
3. What specific type of cybercrime was being conducted? Sources mention "targeting people in Malaysia" but details of the scam type are sparse.
4. Was this villa operation connected to broader regional scam compound networks?
