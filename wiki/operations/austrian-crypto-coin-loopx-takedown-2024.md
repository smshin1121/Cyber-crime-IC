## Summary

In May 2024, a coordinated action day was held against the operators of a 2017-2018 Austrian-based fake-cryptocurrency exit-scam scheme, named **"Operation LoopX"** by the Austrian Federal Criminal Police Office (Bundeskriminalamt) after the cryptocurrency token brand the criminals had used. The action day was supported by [[eurojust|Eurojust]] and [[europol-ec3|Europol]], with national authorities in **[[austria|Austria]], [[cyprus|Cyprus]] and the [[czech-republic|Czech Republic]]** carrying out arrests and searches on the ground. Eurojust and Europol jointly announced the takedown via tier-1 press releases dated **08 May 2024**.

**Action-day results**: six Austrian suspects arrested; six locations searched; EUR 750,000 in liquid assets, a property worth EUR 1.4 million, and two cars frozen or seized. The Europol parallel release adds EUR 500,000+ in cryptocurrencies and EUR 250,000 in fiat currency seized, plus dozens of bank accounts frozen. **Victim losses**: approximately EUR 6 million from the original 2017-2018 fake initial coin offering (ICO) of 10 million tokens, paid for in bitcoin and ethereum. Not all victims have yet been identified.

## Background

**Modus operandi (fake-ICO exit scam).** Between **December 2017 and February 2018**, the six Austrian suspects pretended to have set up a genuine online trading company that had launched a new cryptocurrency, the **"LoopX" token**. They offered **10 million LoopX tokens** (or respective rights to the new currency) for sale through a fake company website and social-media presence, accepting payment exclusively in established cryptocurrencies — **bitcoin (BTC) and ethereum (ETH)**. To gain credibility with investors, the scammers claimed to have developed their own trading software and a unique algorithm for the sale of the tokens; the underlying "cryptocurrency" had no functional blockchain, wallet implementation, or exchange listing — it existed only as a balance entry on the scammers' website. In **February 2018**, the perpetrators suddenly closed all of the project's social media accounts and took the fake company's website offline — a classic **exit scam** terminating after only ~2 months of ICO operation. Investors then realised they had been defrauded.

### Victim profile and impact

Victim losses total approximately **EUR 6 million** based on identified victims, with the Eurojust release noting **not all victims have yet been identified** (a typical signature of ICO-style retail-investor frauds where victim populations are diffuse and self-report only after media coverage). Victims were retail cryptocurrency investors who held BTC/ETH and were induced to swap them for the worthless LoopX token; the cited release does not enumerate per-country victim distribution but the cross-border nature of the action day (Austria + Cyprus + Czech Republic) and the EU-internal investigative scope imply at least an EU-wide victim cohort. No specific victim count is published in the cited releases; only the aggregate EUR 6 million figure is reported.

### Financial flow

Inflows: victim BTC and ETH transferred to scammer-controlled wallets via the LoopX ICO website during December 2017 – February 2018, totalling approximately EUR 6 million at-time-of-receipt value. Conversion / movement: the six-year prosecution gap (2018→2024 action day) implies multi-stage cryptocurrency-to-fiat conversion, mixer use, and fiat-banking deposit; the Europol parallel release reports the eventual seizure footprint as **EUR 500,000+ in cryptocurrencies + EUR 250,000 in fiat currency + EUR 750,000 in liquid bank assets + EUR 1.4 million property + 2 cars + dozens of bank accounts frozen**, indicating proceeds were converted into a mix of held-crypto, deposited fiat, and physical real-estate / vehicle assets. The action-day seizures (~EUR 3 million combined fiat+crypto+property) recover roughly half of the EUR 6 million victim-loss aggregate.

**Criminal organisation structure.** The OCG is a **six-person Austrian-national group** per the Europol parallel release. The Eurojust release describes them collectively as "the Austrian-based fraudsters" without naming a hierarchy or role assignment. The structure is small-cell (six individuals, single-jurisdiction nationality, single-fraud-vehicle) rather than a large hierarchical OCG — consistent with the small-team profile typical of fake-ICO exit-scam operators in 2017-2018 (the peak ICO-fraud era), where a handful of operators could spin up a fake-token website + social-media presence + Telegram channel + Discord without needing operational infrastructure beyond hosting and marketing. The cited releases do not enumerate per-suspect roles (developer / marketer / treasury / spokesperson). The Austrian-only nationality of all six suspects places this case in a different OCG class from multi-jurisdictional cybercrime OCGs (e.g., [[bayern-tirol-romania-action-day-internet-fraud-2024|the Bayern-Tirol-Romania distributed-cells internet-fraud OCG]]).

The investigation was led by the Austrian [[austria|WKStA]] (Wirtschafts- und Korruptionsstaatsanwaltschaft, the Central Public Prosecutor's Office for Combatting Economic Crimes and Corruption) and the Bundeskriminalamt's Cybercrime Competence Center (C4), with the cooperation toolset described below ultimately enabling the 2024 action day arrests and asset seizures.

## Legal Framework

The cooperation toolset reported in the Eurojust press release rests on the standard intra-EU judicial cooperation mechanism stack:

- **[[european-arrest-warrant|European Arrest Warrants (EAWs)]]** rapidly executed under Eurojust coordination centre support — enabling the arrest of the main suspects in Cyprus and Czechia.
- **Search warrants** executed in Cyprus and the Czech Republic in support of the Austrian investigation.
- **Eurojust coordination centre** providing real-time judicial communication between authorities during the action day.
- **Europol Virtual Command Post** at Eurojust providing the technical operational platform for the action day.
- **Europol mobile office in Cyprus** with a deployed specialist supporting operational activities on the ground.

The cited Eurojust release does not name the specific criminal-procedure articles invoked. Austria, Cyprus, and the Czech Republic are all parties to the [[budapest-convention|Council of Europe Budapest Convention on Cybercrime]], all are EU Member States, and all are signatories to standard EU judicial cooperation instruments including the European Arrest Warrant Framework Decision and the European Investigation Order.

## Cooperation Mechanisms Used

The release explicitly names:

- **Eurojust coordination centre**, enabling real-time communication between all judicial authorities involved and the rapid execution of European Arrest Warrants and search warrants.
- **Europol Virtual Command Post** at Eurojust — Europol's core technical operational product set up at Eurojust for the action day.
- **Five operational meetings** organised by Europol's specialists during the multi-year investigation.
- **Austrian desk at Eurojust** as the lead-jurisdiction-coordination touchpoint with Europol.
- **Europol mobile office in Cyprus** — a deployed specialist providing on-the-ground operational support and exchange of information.
- **Austrian police officers on the ground in Cyprus** during the search, providing advice on cryptocurrency seizure and supporting the interrogation of the main suspect.

## Challenges and Friction Points

The Eurojust release does not enumerate friction points explicitly, but the operational structure implies:

- **Multi-year prosecution gap** between the 2017-2018 offence and the 2024 action day — typical of cross-jurisdictional cryptocurrency-fraud cases.
- **Cryptocurrency seizure expertise concentration** — the Austrian lead jurisdiction had to deploy officers to Cyprus to advise on crypto-seizure procedure, suggesting Cyprus Police did not yet have full domestic crypto-seizure capability.
- **Victim identification incompleteness** — Eurojust notes "not all victims of the fraud have been identified at this stage," typical for ICO-style frauds with diffuse retail victim populations.

## Lessons Learned

- The case is a strong worked example of the **EAW + Eurojust coordination centre + Europol Virtual Command Post** cooperation toolset for intra-EU crypto-fraud action days where the lead investigator (Austria) has to seize assets and arrest suspects in two other host EU jurisdictions (Cyprus, Czech Republic).
- The operational deployment of **Austrian police officers physically present in Cyprus during the search** (specifically advising on cryptocurrency seizure and supporting the main-suspect interrogation) is a notable IC capacity-building motif — the lead-jurisdiction expert is co-deployed with the host-jurisdiction officer.
- This pattern complements the JIT-led pattern used in live-infrastructure CaaS takedowns (e.g., [[latvia-sim-box-cybercrime-as-a-service-takedown-2025]] = Operation SIMCartel JIT Austria/Estonia/Latvia/Finland 2025) — Eurojust coordination centre is the appropriate tool when the underlying investigation is a closed-period historical fraud rather than a live infrastructure takedown.

## Follow-Up Actions

The investigation continues: not all victims have yet been identified, and further victim-notification and asset-tracing work is implied by the Eurojust release. The cited press releases do not enumerate subsequent indictment or trial outcomes.

## Korean Involvement (한국의 참여)

South Korea is not named in the Eurojust press release as a participating jurisdiction or victim country for this operation. ## Contradictions & Open Questions

- The Eurojust release attributes the fraud to "the Austrian-based fraudsters"; the Europol parallel release specifies that the **six suspects are Austrian nationals**. The wiki record uses Europol's clarification.
- The Eurojust release names the Czech participating authority as "**National Organised Crime Agency**" (singular); Europol specifies "**Národní centrála proti organizovanému zločinu (NCOZ)**". NCOZ was the cybercrime-competent unit of the Czech Police until 1 January 2023, when cybercrime competence transferred to the new [[czechia-police|NCTEKK]] (National Centre Against Terrorism, Extremism and Cybercrime). Whether the 2024 action day was supported by NCOZ residual capacity or NCTEKK is not explicitly clarified in the cited releases — both wiki entities are listed as `czech-police` (parent) participation for traceability.
- The cited releases do not disclose whether all six suspects were arrested in Austria, or whether the main suspect was specifically arrested in Cyprus (Larnaca) — the Europol release specifies that Austrian officers were on the ground in Cyprus "during a search" and supported "the interrogation of the main suspect," but the arrest location of each suspect is not enumerated individually.
- The cited releases do not enumerate post-action-day indictments or charging counts in any jurisdiction; the wiki record reflects this with `indictments: 0` and a missing-field marker.
- **L26 gap notes**: (a) Per-country victim distribution (Austria vs. other EU vs. extra-EU) is not enumerated in the cited releases; only aggregate EUR 6 million victim losses across ~unknown N victims is reported. (b) Per-suspect roles within the six-person OCG (developer / marketer / treasury / spokesperson) are not enumerated. (c) The intermediate cryptocurrency-to-fiat conversion pathway (mixer usage, exchanges, banking jurisdictions) between the 2018 inflow and the 2024 seizure mix is not documented in the cited tier-1 releases.
