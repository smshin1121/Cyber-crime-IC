## Summary

On 2026-04-29 the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia sentenced **Thomasz Szabo**, 27, of Romania (online aliases "Plank," "Jonah," "Cypher") to **48 months in federal prison** for leading an online swatting ring that, between late December 2023 and early January 2024, directed false bomb threats and "swatting" calls at more than **75 senior U.S. public officials**, **4 religious institutions**, and **multiple journalists**. Szabo had pleaded guilty on **2025-06-02** to one count of conspiracy and one count of threats involving explosives. He had been **extradited from Romania to the United States in November 2024**.

The case is highly likely to be significant for U.S.–Romania cyber cooperation in 2024–2026: the U.S. press release explicitly describes the Romanian authorities' assistance as "critical to the successful investigation of the case and extradition of Szabo," and it credits the DOJ Office of International Affairs with using mutual legal assistance requests to secure evidence from abroad.

## Facts

- According to the U.S. Attorney's Office (D.C.) statement summarised in the 2026-04-29 release, Szabo founded and led an online community that, starting in **late 2020**, engaged in a pattern of bomb threats and "swatting" — falsely reporting an ongoing threat of violence at a victim's home to provoke an armed police response.
- As leader, Szabo personally made false reports to U.S. law enforcement, including (per the release):
  - a **December 2020** threat to commit a mass shooting at New York City synagogues, and
  - a **January 2021** threat to detonate explosives at the U.S. Capitol and to kill the President-elect.
- Beginning **2023-12-24** and continuing through **early January 2024**, subordinate members of Szabo's group perpetrated a spree of swatting and bomb threats whose victims included, per the release:
  - **at least 25 Members of Congress** (or family members of Members of Congress);
  - **at least 6 then-current or former senior U.S. Executive Branch officials**, including multiple cabinet-level officials;
  - **at least 13 then-current or former senior federal law-enforcement officials**, including the heads of multiple federal law-enforcement agencies;
  - multiple members of the federal judiciary;
  - **at least 27 then-current or former state government officials** (or family members);
  - **at least 4 religious institutions**; and
  - multiple members of the media.
- During the spree period the release quotes one subordinate boasting internally to Szabo: *"I did 25+ swattings today,"* and *"creating massive havoc in [A]merica. $500,000+ in taxpayers wasted in just two days."*

## International Cooperation Elements

### Evidence Gathering

The DOJ Office of International Affairs ("[[office-of-international-affairs]]") provided substantial assistance in **securing evidence from abroad, including through mutual legal assistance requests** (`[[mlat-process]]`, `[[mutual-legal-assistance]]`). The release does not enumerate the individual MLA requests or the specific evidence categories.

### Arrest & Extradition

- Szabo was arrested in **Romania** and **extradited to the United States in November 2024**. The release describes Romanian authorities' assistance as **"critical"** to the case.
- In-country U.S. liaison was provided by the **U.S. Secret Service Bucharest Resident Office** and the **FBI Legat Office in Bucharest**.
- The U.S. Office of International Affairs coordinated the extradition request channel.

None reported in the 2026-04-29 sentencing release.

## Legal Analysis

### Jurisdiction

Prosecuted in the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia. Subject-matter jurisdiction over the conduct rests on threats directed against U.S. federal officials and U.S. soil.

### Key Legal Issues

The release does not enumerate substantive legal disputes. The prosecution is *roughly even chance* (US IC standard, deliberately uncertain in the absence of court documents in this wiki) to have rested on:
- **Conspiracy** (one count, per the plea), and
- **Threats involving explosives** (one count, per the plea).

### Precedent Value

*Likely* significant for two patterns: (1) using **MLAT + extradition** to reach a non-U.S. ringleader for cyber-enabled threat conduct (rather than for financial cybercrime, which is the more common posture); and (2) packaging swatting / hoax bomb-threat conduct as transnational organised cyber-enabled threat activity rather than as discrete prank offences.
