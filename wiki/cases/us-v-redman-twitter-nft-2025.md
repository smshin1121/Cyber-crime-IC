## Summary

Cameron Albert Redman, a 22-year-old Canadian national from Mississauga, Ontario, was sentenced on 2025-07-29 in the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Virginia to 12 months' imprisonment plus restitution after pleading guilty to conspiracy to commit wire fraud, wire fraud, and conspiracy to commit aggravated identity theft. The case was prosecuted by the U.S. Attorney's Office for the Eastern District of Virginia (AUSA Zoe Bedell; U.S. Attorney Erik S. Siebert) and investigated by the FBI Washington Field Office (SAC Reid Davis) with valuable assistance from the [[canada-rcmp|Royal Canadian Mounted Police]] Cybercrime Investigative Team Central Region. Sentencing was before U.S. District Judge Leonie M. Brinkema.

The case is a primary tier-1 record of a fully-attributed Canada → Portugal → United States international cooperation chain in a cybercrime prosecution, with the [[office-of-international-affairs|DOJ Office of International Affairs]] handling the formal extradition step from Portugal in March 2025.

## Facts

According to the DOJ Eastern District of Virginia press release of 2025-07-30, in May 2022 Redman gained unauthorized access to an internal Twitter account-management tool — the "Partner Support Portal" — and made that access available for purchase via an online forum. Redman and his co-conspirators used the resulting administrative access to take over the X (formerly Twitter) accounts of various digital artists and direct the artists' followers to fraudulent websites, where victims attempting to claim what they believed were new NFTs from those artists in fact authorized transactions that drained NFTs and cryptocurrency from their digital wallets. Within a few days, the conspirators defrauded over 200 victims of approximately USD 794,000 in NFTs and cryptocurrency; almost all of the victims were located in the United States.

The RCMP Central Region (Ontario) press release of 2025-09-10 corroborates these facts and adds that the Canadian investigation was led by the RCMP Cybercrime Investigative Team (CIT) Central Region under Inspector Patrick Poulin (Officer in Charge). The RCMP characterized the underlying loss to victims as "several million dollars," a figure higher than the USD 794,000 profit figure stated by DOJ; the wiki preserves both numbers and treats USD 794,000 as the "profited" figure and the RCMP's "several million dollars" as the broader victim-loss figure (highly likely, given that the value of stolen NFTs and cryptocurrency at theft was distinct from the conspirators' realized profits after laundering).

## International Cooperation Elements

### Evidence Gathering

- The RCMP Cybercrime Investigative Team Central Region opened a Canadian investigation when Redman was still in Canada and shared information with the [[fbi|FBI]] in the United States. Both press releases describe this as a collaborative information-sharing relationship rather than a formal Mutual Legal Assistance Treaty request, which is consistent with the [[informal-cooperation|informal-police-cooperation]] pattern frequently used between the two G7 partners.

### Arrest and Extradition

- Redman departed Canada at some point after the initial 2022 conduct.
- He was located and arrested in Portugal by Portuguese authorities (presumed to be the [[portugal-policia-judiciaria|Polícia Judiciária]], although the press releases do not specify the arresting Portuguese unit).
- The [[office-of-international-affairs|U.S. DOJ Office of International Affairs]] "provided substantial assistance to secure the arrest and March 2025 extradition from Portugal" (DOJ EDVA, 2025-07-30).
- Extradition occurred in March 2025; arraignment, plea, and sentencing in the Eastern District of Virginia followed within four months, indicating an expedited post-extradition trajectory.

### Asset Recovery

- Sentencing included a restitution order to victims, but the dollar value of the restitution and the mechanism for its recovery are not stated in either primary press release.

## Legal Analysis

### Jurisdiction

The Eastern District of Virginia exercised jurisdiction on the basis that the conduct's effects were felt by victims across the United States and that the prosecuted offenses (wire fraud, conspiracy, aggravated identity theft) are federal U.S. offenses; the [[extraterritorial-jurisdiction|extraterritorial]] reach was unproblematic given that Redman was a foreign national but the harms were domestic and the underlying tool (Twitter Partner Support Portal access) was a U.S. corporate asset.

### Key Legal Issues

- [[extradition-practice|Extradition practice]]: Portugal extradited Redman to the United States. Portugal does not extradite for offenses carrying a sentence of more than 25 years, but the U.S. wire-fraud counts here (each carrying a 20-year statutory maximum) are well below that threshold, and aggravated identity theft carries a 2-year mandatory consecutive term — comfortably within the Portuguese extradition framework.
- Specialty: the U.S. prosecution charged conspiracy to commit wire fraud, wire fraud, and conspiracy to commit aggravated identity theft — likely consistent with the offenses for which extradition was granted, though the primary releases do not enumerate the granted offenses.
- Single-prosecution disposition: Canada formally concluded its parallel investigation when the U.S. prosecution succeeded, deferring to the forum where the bulk of victims and the prosecuting evidence resided. This is a practical application of the resource-sharing logic that avoids [[ne-bis-in-idem]] complications and double-jeopardy in transnational cybercrime.

### Precedent Value

The case has limited formal precedential weight (one-year sentence, plea-based disposition, no published opinion identified in the primary releases), but it has substantial illustrative value as a documented end-to-end CA-PT-US cooperation chain. It is one of relatively few fully-attributed Canadian-side primary records of cybercrime cooperation in the wiki and is therefore useful for [[canada]] capacity assessment.

## Proceedings Timeline

- 2022-05: Redman accesses Twitter Partner Support Portal; sells access via online forum; co-conspirators defraud 200+ victims of approximately USD 794,000 in NFTs and cryptocurrency.
- 2022 to 2025: Canadian RCMP CIT Central Region investigation, with information sharing to FBI.
- Pre-2025: Redman departs Canada.
- 2025-03 (month-level precision per DOJ): Portuguese authorities arrest Redman in Portugal; he is extradited to the United States with substantial assistance from the DOJ Office of International Affairs. Case docketed as 1:25-cr-129 in EDVA.
- 2025-07-29: Sentencing before U.S. District Judge Leonie M. Brinkema in Alexandria, Virginia: 12 months' imprisonment plus restitution.
- 2025-07-30: DOJ U.S. Attorney's Office EDVA issues sentencing press release.
- 2025-09-10: RCMP Central Region (Ontario) issues Canadian-side press release; closes Canadian investigation.

## Cooperation Effectiveness

Roughly even chance that this case would have produced a successful prosecution without the formal extradition request, given that Redman had left Canada and was located in Portugal at the time of arrest. The combined record across the two primary releases shows highly likely effectiveness across three cooperation steps:

1. RCMP-FBI informal information sharing (highly likely effective: both agencies attribute the prosecution outcome partly to this channel).
2. Portuguese authorities' arrest of Redman on a U.S.-driven request (highly likely effective: arrest occurred and the defendant was held for extradition).
3. DOJ Office of International Affairs-led extradition from Portugal (almost certainly effective: extradition completed in March 2025, U.S. prosecution proceeded to verdict within four months).

The Canadian decision to close its parallel investigation once U.S. prosecution succeeded is consistent with the resource-economy logic of [[informal-cooperation|informal-police-cooperation]] frameworks between trusted partners.

## Korean Relevance (한국 관련성)

The case has no direct Korean-side participation. Its indirect relevance to Korean practice on cybercrime international cooperation is in the model of cooperation chain it documents: a partner-state arrest, followed by extradition to the prosecuting forum, with the originating-jurisdiction investigation closed once the prosecuting forum delivers a conviction. This pattern is structurally similar to the disposition path Korea has used in some recent transnational voice-phishing repatriations (see [[korea-cambodia-scam-repatriation]], [[korea-china-voice-phishing-qingdao]]) but with a key difference: those Korean cases ended in repatriation of suspects to Korea for prosecution, whereas Redman ended in extradition away from Canada to the United States. Both models accept the practical primacy of the prosecuting forum where the bulk of victims reside.

## Contradictions & Open Questions

- Victim-loss figure: DOJ EDVA states USD 794,000 in profit; the RCMP press release describes "several million dollars" in victim losses. The wiki treats both as accurate and likely complementary (gross loss vs. realized profit), but the discrepancy is flagged for resolution if a court filing or sentencing memorandum becomes available.
- Restitution amount: Neither primary release specifies the restitution amount or the mechanism for asset recovery from Redman or co-conspirators.
- Co-conspirators: Both releases reference "co-conspirators" but neither identifies them by name or jurisdiction. It is unknown whether any co-conspirators have been separately indicted or are still under investigation.
- Portuguese arresting unit: Neither release specifies whether the arrest in Portugal was made by the Polícia Judiciária, the Polícia de Segurança Pública, or another body. The Polícia Judiciária is the most likely agency given the cybercrime nature of the underlying offense, but this remains unverified in the primary record.
- Existing wiki entry mismatch: A pre-existing thin wiki page at `wiki/cases/canadian-cybercriminal-sentenced-to-a-year-in-prison-for-nft-theft-scheme.md` records the defendant's name as "Conor Brian Fitzpatrick." That name corresponds to a different cybercrime defendant (BreachForums founder), referenced only in the "Related Content" sidebar of the DOJ EDVA press release, not in the body. The mistake is recorded as an open question for separate cleanup; this page does not modify the existing entry per the scope of this ingest.
