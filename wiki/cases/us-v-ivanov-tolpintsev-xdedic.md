## Summary

United States v. Ivanov-Tolpintsev is an xDedic-related prosecution focused on large-scale credential decryption and resale. It is closely related to the supplier-side logic of the Pankov case but with a separate extradition path and sentencing record.

## Facts

DOJ said Ivanov-Tolpintsev decrypted the credentials of thousands of compromised computers and sold them on a dark-web marketplace connected to xDedic. The government treated the conduct as trafficking in unauthorized access devices and computer passwords.

The case helps fill out the xDedic supply side by showing that multiple technical actors fed stolen access into the marketplace ecosystem.

## International Cooperation Elements

### Evidence Gathering

The case rests on the same broader xDedic evidentiary environment involving Belgium, Ukraine, Europol, and related foreign partners, even though the most visible custody event occurred in Poland.

### Arrest and Extradition

Ivanov-Tolpintsev was taken into custody by Polish authorities in October 2020 and extradited to the United States in 2021. DOJ later reported that he pleaded guilty and was sentenced to four years in prison.

### Asset Recovery

The court entered a forfeiture order in the amount of USD 82,648, which DOJ identified as proceeds of the charged conduct.

## Legal Analysis

### Jurisdiction

The Middle District of Florida had jurisdiction because the marketplace sold unauthorized access devices affecting U.S. victims and infrastructure, including Florida-linked systems.

### Key Legal Issues

- **Credential-trafficking liability:** The case demonstrates that decryption and sale of compromised credentials can be charged independently inside a larger access-market ecosystem.
- **Third-country extradition:** It also reinforces the recurring pattern of securing custody through cooperative transit or third-party states.

### Precedent Value

The case has *medium to high* value because it broadens the xDedic case map beyond admins and downstream buyers to repeatable supplier-side prosecutions.

## Cooperation Effectiveness

The case suggests that xDedic enforcement was not one-dimensional. Authorities reached foreign credential suppliers and converted a multinational investigation into custody, forfeiture, and prison time.

## Korean Relevance

No South Korean role is identified. The case remains relevant because credential theft and resale markets frequently underpin later intrusions against organizations far outside the original compromise jurisdiction.
