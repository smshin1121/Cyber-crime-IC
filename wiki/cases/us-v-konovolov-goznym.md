## Summary

United States v. Konovolov et al. is the prosecution of 10 members of the GozNym cybercriminal network, which used GozNym malware to steal online banking credentials from over 41,000 victim computers and attempt approximately USD 100 million in theft. The case is *almost certainly* unprecedented in its use of simultaneous parallel prosecutions: while the US indicted 10 defendants in the Western District of Pennsylvania, Bulgaria, Georgia, Moldova, and Ukraine conducted parallel domestic prosecutions of their own nationals.

## Facts

The GozNym network exemplified "cybercrime as a service," assembling specialists recruited from underground criminal forums. Each member played a specialized role: malware development, credential theft, money mule management, or "cashing out" stolen funds. The network primarily targeted US businesses and financial institutions.

## International Cooperation Elements

### Evidence Gathering

This was described by DOJ as an "unprecedented" international law enforcement operation involving six countries. Europol coordinated the multinational investigation and Eurojust facilitated judicial cooperation.

### Parallel Prosecutions

Rather than extraditing all defendants to the US, participating countries conducted their own prosecutions:
- **Georgia**: Prosecuted Konovolov domestically
- **Bulgaria**: Prosecuted several defendants while extraditing Nikolov to the US
- **Moldova and Ukraine**: Conducted domestic proceedings against their nationals

### Extradition

Krasimir Nikolov was extradited from Bulgaria to the US to face charges.

## Legal Analysis

### Precedent Value

**High.** The case established a model for coordinated multinational cybercrime prosecutions where each country prosecutes its own nationals in parallel, avoiding the delays and complications of mass extradition. This approach *likely* reflects the growing maturity of international cybercrime cooperation frameworks.

## Cooperation Effectiveness

The GozNym case *almost certainly* represents a high-water mark for multinational cybercrime prosecution cooperation, with six countries agreeing to a coordinated approach that respected each nation's sovereignty while ensuring comprehensive enforcement.
