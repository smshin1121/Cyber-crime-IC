## Summary

On **2024-12-12**, the U.S. Department of Justice (Office of Public Affairs) announced the unsealing of a **14-defendant indictment** returned the previous day (**2024-12-11**) by a federal grand jury in the **U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Missouri, Eastern Division (St. Louis)**, charging fourteen Democratic People's Republic of Korea (DPRK) nationals with a multi-year fraudulent IT-worker conspiracy that generated **at least USD 88 million** in illicit revenue for the DPRK regime over approximately six years (April 2017 – March 2023).

The defendants are alleged to have operated under two DPRK-controlled front companies — **Yanbian Silverstar Network Technology Co., Ltd.** (Yanji, Jilin Province, People's Republic of China) and **Volasys Silver Star** (Vladivostok, Russian Federation) — and to have used stolen, borrowed, and purchased identities of hundreds of U.S. persons (alongside AI-generated credentials and front company structures) to fraudulently obtain remote IT employment with U.S. and other businesses. Some defendants additionally **extorted** their U.S. employers post-termination by threatening to release stolen sensitive data.

All 14 defendants remain **fugitives** and are listed on the **FBI Wanted ("DPRK IT WORKERS")** notice. Concurrent with the unsealing, the U.S. Department of State's **Rewards for Justice** program (administered by the **Diplomatic Security Service**) announced a reward of **up to USD 5 million** for information on the defendants and on Yanbian Silverstar / Volasys Silver Star.

## Facts

The Government's allegations as reflected in cross-corroborated tier-1/2 secondary sources covering the unsealing:

- **Conduct period**: approximately April 2017 through approximately March 2023 (approximately six years).
- **Front companies**: Yanbian Silverstar Network Technology Co., Ltd. (Yanji, Jilin Province, PRC); Volasys Silver Star (Vladivostok, Russia). Both were DPRK-controlled.
- **Modus operandi**: defendants used stolen, borrowed, and purchased U.S. (and other) identities to conceal their North Korean nationality and obtain remote IT employment with U.S. and other companies worldwide. AI-generated credentials and front companies were also used.
- **Financial scale**: ≥ USD 88 million generated over six years for the DPRK regime. ~130 DPRK IT workers participated overall (per U.S. State Department Rewards for Justice page).
- **Extortion add-on**: after some workers were terminated, they extorted the affected U.S. employers by threatening to release stolen sensitive employer data unless paid additional sums.
- **Charges (all 14 defendants)**: conspiracy to violate the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA); conspiracy to commit wire fraud; conspiracy to commit money laundering; conspiracy to commit identity theft.
- **Aggravated identity theft**: additionally charged against **eight** of the fourteen defendants.
- **Concurrent seizures**: USD 2.26 million from associated bank accounts; USD 764,800 unsealed concurrently per CyberScoop reporting; **29 internet domains** seized in two waves (17 in October 2023, 12 in May 2024).

## International Cooperation Elements

### Evidence Gathering

No formal international cooperation under MLAT or the Budapest Convention is enumerated in the cited DOJ release. The defendants are all DPRK nationals operating from DPRK-controlled premises located in the territory of the People's Republic of China (Yanbian Silverstar — Yanji, Jilin Province) and the Russian Federation (Volasys Silver Star — Vladivostok). Neither China nor Russia is a party to the Budapest Convention; both have demonstrated an unwillingness to cooperate with U.S. cybercrime evidence-gathering requests against DPRK actors operating from their territory. (Confidence: highly likely, based on absence in this and adjacent DPRK-IT-worker enforcement releases.)

The investigation accordingly relied on:
- U.S.-domestic financial and identity-theft tracing in the victim companies' jurisdictions.
- Domain seizures via U.S. court-authorized warrants directed at U.S.-resolvable infrastructure.
- Identity-theft pattern analysis from victim companies and their U.S.-person identity owners.

### Arrest and Extradition

**No defendant was arrested.** All 14 are at large and almost certainly inside DPRK or operating from DPRK-controlled facilities in PRC (Jilin) or Russia (Vladivostok). Neither China nor Russia has an active extradition treaty with the United States that would cover these defendants; the DPRK has none. The case is therefore an **extradition-unfeasible prosecution** — a charging instrument intended to (a) signal sanctions, (b) disrupt the schemes through asset and domain seizures, and (c) anchor the State Department's Rewards for Justice reward offers.

### Asset Recovery

- **USD 2.26 million** in bank-account seizures.
- **USD 764,800** in concurrent court-authorized seizure orders unsealed on 2024-12-12 (per CyberScoop).
- **29 internet domains** seized across October 2023 (17 domains) and May 2024 (12 domains) actions preceding the December 2024 indictment unsealing.

## Legal Analysis

### Jurisdiction

U.S. jurisdiction rests on:
1. **U.S.-victim nexus** — defendants targeted U.S. employers and stole the identities of U.S. persons, providing wire-fraud and identity-theft jurisdiction.
2. **U.S. financial system nexus** — laundering proceeds through U.S. and U.S.-correspondent banking grounds money-laundering jurisdiction.
3. **IEEPA and DPRK sanctions framework** — IEEPA jurisdiction extends to U.S. persons and to U.S.-nexused transactions, and DPRK-sanctions evasion is criminalized under 31 C.F.R. parts 510 and related implementing regulations of the Office of Foreign Assets Control. (See [[extraterritorial-jurisdiction]] for the broader doctrinal framework.)

### Key Legal Issues

- **[[extraterritorial-jurisdiction|Extraterritorial jurisdiction]]** — defendants are DPRK nationals operating from PRC and Russia targeting U.S. victims. The jurisdictional foundation is the U.S.-victim and U.S.-financial-system nexus, not defendant nationality or location.
- **Effective extraterritorial enforcement absent extradition** — the case illustrates that U.S. cybercrime prosecution doctrine has, in practice, evolved to a **disrupt-and-signal** model when defendants are sheltered in non-cooperating jurisdictions: indictment + asset seizures + Rewards for Justice + concurrent State / Treasury actions, rather than arrest-and-extradite.
- **AI-generated credentials and identity layering** — the indictment is among the earliest U.S. federal cybercrime indictments to allege use of AI-generated credentials in identity-theft conspiracies.

### Precedent Value

This is the largest single-indictment naming of DPRK IT-worker principals in the U.S. DPRK-cybercrime enforcement architecture as of the unsealing date. It establishes the IEEPA + wire-fraud + money-laundering + identity-theft (+ aggravated identity theft for a subset) charging template for state-sponsored fraudulent-IT-worker conspiracies. As a chronological precursor, it sets the prosecution template that the [[us-doj-coordinated-nationwide-actions-dprk-it-workers-2025|2025-06-30 DOJ Coordinated Nationwide Actions]] would later extend to U.S.-domestic enablers.

## Cooperation Effectiveness

The case is **a U.S. unilateral enforcement action with no demonstrated formal IC cooperation from the territorially-implicated jurisdictions** (PRC, Russia). It is included in the wiki for the following reasons:

1. It establishes the precursor charging template for DPRK fraudulent-IT-worker conspiracies later extended to U.S.-domestic facilitators.
2. It illustrates the **disrupt-and-signal** model of cybercrime prosecution where defendants are sheltered in non-cooperating jurisdictions — a recurrent pattern across the DPRK, Russia-criminal-actor, and certain Chinese-actor cybercrime enforcement architectures.
3. It demonstrates the multi-agency US-side architecture (DOJ NSD + USAO E.D. Mo. + FBI St. Louis + State Department / Diplomatic Security Service / Rewards for Justice) that operates as a substitute for absent bilateral cooperation.

The Republic of Korea (South Korea) was not enumerated in this release as an operational cooperation partner. (See Korean Relevance below.)

## Korean Relevance (한국 관련성)

This case is directly relevant to Republic of Korea (ROK) cybercrime international cooperation interests for the following reasons:

1. **DPRK-source defendants**. All 14 defendants are nationals of the Democratic People's Republic of Korea (조선민주주의인민공화국) — an entity of central and continuing security concern to ROK. The scheme generated illicit revenue for the DPRK regime, including for entities and programs subject to UN Security Council sanctions, with which the ROK is in compliance.
2. **DPRK IT-worker schemes are an explicit ROK-US joint advisory subject** — the **October 2023 joint U.S.–Republic of Korea guidance** on DPRK IT-worker schemes (referenced in the [[us-doj-coordinated-nationwide-actions-dprk-it-workers-2025|2025-06-30 DOJ Coordinated Nationwide Actions]] press release) is the operative bilateral framework. ROK government agencies including [[national-intelligence-service-korea|NIS]], [[ministry-of-foreign-affairs-korea|MOFA]], and [[ministry-of-justice-korea|MOJ]] have engaged in DPRK-IT-worker-fraud advisory and investigative cooperation with U.S. counterparts under that and related frameworks.
3. **No operational ROK involvement is enumerated in this 2024-12-12 release.** The cited DOJ press release does not identify any ROK agency, court, or central authority as a cooperating entity. This is consistent with the case being an extradition-unfeasible US-victim-nexused prosecution; it does not exclude ROK operational cooperation in adjacent investigations or in the broader DPRK-IT-worker enforcement architecture as documented elsewhere in the wiki.
4. **The Hangul transliterations of the 14 defendant names** (정성화, 리경식, 김류성, 림은철, 김무림, 조충범, 현철성, 손은철, 석광혁, 최정용, 고충석, 김예원, 정경철, 장철명) reflect the DPRK's North Korean orthography; the corresponding South Korean orthographic conventions would slightly differ (e.g., 리경식 → 이경식; 림은철 → 임은철). This is a small but durable Korean-perspective annotation: the DPRK-form Hangul is what appears in original DPRK-government documents and in the FBI Wanted listing's romanizations.
