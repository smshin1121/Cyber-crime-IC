## Summary

United States v. Alexandre Cazes is the core U.S. criminal case behind the AlphaBay component of Operation Bayonet. The case is *highly likely* one of the most consequential darknet-marketplace prosecutions ever initiated, even though it never reached adjudication because Cazes died in Thai custody before extradition.

## Facts

DOJ stated that Cazes created and administered AlphaBay, a marketplace used to sell drugs, fraudulent identification documents, access devices, malware, hacking tools, firearms, and toxic chemicals. By July 2017, AlphaBay had hundreds of thousands of users and vendors and had facilitated very large cryptocurrency transaction volumes.

The indictment filed on 1 June 2017 charged Cazes with racketeering conspiracy, narcotics conspiracy and substantive narcotics counts, identity-theft conspiracy, access-device-fraud conspiracy, device-making-equipment trafficking, and money-laundering conspiracy. A civil forfeiture complaint followed on 19 July 2017 against assets in several foreign jurisdictions.

## International Cooperation Elements

### Evidence Gathering

The public press release attributes substantial assistance to authorities in Thailand, the Netherlands, Lithuania, Canada, the United Kingdom, France, and Europol. That indicates a broad evidence and operational support network rather than a purely bilateral U.S.-Thailand arrest process.

### Arrest and Extradition

Thai authorities arrested Cazes in Bangkok on 5 July 2017 on behalf of the United States. Extradition did not proceed because he died in custody on 12 July 2017.

### Asset Recovery

The United States targeted millions of dollars in cryptocurrency and high-value real estate. DOJ said the related civil forfeiture action covered assets in Thailand, Cyprus, Liechtenstein, and Antigua and Barbuda.

## Legal Analysis

### Jurisdiction

U.S. jurisdiction was *likely* grounded in the marketplace's use by U.S. narcotics traffickers, fraud actors, and access-device criminals, as well as undercover investigative activity and infrastructure access connected to the Eastern District of California.

### Key Legal Issues

- **Extradition interruption:** The defendant's death prevented testing of the U.S.-Thailand extradition path in court.
- **Global forfeiture enforcement:** The case demonstrates how a criminal indictment can be paired with cross-border asset preservation and forfeiture activity.

### Precedent Value

The case has *high* practical precedent value because it shows that a darknet-market administrator can be identified, indicted, arrested abroad, and stripped of assets through a coordinated multinational process.

## Cooperation Effectiveness

Even though the criminal prosecution was cut short, the cooperation model was *likely* still highly effective: AlphaBay was shut down, core infrastructure was seized, and substantial assets were frozen or seized across several jurisdictions.

## Korean Relevance (한국 관련성)

South Korea did not participate directly. The case is nonetheless *likely* relevant to Korean practice because it shows how darknet-market enforcement in Asia can hinge on arrests and asset actions in third countries such as Thailand.

## Contradictions & Open Questions

1. The exact volume of assets successfully forfeited, as distinct from initially frozen or seized, is not fully specified in the collected source.
2. The public record does not fully spell out how evidence was divided between U.S. and European partners.
3. Because the indictment was dismissed after Cazes' death, the charging theories were never litigated on the merits.
