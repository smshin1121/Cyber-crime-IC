## Summary

**United States v. Kevin James Strutz** is a domestic U.S. cyberstalking prosecution in the Eastern District of California, not a cybercrime international-cooperation operation. Kevin James Strutz, of Ceres, California, pleaded guilty on **2023-05-22** to two counts of cyberstalking and was sentenced on **2023-11-27** to **three years and four months in federal prison**, followed by **three years of supervised release**, plus **USD 1,773 restitution**.

The case involved two adult female victims. DOJ stated that Strutz secretly recorded an Airbnb guest at the Ceres residence where he lived in February 2020, then used multiple Facebook accounts and a non-consensual image to coerce the victim. DOJ also stated that investigators discovered earlier conduct against a second victim between September and December 2018, including electronic messages, calls, and 15 handwritten letters placed on the victim's vehicle.

## Legal and Procedural Posture

The collected DOJ sources identify the charged offense as **cyberstalking** and state that Strutz pleaded guilty to two counts before sentencing. The plea-stage release described the statutory exposure as up to five years in prison and a USD 250,000 fine for each count, subject to judicial discretion and the federal sentencing guidelines. The sentencing-stage release records the final disposition: 40 months in prison, three years of supervised release, and USD 1,773 restitution.

No collected source provides the docket number, plea agreement, sentencing memorandum, or judgment. Those filings would be needed to verify guideline calculations, offense-level adjustments, supervised-release conditions, and any restitution allocation by victim. The public press releases are sufficient for the case chronology and outcome, but not for a complete procedural record.

## Evidence and Platform Use

The case turned on communications and digital evidence rather than infrastructure seizure. DOJ described repeated contact through multiple Facebook accounts, use of a non-consensual image in a coercive message, calls and messages to a second victim, and a series of handwritten letters. The local press sources corroborate the indictment and sentencing posture, while the DOJ releases provide the primary legal account.

From an IC taxonomy perspective, the platform element matters only as electronic evidence. The current source set does not show that investigators used an MLAT, direct foreign provider request, emergency disclosure request, or cross-border preservation mechanism. Facebook is a U.S.-based platform, and the visible investigative path is domestic FBI plus Ceres Police assistance.
