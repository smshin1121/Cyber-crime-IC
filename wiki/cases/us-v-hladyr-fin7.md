## Summary

United States v. Fedir Hladyr is the principal U.S. criminal case publicly tied to the FIN7 prosecution track. DOJ announced in April 2021 that Hladyr, described as a systems administrator for FIN7, was sentenced to 10 years' imprisonment for his role in a long-running intrusion and payment-card theft conspiracy.

## Facts

DOJ stated that FIN7 targeted more than 100 U.S. companies, especially in the restaurant, gaming, and hospitality sectors, and stole millions of payment-card records. Hladyr functioned as a systems administrator for the group, managing servers and infrastructure that supported malware deployment and data exfiltration.

The sentencing release followed Hladyr's earlier arrest in Germany and extradition to the United States. The case therefore connects the public FIN7 disruption story to a named defendant, foreign arrest, and final sentence.

## International Cooperation Elements

### Evidence Gathering

The broader FIN7 investigation almost certainly required evidence from multiple jurisdictions because the group's members, infrastructure, and victims were spread across several countries. Public reporting around Hladyr's case specifically points to German arrest cooperation and likely parallel evidence work touching Eastern Europe.

### Arrest and Extradition

Hladyr was arrested in Dresden, Germany, in January 2018 and extradited to the United States. That extradition step is one of the clearest foreign-custody components publicly associated with the FIN7 investigation.

### Asset Recovery

The sentencing release summarized imprisonment rather than forfeiture. Public materials tied to the case do not clearly describe a major Hladyr-specific asset recovery component.

## Legal Analysis

### Jurisdiction

U.S. jurisdiction was straightforward because the charged conduct targeted U.S. companies and payment systems and was prosecuted in a federal district that handled victim-facing effects of the conspiracy.

### Key Legal Issues

- **Transnational actor attribution:** The case shows how a distributed cybercrime group can still be converted into an individual prosecution when a specific infrastructure operator is identified.
- **Extradition dependency:** Public courtroom outcomes for groups like FIN7 depend heavily on whether defendants travel into cooperative jurisdictions.

### Precedent Value

The case has *high* practical value because it demonstrates that a global carding and intrusion conspiracy can produce an ordinary district-court sentence rather than remaining only an intelligence or disruption narrative.

## Cooperation Effectiveness

The case suggests that FIN7 enforcement was effective when technical investigation, foreign arrest authority, and U.S. prosecution were aligned. Without the German arrest, the public judicial component would likely have remained much thinner.
