## Summary

United States v. Nazarovi et al. is the principal U.S. criminal case tied to the [[qqaazz-money-laundering-takedown|QQAAZZ money laundering network takedown]]. It focuses not on a single malware family, but on a laundering service that allegedly supported multiple major cybercrime groups.

## Facts

DOJ alleged that QQAAZZ opened and maintained hundreds of bank accounts around the world, used shell companies and false identity documents, and laundered tens of millions of dollars stolen from victims of computer fraud, wire fraud, and bank fraud. The network allegedly retained fees of 40 to 50 percent before returning the balance to cybercriminal clients.

The public DOJ summary linked QQAAZZ to cybercrime ecosystems involving Dridex, Trickbot, and GozNym. That makes the case especially useful for understanding how financial-service providers enable multiple malware crews at once.

## International Cooperation Elements

### Evidence Gathering

DOJ described a years-long investigation involving 16 countries and parallel investigative work across Europe. The Office of International Affairs reportedly coordinated requests for searches, arrests, extraditions, and evidence sharing, making this a textbook multinational laundering case.

### Arrest and Extradition

The press release emphasized parallel prosecutions in the United States, Portugal, Spain, and the United Kingdom rather than a single extradition funnel. It also reported more than 40 house searches in Latvia, Bulgaria, the United Kingdom, Spain, and Italy.

### Asset Recovery

DOJ specifically noted the seizure of a bitcoin-mining operation in Bulgaria associated with the group. The operation therefore combined criminal charges with infrastructure and financial disruption.

## Legal Analysis

### Jurisdiction

The Western District of Pennsylvania had a clear U.S. nexus because QQAAZZ allegedly laundered proceeds from cybercrime affecting U.S. victims and U.S. banks, including PNC Bank in Pittsburgh.

### Key Legal Issues

- **Cybercrime-enabler liability:** The case targets laundering specialists who did not necessarily conduct the initial hacks but allegedly made large-scale theft economically workable.
- **Parallel prosecution model:** It shows that money-laundering networks can be fragmented across jurisdictions and still be prosecuted through coordinated domestic cases instead of relying entirely on extradition.

### Precedent Value

The case has *high* value because it maps the financial-service layer of international cybercrime and shows how anti-money-laundering prosecution can cut across multiple malware families and victim sets.

## Cooperation Effectiveness

The case suggests that the operation was effective because it did not just seize assets or publish intelligence. It generated charges across several countries, disrupted bank-account infrastructure, and exposed the service layer connecting separate cybercrime groups.

## Korean Relevance

No South Korean role is identified in the collected source. The case remains relevant because Korean investigators confronting ransomware, banking-trojan, or BEC losses often face the same downstream laundering bottleneck that QQAAZZ allegedly served.

## Contradictions & Open Questions

1. The collected source does not provide final dispositions for the 20 charged QQAAZZ defendants.
2. The exact boundary between U.S. prosecution and the European parallel cases is not fully detailed in public summaries.
3. Public records do not fully quantify how much of the laundered proceeds were ultimately seized rather than only traced.
