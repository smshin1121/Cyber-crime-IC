## Summary

On January 23, 2025 the U.S. Department of Justice announced the indictment in the Southern District of Florida of five defendants — North Korean nationals Jin Sung-Il (진성일) and Pak Jin-Song (박진성), Mexican national Pedro Ernesto Alonso De Los Reyes, and U.S. nationals Erick Ntekereze Prince and Emanuel Ashtor — in connection with a multi-year scheme to obtain remote information-technology employment with U.S. companies on behalf of the DPRK. The release states the scheme ran from approximately April 2018 through August 2024, that the defendants and unindicted co-conspirators obtained work from at least sixty-four U.S. companies, that payments from ten of those companies generated at least USD 866,255, and that most of the proceeds were laundered through a Chinese bank account. Likely [confidence: likely], this case is best read as one of the public-facing prosecutions sitting under the broader DPRK RevGen: Domestic Enabler Initiative documented at [[us-doj-coordinated-nationwide-actions-dprk-it-workers-2025]].

## Facts

According to the DOJ Office of Public Affairs release, the defendants used forged and stolen identity documents — including a U.S. passport bearing the stolen personally identifiable information of a U.S. person — to conceal the identities of Jin, Pak, and other North Korean co-conspirators so that DPRK nationals could circumvent sanctions and other laws and obtain employment with U.S. companies. Ntekereze and Ashtor received laptops from U.S. employer companies at their U.S. residences, downloaded and installed remote-access software on them without authorization, and thereby allowed the DPRK IT workers to log into employer-supplied computers from abroad while appearing to operate from inside the United States. Ashtor had previously operated a "laptop farm" out of his North Carolina residence, which the FBI searched at the time of arrest. The defendants are alleged to have laundered scheme proceeds through a variety of accounts, with most funds passing through a Chinese bank account.

> [!note] DPRK IT-worker context
> The release re-states the standing tri-seal advisory framework (FBI/State/Treasury, May 2022; updated U.S.–ROK guidance October 2023; FBI updated guidance May 2024) that DPRK has dispatched thousands of skilled IT workers, primarily living in China and Russia, generating individual annual revenue up to USD 300,000 and aggregate revenue in the hundreds of millions on behalf of designated entities such as the DPRK Ministry of Defense.

## Legal Analysis

### Jurisdiction

Jurisdiction lies in the Southern District of Florida; the FBI Miami Field Office is investigating, and Assistant U.S. Attorneys for SDFL are prosecuting alongside a National Security Division Trial Attorney. Personal jurisdiction over the DPRK defendants is exercised through the U.S.-effects branch of long-arm jurisdiction principles — i.e. the alleged victimization of U.S. companies and the use of U.S.-based laptop farms to make the defendants appear domestic.

### Key Legal Issues

The indictment, per the release, charges all five defendants with: (1) conspiracy to cause damage to a protected computer (a Computer Fraud and Abuse Act-style theory), (2) conspiracy to commit wire fraud and mail fraud, (3) conspiracy to commit money laundering, and (4) conspiracy to transfer false identification documents. Jin and Pak alone face an additional conspiracy to violate the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA), reflecting the sanctioned-state-actor character of the DPRK principals. The maximum penalty stated in the release is twenty years' imprisonment.

### Precedent Value

This case is likely [confidence: likely] to be cited alongside the broader [[us-doj-coordinated-nationwide-actions-dprk-it-workers-2025|DOJ DPRK RevGen nationwide enforcement waves]] as a template for charging architecture: pairing U.S.-national domestic enablers (laptop farm operators) with DPRK principals on a single indictment, layering an IEEPA conspiracy onto a CFAA-and-fraud core, and using a third-country (here, the Netherlands) to capture an in-transit non-DPRK co-conspirator. The release explicitly situates the announcement in a series of similar actions in October 2023, May 2024, August 2024, and December 2024.

The case has indirect but meaningful Korean relevance. Second, the release explicitly cites the October 2023 joint U.S.–Republic of Korea (ROK) updated guidance on DPRK IT-worker fraud as part of the public-warning framework that this prosecution operationalizes — placing ROK government IT-worker risk advisories squarely in the policy backdrop of the indictment, even though no ROK agency is named as an investigative partner in this specific case.
