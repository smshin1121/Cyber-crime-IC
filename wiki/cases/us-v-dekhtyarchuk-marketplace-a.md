## Summary

United States v. Igor Dekhtyarchuk is the March 2022 Eastern District of Texas indictment targeting the alleged administrator of "Marketplace A," a cybercriminal marketplace that sold stolen login credentials, personally identifiable information, and authentication tools. The case is *likely* one of the clearer U.S. examples of a cross-border prosecution focused on a single marketplace operator rather than a sprawling conspiracy.

## Facts

According to the indictment summary released by DOJ, Dekhtyarchuk operated Marketplace A beginning in 2018 after first advertising stolen-account data on Russian-language forums. Marketplace A allegedly offered more than 48,000 compromised email accounts, more than 39,000 compromised online accounts, and attracted roughly 5,000 daily visitors.

The FBI used an online covert employee in the Eastern District of Texas to make 13 undercover purchases between March and July 2021. Those purchases yielded access devices for 131 victim accounts, which *likely* helped anchor venue and prove the marketplace's commercial functionality.

## International Cooperation Elements

### Evidence Gathering

DOJ stated that the investigation involved the FBI Legal Attaché office in Riga and the State Police of Latvia. The exact evidence-sharing pathway is not public, but Latvian assistance *likely* mattered because the defendant was a Russian national operating from abroad and the investigation targeted an international marketplace ecosystem.

### Arrest and Extradition

No public arrest or extradition event is described in the DOJ release. Dekhtyarchuk instead appears to have been charged while abroad and placed on the FBI Cyber Most Wanted list.

### Asset Recovery

No public forfeiture or asset-freezing component has been identified from the collected source.

## Legal Analysis

### Jurisdiction

U.S. jurisdiction was *likely* grounded in the FBI's undercover purchases from the Eastern District of Texas, the use of victim access devices tied to U.S. accounts, and the impact on U.S. victims and platforms.

### Key Legal Issues

- **Cross-border attribution:** The case shows how prosecutors can attribute operation of an overseas marketplace through undercover purchases, forum activity, and messaging-platform delivery.
- **Data-sovereignty questions:** Because the defendant and at least some infrastructure were outside the United States, the investigation *likely* required careful handling of foreign-held evidence and liaison support.

### Precedent Value

The case has *moderate* precedent value because it demonstrates a practical route for charging a single marketplace administrator whose conduct is dispersed across Russia-based forums, messaging services, and victim accounts in the United States.

## Cooperation Effectiveness

The public record suggests the cooperation model was effective enough to support a formal indictment despite the defendant's overseas location. That is *likely* a meaningful outcome even without an arrest, because it converts intelligence on a criminal marketplace into a prosecutable U.S. case and creates a public wanted target for future partner action.

## Korean Relevance (한국 관련성)

South Korea was not directly involved. The case is nonetheless *likely* relevant to Korean practice because credential-theft marketplaces routinely victimize global e-commerce and payment ecosystems that include Korean users and institutions.
