## Summary

The Second Additional Protocol to the Budapest Convention (CETS No. 224) is a **modernization instrument** designed to address the critical bottleneck of cross-border evidence access in cybercrime investigations. Adopted on 2022-05-12 by the Council of Europe Committee of Ministers, the Protocol introduces direct cooperation mechanisms with service providers, expedited emergency disclosure, joint investigation team provisions, and video conferencing for evidence — all aimed at reducing reliance on the slow traditional [[mlat-process|MLAT process]].

The Protocol is not yet in force as of April 2026; it requires **5 ratifications, including at least 3 Council of Europe member states**, to enter into force.

## Historical Context

- **2013-2014:** Cloud evidence problem identified by the T-CY (Cybercrime Convention Committee) as the single greatest challenge to Budapest implementation
- **2015-2017:** T-CY cloud evidence reports; decision to negotiate a new protocol rather than amend the Convention
- **2017-2021:** Protocol Drafting Group (PDG) negotiations, with significant debate over data protection safeguards
- **2019-2020:** Parallel CLOUD Act developments in the US influenced the negotiation dynamic
- **2021-11-17:** T-CY approved the final draft
- **2022-05-12:** Committee of Ministers adopted the Protocol
- **2022-05-12 onward:** Opened for signature by Budapest Convention parties

The Protocol was developed in direct response to the "loss of location" problem: digital evidence increasingly stored in foreign or unknown jurisdictions, making traditional territorial-based MLA impractical.

## Key Provisions for International Cooperation

### Direct Cooperation with Service Providers (Art. 6-7)

The Protocol's most innovative provisions allow law enforcement to **directly contact service providers** in another party's territory to request subscriber information (Art. 6) or to give effect to domestic production orders (Art. 7). This bypasses the slow MLAT channel for basic data categories.

**Art. 6 — Subscriber information:**
- Requesting authority contacts the service provider directly
- Provider discloses subscriber info (name, address, contact details, IP allocation) under domestic law authority
- No prior government-to-government channel required
- Subject to data protection conditions (Art. 14)

**Art. 7 — Giving effect to orders:**
- A party can issue a domestic order and request a service provider in another party's territory to comply
- The service provider's home state can object under specified grounds
- More intrusive than Art. 6; involves a notification mechanism

### Emergency Disclosure (Art. 8)

In cases of **imminent risk of danger to life or physical integrity**, authorities may request expedited disclosure of subscriber information and traffic data from another party's authorities. The requested party must respond within 24 hours where possible. This provision fills a critical gap: emergencies that cannot wait for MLAT but require more than subscriber info.

### Joint Investigation Teams for Cyber Cases (Art. 9-12)

The Protocol provides an explicit legal basis for JITs between Budapest parties, including provisions for:
- Formation and participation rules
- Evidence sharing within JITs
- Role of Europol and Eurojust (for EU members)
- Temporary transfer of personnel

### Video Conferencing (Art. 13)

Enables **testimony and hearing of witnesses via video conferencing** across borders, reducing the need for physical travel and accelerating proceedings.

### Data Protection Safeguards (Art. 14)

Establishes conditions and safeguards for personal data processed under the Protocol:
- Purpose limitation
- Data quality and accuracy
- Data security
- Record keeping
- Restrictions on onward transfer
- Effective oversight mechanisms

This was the **most contentious provision** during negotiations, with the EU insisting on GDPR-compatible safeguards while some non-EU parties sought more flexible terms.

## Parties and Participation

As of April 2026, the Protocol has been signed by a number of Budapest Convention parties but has not yet reached the 5-ratification threshold (including 3 CoE members) for entry into force.

## Implementation in Practice

The Protocol has not yet entered into force. However, its provisions are already influencing:
1. **Service provider cooperation practices:** Major tech companies are adjusting their law enforcement response processes in anticipation
2. **Domestic legislation:** Several states are aligning their domestic frameworks with Protocol requirements
3. **CLOUD Act executive agreements:** The Protocol and the [[cloud-act]] framework address overlapping problems, and states are evaluating which path to pursue

## Relationship to Other Frameworks

### Budapest Convention

The Protocol supplements (does not replace) the [[budapest-convention]]. All Budapest parties may sign and ratify the Protocol. The Protocol's provisions operate alongside the Convention's existing IC chapter (Art. 23-35).

### CLOUD Act

The Protocol and the [[cloud-act]] represent **parallel approaches** to the same problem — cross-border access to electronic evidence held by service providers. Key differences:

| Feature | Second Additional Protocol | CLOUD Act |
|---------|---------------------------|-----------|
| Legal nature | Multilateral protocol | US domestic law + bilateral executive agreements |
| Scope | All Budapest parties (70+) | US + executive agreement partners |
| Direct provider access | Yes (Art. 6-7) | Yes (under executive agreement) |
| Data protection | Art. 14 conditions | Executive agreement terms |
| Emergency provisions | Art. 8 (24-hour response) | Emergency exception in 18 U.S.C. 2702 |

### UN Cybercrime Convention

The Protocol was partly motivated by urgency to modernize Budapest before the [[un-cybercrime-convention-2024]] was finalized, ensuring the Budapest framework remained competitive and relevant.

## Criticisms and Debates

1. **Privacy concerns:** Direct law enforcement access to service providers raises concerns about bypassing judicial oversight in the requested state
2. **Power asymmetry:** Service providers are overwhelmingly headquartered in the US and a few EU states, meaning the Protocol primarily benefits states *requesting from* those jurisdictions
3. **Complexity:** States must navigate Protocol provisions alongside existing MLAT obligations, CLOUD Act agreements, and (potentially) the UN Convention
4. **Slow ratification:** As of April 2026, ratification pace has been slower than hoped, delaying practical impact

## Korean Perspective (한국 관점)

South Korea, as a 2024 accession party to the [[budapest-convention]], is **eligible to sign and ratify** the Second Additional Protocol. Korean authorities are *likely* evaluating the Protocol in the context of:

- **Access to US-held data:** The Protocol's direct provider access provisions (Art. 6-7) would be particularly valuable for Korean law enforcement seeking data from US-based tech companies (Google, Meta, Apple, Microsoft)
- **CLOUD Act alternative:** Korea does not yet have a [[cloud-act]] executive agreement with the US; the Protocol may offer a complementary path
- **Data protection alignment:** Korea's Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA) would need to be assessed for compatibility with Art. 14 safeguards
- **Domestic legislative review:** The Ministry of Justice (법무부) is *likely* reviewing whether domestic legislation needs amendment for Protocol implementation

## Contradictions & Open Questions

1. **Ratification pace:** Will the Protocol reach the 5-ratification threshold soon? Slow ratification undermines its relevance as the UN Convention advances.
2. **CLOUD Act interaction:** How will states that are both Protocol parties and CLOUD Act agreement partners handle overlapping requests? No coordination mechanism is defined.
3. **Service provider compliance:** Will service providers consistently comply with Art. 6-7 requests from all parties, or will de facto compliance disparities emerge?
4. **Korean signature timeline:** When will South Korea sign the Protocol? No public timeline has been announced.
5. **EU adequacy:** Will the EU consider the Protocol's data protection safeguards (Art. 14) sufficient for GDPR purposes? This affects EU member state ratification.
