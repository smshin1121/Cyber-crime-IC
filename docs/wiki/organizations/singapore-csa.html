개요Summary

The Cyber Security Agency of Singapore (CSA) is Singapore's lead national cybersecurity agency. Established on 1 April 2015, CSA is responsible for protecting Singapore's cyberspace, operating SingCERT, regulating Critical Information Infrastructure (CII) under the Cybersecurity Act 2018, licensing cybersecurity service providers, and coordinating Singapore's response to major cyber incidents.

CSA is distinct from the Singapore Police Force Cybercrime Command: CSA handles defensive cybersecurity, CII regulation, and incident response, while SPF Cybercrime Command handles criminal investigation and prosecution. The two agencies coordinate closely on incidents that have both incident-response and criminal dimensions.

Current Status (as of 2026-04-10)

Present-day name: Cyber Security Agency of Singapore (CSA) Commissioner and Chief Executive: David Koh (Commissioner of Cyber Security) [1][4] Parent: Ministry of Digital Development and Information (MDDI) — operationally managed by MDDI, with statutory authority under the Prime Minister's Office [2][3] Status: Active

CSA remains Singapore's lead national cybersecurity agency as of April 2026. Notable recent developments:

  • July 2025 UNC3886 incident response: CSA mobilised over 100 cyber defenders in response to cyberattacks on Singapore's critical infrastructure attributed to UNC3886, a cyber-espionage group. The incident affected Singtel, StarHub, M1 and Simba networks and was described by Minister K Shanmugam as Singapore's largest coordinated cyber incident response to date [4]. CSA's Commissioner David Koh characterized cyber stability as "a necessity, not a luxury" following the incident [4].
  • Organizational placement: CSA is administratively a government agency under the Prime Minister's Office, but is managed by the Ministry of Digital Development and Information (MDDI), which took over the management role from the former Ministry of Communications and Information (MCI) when MDDI was created in July 2024 [2][3].
  • Continuity of leadership: David Koh continues to serve as Commissioner of Cyber Security and Chief Executive [1][4].

임무와 권한Mandate and Authority

CSA's mandate and statutory powers include:

  • Cybersecurity Act 2018: CSA is the regulator of Critical Information Infrastructure (CII) sectors (energy, water, banking, healthcare, transport, infocomm, media, security/emergency services, government, aviation, maritime).
  • CII owner obligations: Licensed CII owners must report cybersecurity incidents to CSA, conduct audits, and comply with CSA-issued codes of practice.
  • Cybersecurity service provider licensing: CSA licenses penetration testing and managed security operations centre (SOC) providers.
  • National incident response: CSA operates SingCERT and coordinates national response to major cyber incidents.
  • International cooperation: CSA represents Singapore in international cybersecurity fora and engages in capacity building under ASEAN frameworks.

국제공조 역량IC Capabilities

Because CSA is a defensive/regulatory agency, its international cooperation profile differs from law-enforcement agencies:

  • CERT-to-CERT cooperation: SingCERT cooperates with other national CERTs through FIRST (Forum of Incident Response and Security Teams) and APCERT.
  • ASEAN Cyber Capacity Programme (ASCCP): CSA operates the ASEAN Singapore Cybersecurity Centre of Excellence (ASCCE), launched 2019, to build ASEAN member state cyber capacity.
  • Bilateral MOUs: CSA maintains bilateral cybersecurity MOUs with a number of countries.
  • Liaison with law enforcement: For incidents with criminal elements, CSA refers cases to SPF Cybercrime Command. For transnational incidents with law-enforcement dimensions, coordination runs through SPF rather than directly CSA-to-foreign-LE.

Relationship to Cybercrime International Cooperation

CSA's relevance to cybercrime international cooperation (the focus of this wiki) is primarily indirect:

  • CSA is almost certainly the first Singaporean touchpoint for major APT and ransomware incidents hitting Singaporean CII.
  • For the criminal investigation and cross-border evidence/arrest dimensions of such incidents, lead responsibility transfers to SPF Cybercrime Command.
  • CSA provides technical incident attribution, forensic artifact preservation, and capacity-building that supports law-enforcement cooperation indirectly.

모순점 및 미해결 문제Contradictions & Open Questions

  • The exact division of labour between CSA and SPF Cybercrime Command on incidents with both national-security and criminal dimensions requires clearer public documentation.
  • CSA's role in mutual legal assistance (MLA) channels — whether it is a central authority or a technical contributor — is not clearly documented in public sources.
  • The operational impact of the July 2025 UNC3886 incident on Singapore's cybersecurity regulatory posture has not yet been fully reported in public sources as of April 2026.
