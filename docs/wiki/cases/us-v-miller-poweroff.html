United States v. Jeremiah Sam Evans Miller (Operation PowerOFF)

: 2026-04-17 : 1

개요Summary

United States v. Jeremiah Sam Evans Miller is the Central District of California prosecution of a RoyalStresser.com operator whose case formed part of Operation PowerOFF. The case is likely one of the most concrete publicly traceable U.S. prosecutorial outcomes tied to that broader multinational booter-service campaign.

Facts

DOJ stated that Miller operated RoyalStresser.com, formerly known as Supremesecurityteam.com, as a DDoS-for-hire service. He pleaded guilty in April 2023 and was sentenced on 15 July 2024 to nine months in federal prison.

The DOJ sentencing release connects Miller's case directly to the December 2022 enforcement wave in which authorities seized 48 booter domains and charged six defendants. That linkage makes the case a useful judicial anchor for an operation that is otherwise often described through infrastructure seizures and coordination announcements.

International Cooperation Elements

Evidence Gathering

The sentencing release does not enumerate specific foreign evidence used against Miller. Even so, because DOJ expressly framed the case as part of Operation PowerOFF, evidence or operational intelligence from UK and Dutch partners was likely part of the broader investigative environment that enabled the U.S. charges.

Arrest and Extradition

No foreign arrest or extradition component has been identified for Miller individually.

Asset Recovery

The sentencing release notes that a DOJ forfeiture specialist was handling seizure of the Astrostress domain in a related case, but it does not describe a separate Miller-specific forfeiture order.

Legal Analysis

Jurisdiction

The case used ordinary U.S. criminal jurisdiction because Miller was a domestic defendant running a booter service that attacked protected computers in the United States and abroad. What makes the case notable is not the jurisdictional theory itself, but its embedding within a multinational disruption campaign.

Key Legal Issues

  • Operation-to-case conversion: The case shows how a multinational disruption campaign can produce ordinary district-court prosecutions against identifiable administrators.
  • Service reconstitution risk: Operation PowerOFF repeatedly confronted booter reappearance under new domains, so the deterrent value of prosecutions like Miller's is likely greater when paired with recurring seizures.

Precedent Value

The case has moderate value as a model for linking international disruption activity to a concrete sentencing outcome that can be cited on a case page rather than only on an operation page.

Cooperation Effectiveness

The case suggests that Operation PowerOFF was not merely a domain-seizure exercise. It likely translated international coordination into at least some individualized criminal accountability, which strengthens the operation's deterrence narrative.

Korean Relevance (한국 관련성)

South Korea was not directly involved in Miller's case. The case is still potentially relevant because Korean agencies confronting DDoS-for-hire services face the same problem of turning technical takedowns into sustained prosecutorial outcomes.

모순점 및 미해결 문제Contradictions & Open Questions

  1. The public case materials do not specify the precise foreign-partner evidence tied to Miller personally.
  2. The public record remains clearer on Miller than on some other PowerOFF defendants, creating an uneven case-level picture of the broader operation.
  3. The exact deterrent impact of individual sentencings within PowerOFF remains unmeasured.
