United States v. Odufuye and Nwoke (BEC)

: 2026-04-17 : 1

개요Summary

United States v. Odufuye and Nwoke is a District of Connecticut BEC case that provides a concrete judicial counterpart to Operation WireWire. The case matters because it moved beyond arrest headlines into extradition, guilty pleas, sentencing, and restitution.

Facts

DOJ described a spoofing-based business email compromise scheme that targeted controllers, CFOs, CEOs, nonprofits, and schools across the United States. The conspirators allegedly used deceptive executive-style emails and, in some instances, malware-bearing attachments to induce victims to send funds to accounts controlled by the scheme.

The public Connecticut materials describe one Torrington victim company that sent five wire transfers totaling more than USD 500,000 to accounts in Virginia, Florida, Washington, D.C., and Hong Kong. DOJ also identified dozens of wire confirmations linked to the conspirators' accounts and millions of dollars in intended losses.

International Cooperation Elements

Evidence Gathering

The case had a clear cross-border evidence picture: the scheme used overseas actors, funds moved through foreign jurisdictions including Hong Kong, and DOJ specifically thanked the Office of International Affairs and foreign police partners for assistance.

Arrest and Extradition

Odufuye was arrested in the United Kingdom in December 2016 and extradited to the United States. Nwoke was arrested in Mauritius on 8 May 2018 and extradited on 25 May 2018, which DOJ described in broader Operation WireWire materials as the first Mauritius extradition in more than 15 years.

Asset Recovery

The collected case materials emphasize restitution rather than a large forfeiture action. Nwoke agreed to restitution of USD 662,053.87, and Odufuye was ordered to pay USD 921,497.87.

Legal Analysis

Jurisdiction

The District of Connecticut had a direct territorial and victim nexus because the spoofing campaign targeted Connecticut businesses and caused actual losses there, even though important actors and transfers were outside the United States.

Key Legal Issues

  • Extradition-backed BEC enforcement: The case shows that high-value spoofing schemes can support actual custody over foreign defendants rather than only fugitive indictments.
  • Hybrid email-fraud and malware conduct: DOJ's references to malware-bearing attachments indicate that some BEC cases sit at the boundary between pure social-engineering fraud and more technical intrusion conduct.

Precedent Value

The case has high practical value because it demonstrates a full enforcement arc for international BEC: indictment, foreign arrest, extradition, guilty plea, sentencing, and restitution.

Cooperation Effectiveness

This case suggests that Operation WireWire was not just a sweep with diffuse outcomes. At least in this Connecticut matter, the operation translated into custody, convictions, restitution, and a durable evidentiary record.

Korean Relevance

No South Korean involvement is identified in the collected source. The case is still relevant because export-oriented companies in Korea face the same spoofed-invoice and executive-impersonation risks that made this prosecution viable.

모순점 및 미해결 문제Contradictions & Open Questions

  1. The public summaries do not identify every foreign bank-account jurisdiction used in the laundering chain.
  2. The second defendant named in the original 2016 indictment is not fully described in the earliest public release, while later case materials focus on Odufuye, Nwoke, and Adejumo.
  3. The public record does not fully separate attempted loss from final realized loss across all victims.
