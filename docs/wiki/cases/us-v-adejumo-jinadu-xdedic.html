United States v. Adejumo and Jinadu (xDedic Tax-Fraud Proceeds)

: 2026-04-17 : 1

개요Summary

United States v. Adejumo and Jinadu is an xDedic-related tax-fraud proceeds case in which U.S.-based defendants handled money generated from stolen access and PII. It complements the Levinson case by showing a different downstream fraud architecture within the same ecosystem.

Facts

DOJ said international conspirators obtained unauthorized access to U.S. business servers, stole U.S. residents' identifying information, and used the data to file false tax forms seeking millions in refunds. Adejumo and Jinadu handled the proceeds through prepaid debit cards and bank accounts under their control.

The case helps show that xDedic-driven fraud was operationally distributed: foreign access acquisition, identity theft, domestic proceeds handling, and pending extradition for additional conspirators.

International Cooperation Elements

Evidence Gathering

The public releases credit DOJ's Office of International Affairs, IRS-CI and FBI international operations in Mission UK, and U.K. authorities. That points to coordinated evidence and custody work rather than a purely domestic tax-fraud case.

Arrest and Extradition

Adejumo and Jinadu were arrested in the United States, but Odedeyi was arrested in London and remained pending extradition according to DOJ. This created a mixed domestic-and-extradition enforcement pattern.

Asset Recovery

The collected sentencing release focuses on imprisonment rather than forfeiture, but the case centered on millions of dollars in attempted fraudulent refunds.

Legal Analysis

Jurisdiction

The Middle District of Florida had jurisdiction because the fraud targeted the U.S. Treasury and involved U.S.-based accounts, U.S.-resident identities, and conduct occurring partly inside the United States.

Key Legal Issues

  • Split-role conspiracy design: The case illustrates how cyber-enabled tax fraud can be organized across access thieves, identity-data users, and domestic cash-out handlers.
  • International-to-domestic proceeds flow: It also shows how prosecutors connect foreign credential theft to domestic financial movement and restitution narratives.

Precedent Value

The case has medium to high value because it clarifies the downstream financial mechanics of xDedic-enabled identity and tax fraud.

Cooperation Effectiveness

The case suggests that xDedic enforcement reached money-handling layers inside the United States while also maintaining pressure on foreign conspirators through extradition proceedings.

Korean Relevance

No South Korean role is identified. The case remains relevant because stolen enterprise access often leads to second-order financial fraud far beyond the initial intrusion.
