United States v. Blake Benthall (Silk Road 2.0)

: 2026-04-17 : 1

개요Summary

United States v. Blake Benthall is the Southern District of New York criminal case tied to the Silk Road 2.0 segment of Operation Onymous. It is the most concrete U.S. prosecutorial record identified so far for that broader takedown and therefore serves as the operation's primary case anchor.

Facts

According to DOJ, Benthall operated Silk Road 2.0 under the alias "Defcon" after the original Silk Road was shut down in October 2013. The site allegedly enabled large-scale drug sales and other illicit transactions over the Tor network and, by September 2014, was generating at least USD 8 million per month with about 150,000 active users.

DOJ said Benthall oversaw the marketplace's infrastructure, code, commissions, administrative staff, and profits. The public source also states that an HSI undercover agent infiltrated the support staff and obtained direct access to private administrative areas used by Benthall.

International Cooperation Elements

Evidence Gathering

The SDNY press release expressly credited foreign authorities in France, Germany, Lithuania, the Netherlands, and the United Kingdom, as well as DOJ's Office of International Affairs. That makes this more than a domestic undercover case: cross-border infrastructure tracing and evidence support were clearly part of the investigative model.

Arrest and Extradition

Benthall was arrested in San Francisco, so the case did not require extradition. The international component instead appears in evidence development and synchronized site-seizure activity rather than in custodial transfer.

Asset Recovery

DOJ announced the arrest in parallel with a forfeiture action targeting dozens of dark-market sites. Even where the public record is operation-centric, that linkage matters because it shows the case was paired with direct infrastructure and asset-denial measures.

Legal Analysis

Jurisdiction

The Southern District of New York asserted jurisdiction because Silk Road 2.0 enabled criminal trade affecting U.S. victims and U.S. criminal law interests, and because the marketplace was investigated through U.S.-based undercover activity and supporting digital evidence.

Key Legal Issues

  • Dark-market operator attribution: The case shows how prosecutors combined undercover infiltration with technical and cross-border evidence to attribute operation of a Tor-hidden market to a specific individual.
  • Operation-to-case conversion: It also illustrates the conversion of a large international seizure operation into a named U.S. criminal complaint rather than leaving the action at the infrastructure-disruption level.

Precedent Value

The case has high practical value because it provides a prosecution-centered lens on Operation Onymous, which is often described only as a multinational takedown. It is also useful for studying the legal boundary between undercover access, hidden-service attribution, and multinational evidence support.

Cooperation Effectiveness

The case suggests that Operation Onymous was operationally effective not only because it seized hidden services, but because it also produced at least one clear U.S. operator prosecution. That materially strengthens the operation's evidentiary and deterrence value.

Korean Relevance

No South Korean participation is publicly identified in this case. It is still relevant because it shows how darknet-market investigations can combine undercover access, foreign-partner assistance, and synchronized infrastructure action to produce prosecutable operator attribution.

모순점 및 미해결 문제Contradictions & Open Questions

  1. The final disposition of Benthall's case is not established in the collected source and needs separate court-record confirmation.
  2. The exact technical method used to locate the Silk Road 2.0 infrastructure remains controversial in public reporting.
  3. The foreign partners' specific evidentiary contributions are acknowledged but not individually detailed in the SDNY release.
