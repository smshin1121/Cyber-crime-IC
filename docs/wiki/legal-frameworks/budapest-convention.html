Budapest Convention on Cybercrime

legal-framework 법적 프레임워크 최종 수정: 2026-04-08

Summary

The Budapest Convention on Cybercrime is the first and most significant binding international treaty dedicated to combating cybercrime through harmonized criminal law, common investigative powers, and international cooperation mechanisms. Adopted by the Council of Europe in 2001 and in force since 2004, it has grown to approximately 70 states parties, including major non-European states such as the United States, Canada, Japan, Australia, and — since 2024 — South Korea.

The Convention is highly likely the single most important legal instrument for international cooperation on cybercrime. It provides the legal basis for expedited data preservation, mutual legal assistance tailored to digital evidence, and the 24/7 Network for urgent cross-border contacts.

Historical Context

The Convention was drafted by the Council of Europe's Committee of Experts on Crime in Cyberspace (PC-CY) from 1997 to 2001, with active participation from non-member observer states (US, Canada, Japan, South Africa). It was opened for signature on November 23, 2001 at the International Conference on Cybercrime in Budapest.

Key milestones: - 2001-11-23: Opened for signature - 2004-07-01: Entry into force (5 ratifications including 3 CoE members) - 2003-01-28: Additional Protocol on xenophobia/racism through computer systems (CETS No. 189) - 2022-05-12: Second Additional Protocol (CETS No. 224) adopted - 2024: South Korea accedes, becoming the first major East Asian party

Key Provisions for International Cooperation

Chapter III: International Cooperation (Art. 23-35)

Art. 23 — General principles: Parties shall cooperate "to the widest extent possible" for investigations and proceedings concerning criminal offenses related to computer systems and data.

Art. 29 — Expedited preservation of stored computer data: A party may request another to order the expedited preservation of data stored by means of a computer system in its territory, pending a formal MLA request. Preservation lasts up to 60 days, extendable. This is critical because it prevents data deletion during the typically slow MLAT process.

📝 Dual Criminality Under Art. 29(3), a party may require dual-criminality as a condition for preservation, but ONLY if it declared this reservation upon signature/ratification.

Art. 31 — Mutual assistance regarding accessing stored computer data: One party can request another to search, access, seize, and disclose stored data in its territory. This supplements traditional MLA with procedures specifically designed for digital evidence.

Art. 32 — Trans-border access to stored computer data with consent or publicly available: Permits unilateral access WITHOUT authorization from the other party when: - (a) the data is publicly available, or - (b) the person lawfully authorized to disclose the data consents.

This is the most controversial provision — critics argue it permits extraterritorial evidence access that infringes sovereignty. Proponents argue it reflects practical reality and is limited in scope.

Art. 35 — 24/7 Network: Each party designates a point of contact available 24/7 to provide immediate assistance including: technical advice, preservation of data, collection of evidence, and provision of legal information. See 24-7-network for details.

Parties and Participation

As of 2024, 76 states parties (per C-PROC data) with accessions continuing steadily into 2026. The Convention is notable for its openness to non-Council of Europe members.

Major parties include: All 46 Council of Europe members, plus the United States, Canada, Japan, Australia, Israel, Argentina, Chile, Colombia, Costa Rica, Dominican Republic, Ghana, Morocco, Mauritius, Panama, Peru, Philippines, Senegal, South Korea, Sri Lanka, Tonga, and others.

Notable non-parties and their stated reasons:

Russia Opposes on sovereignty grounds; argues Convention was drafted without its input; objects to Art. 32 trans-border access; championed UN Convention as alternative
China Sovereignty concerns similar to Russia; prefers UN-led framework; does not accept CoE oversight

Implementation in Practice

The Convention's practical impact is significant:

  1. Law harmonization: Parties have enacted compatible cybercrime legislation, reducing dual-criminality barriers.
  2. Expedited preservation (Art. 29): Used thousands of times annually. Enables evidence preservation within hours, bridging the gap before formal MLA.
  3. 24/7 Network: Operational in 70+ countries. First responders for urgent cross-border cases.
  4. Capacity building: The Council of Europe's c-proc office in Bucharest runs extensive capacity building programs (GLACY+, iPROCEEDS, CyberEast) to help countries implement the Convention.

Known challenges: - Art. 32's scope remains disputed, limiting its use in practice - Some parties have not fully implemented all provisions domestically - The 24/7 Network's effectiveness varies by country

Relationship to Other Frameworks

second-additional-protocol (CETS No. 224): Modernizes the Convention with direct provider cooperation, emergency MLA, JITs, and videoconferencing provisions. Adopted 2022; entry into force pending sufficient ratifications (5 required, including 3 CoE members). As of early 2025, 40+ signatures but single-digit ratifications.

un-cybercrime-convention-2024: Adopted August 8, 2024 by the UN General Assembly. Broader membership potential but reflects different geopolitical priorities. The two instruments are formally complementary but represent competing visions: Budapest (Western-led, existing cooperation framework) vs. UN (developing-world participation, sovereignty emphasis). It is likely that both will coexist, with Budapest remaining the operational gold standard for IC while the UN Convention expands participation.

Criticisms and Debates

  1. Art. 32 sovereignty concerns: Cross-border access without host state authorization, even with consent, is seen by some as violating traditional sovereignty in criminal matters.
  2. Human rights safeguards: Critics argue the Convention lacks sufficient data protection and human rights safeguards, particularly regarding surveillance powers.
  3. Developed-world bias: The Convention was drafted primarily by Western states. Developing countries had limited input, contributing to the push for the UN Convention.
  4. Slow MLAT channel: Despite improvements, the core MLAT pathway remains slow for the pace of cybercrime.

Korean Perspective (한국 관점)

South Korea acceded to the Budapest Convention in 2024, becoming the first major East Asian economy to do so. Korea had held observer status at the Cybercrime Convention Committee (T-CY) for several years prior.

Significance for Korea: - Access to the 24/7 Network for expedited cross-border requests - Enhanced legal basis for cooperation with European and North American partners - Signal of Korea's commitment to international cybercrime cooperation standards - Basis for capacity building partnerships

Domestic implementation: Korea's existing legislation (정보통신망법, 형법 cyber provisions) already covered most substantive offenses. Procedural adjustments for Art. 29 preservation and Art. 35 designation were the main implementation requirements.
