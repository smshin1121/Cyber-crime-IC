Source Summary

Anadolu Ajansı wire of 2026-03-31 (timestamp 2026-03-31T20:45:37) reproducing a statement by T.C. İçişleri Bakanlığı (Ministry of Interior of Türkiye) issued via the Bakanlık's NSosyal social-media account. The Ministry announces a Hakkari-centred, four-month investigation that culminated in simultaneous operations across 18 provinces of Türkiye, with 52 suspects detained domestically and 1 additional Red-Notice fugitive — identified as part of the criminal organisation's technical infrastructure — apprehended in Sarajevo, Bosnia and Herzegovina, in coordination with the Interpol Daire Başkanlığı (Interpol Department of the Turkish General Directorate of Security).

The Ministry characterises the seized network as the largest cyber-crime / fraud-related transaction-volume case ever identified in Türkiye, with approximately 20 billion Turkish lira in money traffic associated with illegal betting platforms, qualified fraud (nitelikli dolandırıcılık) committed via information-technology systems, and laundering of criminal proceeds — including layering through third-party accounts and conversion to crypto-assets. The press release explicitly states that "the international structure of the criminal organisation was also disclosed; the illegal activities were determined to extend to server and management layers located in Bosnia and Herzegovina."

Relevance to IC

  • Tier-1 primary source for a Türkiye → Bosnia and Herzegovina cybercrime extradition / Red-Notice cooperation pattern that is otherwise not covered in the wiki.
  • Confirms named cooperation channel: Interpol Daire Başkanlığı (Turkish national Interpol Bureau within the General Directorate of Security) operating an Interpol Red Notice that produced an apprehension in Sarajevo.
  • Establishes a Türkiye-side case-architecture in which a provincial prosecutor's office (Yüksekova Cumhuriyet Başsavcılığı, in Hakkari) and a provincial police directorate (Hakkari Emniyet Müdürlüğü) anchor an operation whose international layer reaches into BiH-hosted servers — useful comparative datapoint for "provincial-led, Interpol-extended" Turkish cyber investigations.
  • Identifies a recurring laundering typology — "panel system + third-party accounts + crypto conversion" — directly attributed to the targeted organisation by an official source.
  • Identifies BiH not only as the apprehension venue but also as the server-hosting jurisdiction of the criminal infrastructure, which is the kind of fact pattern most often required for cross-border evidence preservation requests but rarely confirmed by a sending-state primary source.

Limitations and gaps

  • Original statement does not name the suspect publicly; subsequent Turkish outlets (Hakkari local press, May 2026) refer to the apprehended fugitive only by initials "M.O.B." Identification beyond initials is not in the AA wire.
  • The press release does not name the BiH counterpart agency that executed the Sarajevo arrest (Federalna policija, MUP RS, SIPA, or local cantonal police are all candidates); it credits the apprehension to the Red Notice and to "simultaneous operation" without naming a BiH executing authority.
  • Mutual legal assistance treaty basis is not specified in the wire. Türkiye and Bosnia and Herzegovina are both parties to the Council of Europe extradition framework and to the Budapest Convention on Cybercrime, but the AA wire does not invoke specific instruments.
  • Defendant-level case numbers, indictment text, and downstream extradition timeline are not in this single source.
  • The 20 billion TL figure is presented as "money traffic" / illegal financial ecosystem layering, not as net victim loss; the wire does not enumerate victim count or victim losses.
