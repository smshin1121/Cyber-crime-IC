주요 발견사항

  • Tycoon 2FA phishing-as-a-service (PhaaS) platform — active since at least August 2023 — disrupted by an Europol EC3-coordinated public-private action announced 2026-03-04.
  • 330 domains forming the core infrastructure (phishing pages and control panels) were taken down. Technical disruption was led by Microsoft with a coalition of private partners; seizures and operational measures were carried out by law enforcement in 6 countries: Latvia, Lithuania, Portugal, Poland, Spain, and the United Kingdom.
  • Platform scale at peak: tens of millions of phishing emails per month; unauthorised access facilitated to nearly 100 000 organisations globally including schools, hospitals, and public institutions.
  • By mid-2025 Tycoon 2FA accounted for roughly 62% of all phishing attempts blocked by Microsoft.
  • Investigation triggered by intelligence shared by Trend Micro; disseminated by Europol through EC3 Advisory Groups; orchestrated through Europol's Cyber Intelligence Extension Programme (CIEP) — a first-of-its-kind Europol programme bringing private-sector experts to The Hague to work side-by-side with EC3 analysts and investigators on specific projects.
  • Law enforcement authorities named: Latvia State Police; Lithuania Criminal Police Bureau; Portugal Judicial Police; Poland Central Cybercrime Bureau (Centralne Biuro Zwalczania Cyberprzestępczości / CBZC); Spain National Police and Guardia Civil; UK National Crime Agency.
  • Private partners named: Cloudflare, Coinbase, Intel471, Microsoft, Proofpoint, Shadowserver Foundation, SpyCloud, Trend Micro.
  • Europol's role: central hub between private partners and investigators; intelligence sharing with affected countries; translation into coordinated operational action.

Source Summary

Europol newsroom press release dated 2026-03-04 announcing the coordinated public-private disruption of Tycoon 2FA, one of the largest phishing-as-a-service (PhaaS) platforms worldwide. The disruption took down 330 domains forming the platform's core infrastructure (phishing pages + control panels) and was executed by law enforcement in six countries — Latvia, Lithuania, Portugal, Poland, Spain, and the United Kingdom — coordinated by Europol's European Cybercrime Centre (EC3). The technical takedown was led by Microsoft with a coalition of private partners. Investigation triggered by intelligence-sharing from Trend Micro through Europol's Cyber Intelligence Extension Programme (CIEP), a first-of-its-kind Europol framework that hosts private-sector experts at The Hague to work alongside EC3 analysts on specific operational projects.

IC Pattern

This release establishes several discrete IC mechanism classes worth recording:

  • Cyber Intelligence Extension Programme (CIEP) — first Europol-described public-private operational framework that brings industry experts physically to The Hague to work alongside EC3 analysts and investigators on specific projects. Distinct from the EC3 Advisory Groups (information-sharing forums) and from one-off mobile-office deployments. The Tycoon 2FA case is described as the first publicised CIEP-driven operational outcome.
  • Microsoft-led technical takedown + LE-led seizure split — a recurring pattern in PhaaS/malware-as-a-service disruptions where a private vendor with platform-level visibility (Microsoft, Cloudflare) executes the technical disruption (domain takedowns, control-panel seizures, abuse-channel processing) in parallel with national LE seizing physical / hosted infrastructure and pursuing operator identification. Compare with LabHost (April 2024) (Europol J-CAT 19-country PhaaS takedown) and the Lumma Stealer takedown (May 2025) (Microsoft-led with US DOJ + Europol).
  • EC3 Advisory Groups → CIEP escalation — the release frames Trend Micro's intelligence-sharing as flowing first through EC3 Advisory Groups (steady-state info-sharing), then escalating into CIEP (operational embedding) once the strategy required deeper public-private collaboration. This is a documented two-tier public-private mechanism class.
