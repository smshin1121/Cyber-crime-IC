주요 발견사항

  • On 1 July 2025, OFAC, in coordination with the United Kingdom's National Crime Agency (NCA), designated Aeza Group — a St. Petersburg, Russia bulletproof hosting (BPH) provider — for supporting ransomware operators, infostealer operators, and a Russian darknet drug marketplace targeting victims in the United States and worldwide.
  • OFAC additionally designated three Aeza-affiliated companies — Aeza International Ltd. (UK branch), Aeza Logistic LLC, and Cloud Solutions LLC (both 100%-owned Russia-based subsidiaries) — and four Aeza Group leaders: Arsenii Aleksandrovich Penzev (CEO, 33% owner), Yurii Meruzhanovich Bozoyan (general director, 33% owner), Vladimir Vyacheslavovich Gast (technical director), and Igor Anatolyevich Knyazev (33% owner).
  • Treasury named Meduza and Lumma infostealer operators (targeting the U.S. defense industrial base and technology companies), BianLian ransomware, RedLine infostealer panel hosting, and BlackSprut darknet drug marketplace as the principal cybercriminal customers of Aeza's BPH infrastructure.
  • All designations were made pursuant to E.O. 13694, as further amended by E.O. 14144 and E.O. 14306. The release explicitly frames the action as a follow-on to OFAC's February 2025 designation of ZServers BPH and as part of a sustained Treasury campaign against the Russian-aligned BPH ecosystem.
  • Penzev and Bozoyan were arrested by Russian law enforcement in connection with placing the BlackSprut illicit-drug marketplace onto Aeza Group infrastructure — an unusual on-the-record acknowledgment by Treasury of domestic Russian enforcement against the same individuals being designated under U.S. sanctions authority.

주요 발견사항Key Findings

  1. Bilateral US-UK coordination on a Russian BPH provider (high confidence). The action is described on the record as conducted "in coordination with" the United Kingdom's National Crime Agency (NCA), with OFAC designating an Aeza Group front company in the UK (Aeza International Ltd.). This is a notable deployment of U.S. cyber-sanctions authority against a third-country shell entity, with on-the-record UK law-enforcement coordination.
  2. Bulletproof hosting as cybercrime enabler (high confidence). Treasury characterizes Aeza Group as having provided BPH services to a specific named portfolio of cybercriminal customers — the Meduza and Lumma infostealer operators (targeting the U.S. defense industrial base and technology companies), the BianLian ransomware operation, RedLine infostealer panels, and the BlackSprut darknet drug marketplace.
  3. Statutory basis (confirmed). All designations cite E.O. 13694, as further amended by E.O. 14144 and E.O. 14306 — the U.S. cyber-sanctions authority chain. The Aeza Group designation invokes the misappropriation-for-commercial-advantage prong; the affiliates' designations invoke the owned-or-controlled-by-blocked-person prong; the four individuals' designations invoke the leader/officer prong.
  4. Continuity with February 2025 ZServers action (confirmed). The release explicitly states that the Aeza action "builds on OFAC's February action targeting ZServers BPH," establishing a documented Treasury campaign against the Russian-aligned BPH ecosystem in 2025.
  5. Russian domestic enforcement parallel (high confidence). Treasury notes on the record that two of the designated individuals (Penzev and Bozoyan) had been arrested by Russian law enforcement for placing the BlackSprut illicit-drug marketplace onto Aeza infrastructure. This is an unusual pattern in which U.S. sanctions, UK NCA coordination, and host-state criminal enforcement converge on the same individuals.
  6. Sanctions-evasion follow-on (cross-source, confirmed). The 19 November 2025 OFAC action (Treasury SB-0319) subsequently designated Hypercore Ltd. (UK), Smart Digital Ideas DOO (Serbia), and Datavice MCHJ (Uzbekistan), explicitly framed as a response to Aeza Group's post-July 2025 rebranding and IP-relocation strategy. The July 2025 SB-0185 action is the direct predicate for that follow-on.
