주요 발견사항

  • OFAC designated Russian national Mikhail Matveev under E.O. 13694 (as amended by E.O. 13757) on 2023-05-16 for cyber-enabled activity targeting U.S. critical infrastructure.
  • Concurrently, the U.S. District Courts for the District of New Jersey and the District of Columbia unsealed criminal indictments against Matveev, and the U.S. Department of State announced an up-to-USD-10-million Transnational Organized Crime Rewards Program offer for his arrest/conviction.
  • Treasury attributes Matveev a central role in developing and deploying the Hive, LockBit, and Babuk ransomware variants, including a 2021 Babuk attack on a major U.S. city police department.
  • The release illustrates the tri-track parallel-action pattern (administrative sanctions + criminal indictment + reward offer) used by the U.S. against Russia-based ransomware actors who remain beyond extradition reach.

Key claims and findings

  1. OFAC designated Mikhail Pavlovich Matveev (Russian national) pursuant to section 1(a)(ii)(C) of Executive Order 13694, as amended by E.O. 13757, blocking all property and interests in property within U.S. jurisdiction and prohibiting U.S. persons from dealings with him. (Almost certainly accurate as a primary-source statement.)
  2. On the same day, indictments against Matveev were unsealed in the U.S. District Court for the District of New Jersey and the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia. (Confirmed by Treasury; the parallel DOJ press releases are the corroborating primary sources.)
  3. The U.S. Department of State Transnational Organized Crime Rewards Program announced an offer of up to USD 10 million for information leading to Matveev's arrest and/or conviction.
  4. Matveev is described as "a central figure in the development and deployment of the Hive, LockBit, and Babuk ransomware variants, among others."
  5. Treasury cites a 2021 Babuk affiliate attack on the police department of a major U.S. city in which personal information of officers and witnesses was stolen and posted online; Matveev publicly claimed responsibility.
  6. FinCEN analysis cited in the release: 75% of ransomware-related incidents reported July–December 2021 were linked to Russia, its proxies, or persons acting on its behalf. Hive alone had targeted more than 1,500 victims in over 80 countries.

International cooperation relevance

  • The release does not describe extradition or arrest — Matveev remains in Russia (highly likely, given the explicit reward-for-information posture and the release's framing of Russia as a "haven for ransomware actors").
  • The action illustrates a tri-track parallel pattern combining (a) OFAC sanctions, (b) DOJ criminal indictments in two federal districts, and (c) a State Department reward — a U.S.-unilateral toolkit deployed when MLA-based extradition from the home jurisdiction is unavailable.
  • The release attributes Matveev to three separately-tracked ransomware ecosystems (Hive, LockBit, Babuk), creating cross-references to multiple subsequent international takedowns that did secure cooperation from non-Russia jurisdictions.
