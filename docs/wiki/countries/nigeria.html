Federal Republic of Nigeria

country 국가 최종 수정: 2026-04-08

Summary

Nigeria occupies a unique position in the international cybercrime cooperation landscape — it is both a major source country for Business Email Compromise (BEC), advance-fee fraud, and romance scams, and an increasingly active cooperation partner through the Economic and Financial Crimes Commission (EFCC). INTERPOL operations targeting Nigerian cybercrime networks — including Operation Falcon, Operation Jackal, and Operation Serengeti — have demonstrated growing Nigerian willingness and capacity to cooperate.

Nigeria is not a party to the budapest-convention, which limits the formal legal channels available for cooperation. The Cybercrimes Act 2015 (amended 2024) provides the primary domestic legal framework. The Black Axe and related confraternity-linked cybercrime networks remain a major transnational threat originating from Nigeria.

Legal Framework for Cybercrime

Substantive Criminal Law

Cybercrimes (Prohibition, Prevention, Etc.) Act 2015 (amended 2024): - § 6: Unauthorized access to computer systems - § 7: System interference - § 8: Interception of electronic messages/communications - § 9: Theft of electronic devices - § 11: Computer-related forgery - § 12: Computer-related fraud - § 14: Identity theft and impersonation - § 15: Child pornography - § 17: Cybersquatting - § 18: Cyberstalking - § 27: Attempts, conspiracy, and abetting - § 33: Advance fee fraud committed online

Advance Fee Fraud and Other Fraud Related Offences Act 2006: - Targets traditional "419" fraud and online variants

Money Laundering (Prohibition) Act 2011: - Relevant to BEC and cybercrime proceeds laundering

Economic and Financial Crimes Commission Act 2004: - Establishes EFCC with mandate to investigate financial crimes including cybercrime

Procedural Powers

  • Search and seizure: Cybercrimes Act § 38; requires court order for computer evidence
  • Preservation orders: Cybercrimes Act § 38(2); service providers must preserve data upon request
  • Interception: Cybercrimes Act § 39; requires court authorization
  • Production orders: Cybercrimes Act § 38; compels service providers to produce subscriber and traffic data
  • Data retention: Cybercrimes Act § 38(5); 2-year mandatory retention for service providers

Mutual Legal Assistance

  • Central authority: Federal Ministry of Justice
  • Mutual Legal Assistance in Criminal Matters Act (2019): Provides domestic framework for incoming/outgoing MLA
  • No Budapest Convention access: Nigeria is not a party, limiting access to Convention Art. 29-35 cooperation channels
  • INTERPOL channel: Primary international cooperation channel via I-24/7 network
  • EFCC direct cooperation: EFCC has developed bilateral relationships with FBI, UK NCA, and other agencies for BEC investigations

Nigeria participates in INTERPOL operational networks but does not participate in the Budapest Convention 24-7-network.

Key Agencies

Economic and Financial Crimes Commission (EFCC)

  • Primary agency for financial cybercrime investigations
  • Mandate covers BEC, advance-fee fraud, money laundering, and cyber-enabled financial crime
  • Has developed significant capacity for BEC investigations with international partners
  • Key INTERPOL partner for Jackal operations targeting Black Axe
  • Received capacity building support from FBI, UK NCA, and INTERPOL

Nigeria Police Force — Cybercrime Unit

  • National police with cybercrime investigation capacity
  • Works alongside EFCC on major cases
  • Capacity building ongoing through INTERPOL and bilateral programs

National Information Technology Development Agency (NITDA)

  • Policy and regulatory body for IT
  • Supports cybersecurity capacity building

BEC and Financial Cybercrime

Nigeria is the almost certainly the most significant source country for BEC schemes globally. INTERPOL's Jackal operations specifically target the Black Axe confraternity and related organized crime groups:

  • bec-nigeria-2016: Early Nigeria-US BEC cooperation
  • black-axe-bec-2021: Black Axe-linked BEC takedown
  • nigerian-bec-convictions-2023: Domestic prosecutions demonstrating EFCC capacity

Challenges

  • Limited digital forensics capacity outside EFCC
  • Slow formal MLAT processing
  • Corruption risk within some law enforcement institutions
  • No Budapest Convention membership limits formal cooperation channels
  • Jurisdictional complexity (federal vs. state law enforcement)

Korean Interactions (한국과의 협력)

Korea-Nigeria cybercrime cooperation is roughly even chance to be direct and is more likely conducted through INTERPOL:

  • INTERPOL HAECHI series: Both countries participate in operation-haechi-v (2024) and operation-haechi-vi (2025) operations against cyber-enabled financial fraud
  • BEC threat to Korea: Nigerian-origin BEC schemes likely target Korean businesses, though the scale is less documented than targeting of US/European companies
  • No known bilateral cybercrime cooperation agreement between Korea and Nigeria
  • INTERPOL channel: Primary cooperation pathway via INTERPOL I-24/7 network and NCBs
