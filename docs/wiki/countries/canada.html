개요Summary

Canada signed the Budapest Convention on Cybercrime on 23 November 2001 — the day it was opened for signature — and completed ratification on 8 July 2015 after passing the Protecting Canadians from Online Crime Act (Bill C-13, 2014) which introduced the procedural tools required by Budapest Convention Articles 16-21 [1][2].

Canada's substantive cybercrime offences have been codified in the Criminal Code since the 1985 amendments, notably §§ 342.1 (unauthorized use of a computer), 342.2 (possession of device to obtain computer service), and 430(1.1) (mischief in relation to computer data) [3][4]. Canada is a Five Eyes intelligence partner and has longstanding MLAT relationships with the United States (1985 treaty) and other G7 states. The RCMP's National Cybercrime Coordination Centre (NC3) serves as the national operational hub [5].

사이버범죄 법적 체계Legal Framework for Cybercrime

실체법Substantive Law

Criminal Code provisions: - §§ 342.1 — Unauthorized use of computer (fraudulent access, interception, use of passwords); maximum 10 years indictment [3][4] - § 342.2 — Possession of device to obtain computer service/password - § 430(1.1) — Mischief in relation to computer data (data alteration, destruction, denial of access) - §§ 162.1, 163.1 — non-consensual distribution of intimate images, child sexual abuse material (added by Bill C-13)

절차적 권한Procedural Powers

Bill C-13 (Protecting Canadians from Online Crime Act, 2014) introduced: - Preservation demands (police-initiated) and preservation orders (judicial) - Production orders for transmission data, tracking data, financial data - Warrants for tracking devices and transmission data recorders

These brought Canadian procedural law into alignment with Budapest Convention Articles 16-21 and enabled 2015 ratification [2].

Mutual Legal Assistance

The International Assistance Group (IAG) of the Department of Justice Canada is the central authority for MLAT requests. Canada's primary MLAT partner is the United States (1985 treaty, supplemented). English and French are both accepted.

주요 기관Key Agencies

RCMP National Cybercrime Coordination Centre (NC3)

Established pursuant to Canada's National Cyber Security Strategy and the RCMP Cybercrime Strategy. NC3 coordinates investigations across Canadian law enforcement and international partners, focusing on "tech-as-target" crimes [5].

Canadian Centre for Cyber Security (CCCS)

Part of the Communications Security Establishment (CSE); provides technical capacity and incident response.

Provincial and Municipal Cybercrime Units

Multiple police services (Toronto, Montréal, Vancouver) maintain specialized cybercrime units.
