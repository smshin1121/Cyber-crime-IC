к°ңмҡ”Summary

Poland is a European Union member and Budapest Convention party. Its cybercrime law enforcement underwent a major institutional reform in 2022 with the creation of the Central Bureau for Combating Cybercrime (Centralne Biuro Zwalczania CyberprzestДҷpczoЕӣci, CBZC), a dedicated national cybercrime investigation service within the Polish Police.

Poland participates in EU cybercrime cooperation frameworks through Europol's EC3 and Eurojust.

мӮ¬мқҙлІ„лІ”мЈ„ лІ•м Ғ мІҙкі„Legal Framework for Cybercrime

мӢӨмІҙлІ•Substantive Law

Poland's primary cybercrime provisions are contained in the Criminal Code (Kodeks karny, 1997, amended): - Art. 267: Unauthorized access / interception - Art. 268-268a: Data interference - Art. 269-269b: System interference, computer sabotage

Poland's Criminal Code implements the substantive law requirements of the Budapest Convention following accession.

м Ҳм°Ём Ғ к¶Ңн•ңProcedural Powers

Under the Code of Criminal Procedure, Polish law enforcement has access to search and seizure, production orders, data preservation, and lawful interception. Likely compliant with Budapest Convention procedural requirements.

Mutual Legal Assistance

Central authority for MLAT requests is the Ministry of Justice (Ministerstwo SprawiedliwoЕӣci).
