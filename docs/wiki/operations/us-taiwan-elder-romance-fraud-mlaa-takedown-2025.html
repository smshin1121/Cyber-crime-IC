개요Summary

On 14 May 2025, the United States (through the American Institute in Taiwan, AIT) and Taiwan (through the Taiwan Taichung District Prosecutors Office, the National Police Agency Criminal Investigation Bureau, and the Ministry of Justice) jointly announced the dismantling of a Taiwanese-led international financial-fraud organisation that defrauded "hundreds of elderly victims across the United States" with "over US$100 million" laundered. The operation produced the first in-country proffer interview by a Taiwanese prosecutor in the United States under the bilateral Mutual Legal Assistance Agreement (MLAA, signed March 2002) — a milestone almost certainly (>95%) relevant for both jurisdictions' future MLAA practice given the 23-year interval between the agreement's signing and this on-site witness questioning.

A subset indictment by the Taichung District Prosecutors Office (TTDPO) names defendants Liao (廖, 36, alleged ring leader, arrested 2025-01-24 at Taoyuan International Airport, 10-year sentence recommended) and Huang (黃, 39, alleged collections handler, 5-year sentence recommended), plus U.S.-based co-conspirators by surname Hsiao (肖) and Sun (孫). The TTDPO subset addresses three U.S. victims and US$5.32 million; the broader organisational scope per AIT is "hundreds of elderly victims" and "over US$100 million" laundered.

배경Background

The investigation opened in May 2023 when law enforcement (the AIT release does not specify which jurisdiction's officers) identified suspicious financial documents carried by two Taiwanese travelers. Subsequent investigation revealed a sophisticated multi-layer organisation that:

  • Targeted elderly U.S. victims under romance-scam pretexts and solicited fraudulent investments;
  • Opened U.S. bank accounts using fake passports (charged under Taiwan's Passport Act in the TTDPO indictment);
  • Laundered proceeds through shell companies, international accounts, family-member accounts (Liao's mother), luxury asset purchases (Swiss watch, diamond ring, Lexus vehicle), and cryptocurrency (Bitcoin, Tether).

참여 기관Participating Parties

Taiwan side:

  • Taiwan National Police Agency (NPA) Criminal Investigation Bureau (CIB) — Taiwan's lead unit for transnational financial crime, identified by AIT as a "key partner".
  • Ministry of Justice (MOJ) — central authority for the U.S.-Taiwan MLAA.
  • Taichung District Prosecutors Office (TTDPO, 臺灣臺中地方檢察署) — investigating prosecutorial office; venue of 2025-05-14 joint press conference.
  • Administrative Enforcement Agency and the Taichung City Police Department — listed in the AIT release as joint press-conference participants (administrative enforcement role likely relates to asset freezes / civil collection of seized funds).

United States side:

  • American Institute in Taiwan (AIT) — the formal U.S. communication and protocol channel for Taiwan; issued PR-2510.
  • Homeland Security Investigations (HSI) Taipei — HSI's Taiwan attaché office.
  • HSI Los Angeles — investigative office (West Coast presence consistent with U.S. Pacific banking nodes used by the organisation).
  • HSI Nashville — investigative office where Sun was questioned by TTDPO prosecutor Chang Fu-chun under MLAA.
  • U.S. Department of State — operational backstop to AIT.
📝 No bilateral diplomatic recognition The United States and Taiwan do not maintain formal diplomatic relations. AIT and Taiwan's Taipei Economic and Cultural Representative Office (TECRO) function as the de-facto bilateral channels under the Taiwan Relations Act (1979) and successor instruments. The MLAA is a bilateral law-enforcement instrument that operates within this unofficial-relations framework.

법적 근거Legal Framework

The principal framework is the U.S.-Taiwan Mutual Legal Assistance Agreement (MLAA), signed in March 2002. The MLAA is implemented on the U.S. side under the Taiwan Relations Act and is the formal channel for evidence requests, witness interviews, and document production between the two jurisdictions in the absence of formal diplomatic recognition.

⚠️ Legal status check needed The U.S.-Taiwan MLAA is highly likely (≥80%) the appropriate primary citation, but the AIT release does not enumerate specific MLAA articles invoked. The MLAA operates alongside other bilateral arrangements; readers should confirm specific articles before relying on this page for litigation purposes.

Taiwan-side charges in the TTDPO indictment subset:

  • Organized Crime Prevention Act (組織犯罪防制條例) — aggravated fraud under organised-crime structure.
  • Money Laundering Control Act (洗錢防制法) — laundering of fraud proceeds.
  • Passport Act (護照條例) — use of fake passports to open U.S. bank accounts.
  • Criminal Code (刑法) — base fraud charges.

U.S.-side federal charges (if any) are not enumerated in the AIT release.

활용된 공조 메커니즘Cooperation Mechanisms Used

  • MLAA proffer interview — TTDPO prosecutor traveled in person to Nashville to question witness Sun. This is the case's historic feature: the first such on-site interview since 2002 (mutual-legal-assistance; cf. mlat-process for analogous formal-MLAT process).
  • HSI–CIB joint investigative working contact — HSI team visited TTDPO in March 2025; CIB investigators travelled with prosecutors in April 2025. This represents direct police-to-police working contact alongside the formal MLAA channel (informal-cooperation).
  • AIT diplomatic facilitation — AIT acts as the bilateral protocol channel given the absence of formal diplomatic recognition.
  • Asset-tracing across categories — fiat (mother's accounts, shell companies), luxury goods (watch, ring, vehicle), and cryptocurrency (BTC, USDT). Full crypto-tracing tooling and exchanges used are not disclosed in the source.

과제 및 마찰 요인Challenges and Friction Points

  • No formal U.S.-Taiwan extradition treaty. Movement of Taiwanese suspects from the U.S. to Taiwan, or vice versa, depends on the MLAA, voluntary surrender, deportation, or ad hoc arrangements. Hsiao and Sun (U.S.-based) have not been reported as transferred to Taiwan; Liao was arrested upon arrival in Taiwan, not extradited.
  • Diplomatic-recognition framing. Cooperation runs through AIT/TECRO rather than embassies, which is institutionally workable but adds a unique administrative layer relative to a fully diplomatically-normalised bilateral.
  • 23-year MLAA gap. That this is the first on-site MLAA proffer interview by a Taiwanese prosecutor since the 2002 signing suggests pre-existing institutional reluctance or lack of trigger cases — a friction point for both sides' historical MLAA practice.
  • Aggregate victim identification. AIT cites "hundreds" of elderly U.S. victims; the public TTDPO indictment subset enumerates only 3. The remaining victim cohort's notification status, restitution prospects, and the corresponding U.S. federal docket are not disclosed in the source.

교훈Lessons Learned

  1. Almost certainly (>95%) the 2025 in-country MLAA proffer establishes a procedural precedent for future U.S.-Taiwan cooperation: that on-site witness questioning by a Taiwanese prosecutor is a usable rather than theoretical channel under the 2002 MLAA.
  2. Highly likely (≥80%) that the AIT/TECRO de-facto-mission framework, combined with HSI's regional attaché network (Taipei), is sufficient for tier-1 transnational financial-crime cooperation despite absence of formal diplomatic recognition — a lesson with broader applicability for jurisdictions in similar diplomatic positions (e.g. Kosovo, certain Pacific entities).
  3. The case's organisational typology — Taiwan-resident leadership / U.S.-resident co-conspirators / elderly U.S. victims / mixed luxury-and-crypto laundering — is consistent with broader pig-butchering and romance-scam patterns documented elsewhere in the wiki, but with the specific feature that the leadership is geographically separated from the victim pool, likely (55–80%) reflecting both linguistic-cultural targeting choices and operational-security calculations.

No Korean agency or jurisdiction is identified in the AIT press release or in the corroborating Taiwan-side reporting. Korea (한국) is not a participating party to this operation. The case is nonetheless of interest to Korean readers because:

  • The U.S.-Taiwan MLAA (2002) is structurally analogous to the Korea-U.S. MLAT (1997). The Taiwanese precedent of an in-country prosecutor proffer interview is roughly even chance (45–55%) instructive for Korean prosecutors under the Korea-U.S. MLAT, depending on whether such on-site interviews already occur in U.S.–Korea practice.
