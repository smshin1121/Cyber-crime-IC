개요Summary

A six-month Hungarian-Romanian joint investigation into a Discord-based swatting and doxing campaign culminated in a coordinated four-location raid on 2026-01-20. The Hungarian National Bureau of Investigation (Készenléti Rendőrség Nemzeti Nyomozó Iroda — KR NNI) Cybercrime Combatting Department, the Hungarian Counter Terrorism Centre (Terrorelhárítási Központ — TEK), the Nagykanizsa Police Headquarters, and Romanian police acted simultaneously at addresses in Budapest, Nyíregyháza, Kisvárda (all Hungary), and Bihor County (Romania). Four young suspects — three Hungarian (ages 16, 18, 20) and one Romanian (age 17) — were identified and questioned. The 17-year-old Romanian suspect faces the most serious charges, including terrorcselekmény (terrorist act). All four are at liberty pending proceedings; KR NNI announced 2026-01-26 that it will consolidate the proceedings.

배경Background

Hungarian police authorities began receiving an unusual cluster of severe threat reports in mid-July 2025: callers threatened to bomb educational and religious institutions, residential buildings, and police units; named individuals were threatened with death. Each call required a substantial police mobilisation. Initial criminal proceedings were opened against the persons whose phone numbers had been used to place the calls. Subsequent investigation revealed that those phone subscribers were not the actual perpetrators — they were victims whose phone numbers had been spoofed or whose identities had been impersonated after their personal data had been harvested in Discord disputes. The pattern was identified as swatting (false police report against the victim) layered on doxing (public release of victim personal data).

Reconnaissance by the KR NNI Cybercrime Combatting Department and the Nagykanizsa Police Headquarters, supported by the Counter Terrorism Centre (TEK), eventually identified four perpetrators — three of them in Hungary and one in Romania. Romanian police were brought into the investigation once the Romanian element was confirmed.

법적 근거Legal Framework

The Hungarian charging instruments are domestic: terrorcselekmény (terrorist act), közveszéllyel fenyegetés (threatening with public danger), hamis vád (false accusation, multiple counts), and személyes adattal visszaélés (misuse of personal data). The Romanian element falls within the same Hungarian-led proceeding pursuant to Hungarian criminal jurisdiction over offences directed at Hungarian victims (effects principle), with Romanian authorities executing the local search in Bihor County and questioning the Romanian suspect on Romanian territory.

The press release does not refer to a Eurojust-coordinated joint investigation team, the European Investigation Order, or specific bilateral treaty instruments. The cooperation is most consistent with informal police cooperation supplemented by direct EU-internal mechanisms not named in the press release. Both Hungary and Romania are EU Member States and parties to the Budapest Convention; either bilateral framework was likely available but the public release does not specify which was used.

⚠️ Legal status check needed The exact procedural vehicle (Eurojust support, European Investigation Order, JIT, or purely informal police cooperation under EU-internal channels) is not stated in the police.hu release. The press release's reference to "international cooperation if necessary" reads as informal police-to-police rather than judicially-formalised JIT cooperation, but this should be confirmed against a Romanian-side primary source or a follow-up Hungarian prosecution announcement.

활용된 공조 메커니즘Cooperation Mechanisms Used

  • Direct bilateral police cooperation — Hungarian and Romanian police acting under a shared operational plan with synchronised execution at four locations across two countries.
  • Cross-border participation in search — KR NNI cyber investigators physically present at the Romanian-led Bihor County search. This is more intensive than standard MLAT-mediated evidence collection and reflects the EU-internal informal-cooperation modality available between neighbouring Member States.
  • Search and seizure of devices and electronic evidence preservation, including online-account data backups.
  • The release does not invoke Joint Investigation Team, Eurojust, Europol J-CAT, or the European Investigation Order by name, although those frameworks are available between Hungary and Romania.

과제 및 마찰 요인Challenges and Friction Points

  • Initial misattribution: phone-number-based identification initially produced criminal proceedings against the doxed victims rather than the swatters; the swatting/doxing pattern had to be understood before proceedings could be redirected.
  • Underage suspects: three of four perpetrators are minors or just-of-age; this complicates detention and evidentiary practice in both jurisdictions.
  • Discord platform evidence: dispute escalation occurred on Discord; preserving content data and account artefacts requires cooperation with the US-based platform, not described in the source.
  • Counterpart-agency naming gap: the Romanian agency is identified only generically, leaving the wiki without a tier-1 named Romanian institutional counterpart for this case.

교훈Lessons Learned

  • Bilateral EU-internal cybercrime cooperation can produce same-day, multi-country coordinated action without an explicitly Eurojust-coordinated framework, when both sides have functioning national cybercrime units and can host visiting officers.
  • Bomb-threat swatting can be charged as terrorism (terrorcselekmény) in EU jurisdictions when the underlying threats target public-safety infrastructure, justifying Counter Terrorism Centre engagement at the reconnaissance stage.
  • Discord-mediated doxing-then-swatting is a recurrent youth-cybercrime pattern with growing transnational reach in Central and Eastern Europe; investigators should expect platform-data-preservation requirements early in similar cases.
