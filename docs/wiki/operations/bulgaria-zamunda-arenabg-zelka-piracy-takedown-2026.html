교훈

  • Long-arm domain seizure as a structural enforcement mechanism: the U.S. asserted custody of three .net/.com/.org domains at the registrar level, allowing simultaneous takedown of the public-facing infrastructure regardless of where the underlying servers are physically located. This pattern complements — rather than duplicates — the Bulgarian search-and-seizure operations targeting operators in Bulgaria.
  • Capacity-building → enforcement pipeline: ICHIP Bucharest (a DOJ in-region IP/cybercrime advisor) and OPDAT/CCIPS legislative-drafting cooperation produced both the Bulgarian piracy-platform-operator statute used to charge the three individuals AND the operational coordination during the takedown — i.e., the statute under which Bulgarian operators were charged was itself co-drafted with U.S. assistance, demonstrating end-to-end legal-framework-plus-enforcement IC posture.
  • Triangulated DHS in-region attachés (HSI Athens + CBP Sofia): the operation explicitly named two DHS attachés stationed in third countries (HSI Athens in Greece, CBP Sofia in Bulgaria) as 'critical roles' in coordination and technical assistance, alongside Europol — a documented multi-attaché triangulation pattern for southern-EU cybercrime IC.
  • Bulgaria → U.S. — rare directionality for a U.S. piracy enforcement: per TorrentFreak background reporting, Bulgaria 'asked the U.S. authorities for help' more than six years prior to the takedown, an inversion of the more common U.S.-requesting-Bulgaria MLA pattern; the new Bulgarian statute (drafted with OPDAT and CCIPS assistance) was the legal precondition that finally enabled the operation.

개요Summary

On Friday, 30 January 2026 the U.S. Department of Justice's Office of Public Affairs (press release 26-82) announced the coordinated U.S.–Bulgaria takedown of three of the largest internet piracy platforms in the European Union — zamunda.net, arenabg.com, and zelka.org — allegedly operated from Bulgaria. The operation paired (i) U.S. seizure of the three U.S.-registered domains (.net/.com/.org) with (ii) Bulgarian search-and-seizure operations executed at 30 addresses across Bulgaria by the National Investigative Service (NIS), the Ministry of the Interior's General Directorate Combating Organized Crime (GDCOC/GDBOP), and the State Agency for National Security, supervised by the Sofia City Prosecutor's Office and Sofia District Prosecutor's Office.

According to the affidavits in support of the U.S. seizure warrants, the three domains "receive tens of millions of visits a year, offer thousands of infringed works, and result in millions of downloads of those works, the retail value totaling millions of dollars" — and one is "often ranked as one of the top 10 most visited domains in Bulgaria." The seized domains' content categories include movies, television shows, video games, software, e-books, and other copyrighted works belonging predominantly to U.S. companies and individuals.

Per the same-day Bulgarian News Agency (BTA) report, four individuals were detained; the DOJ ICHIP April 2026 program summary records that three individuals have been charged with online piracy and money laundering under a new Bulgarian criminal statute that criminalizes operating piracy platforms — described as "the first law of its kind in Europe, drafted with OPDAT and CCIPS assistance."

배경Background

The Bulgarian platforms zamunda.net, arenabg.com, and zelka.org were among the most popular Bulgarian-language torrent indexes for at least a decade, serving tens of millions of users with copyrighted content from U.S. and other rightsholders. Per the DOJ press release 26-82, they were assessed by the affidavits to receive "tens of millions of visits a year" and produced "considerable money from advertisements." The takedown is the culmination of cooperation that, per third-party background reporting, took root more than six years prior, when Bulgaria approached the U.S. for assistance in shutting down the country's largest torrent trackers.

The operational precondition was a new Bulgarian criminal statute that criminalizes operating piracy platforms — co-drafted with the DOJ's Office of Overseas Prosecutorial Development, Assistance and Training (OPDAT) and the Computer Crime and Intellectual Property Section (CCIPS). Per the DOJ ICHIP April 2026 program summary, this is "the first law of its kind in Europe."

법적 근거Legal Framework

  • U.S. side — Federal seizure warrants for the three U.S.-registered domains, issued in the Southern District of Mississippi. Underlying conduct charged in the U.S. is willful criminal copyright infringement; the seizure banner displayed on the domains explicitly cites willful copyright infringement as a crime.
  • Bulgarian side — Three individuals charged with online piracy and money laundering under a new Bulgarian piracy-platform-operator statute. Per the DOJ ICHIP April 2026 program summary, the statute was drafted with OPDAT and CCIPS assistance and is "the first law of its kind in Europe." (The DOJ release does not enumerate the specific Criminal Code article number; flagged as missing_fields.)
  • Cooperation channel — DOJ ICHIP program (jointly administered by OPDAT and CCIPS in partnership with the U.S. Department of State's Bureau of International Narcotics and Law Enforcement Affairs); regional advisor based in Bucharest, Romania. The ICHIP program operates as a bilateral capacity-building → in-region operational-assist conduit; here it produced both the Bulgarian statute (legislative drafting) and the operational coordination during the takedown.
📝 Translation note Bulgarian agency abbreviations are not fully standardized in the English DOJ release. The Bulgarian acronym ГДБОП transliterates as GDBOP (the more common form in Bulgarian-language sources) and is rendered as GDCOC in the DOJ press release ("General Directorate Combating Organized Crime"). The wiki uses GDBOP / GDCOC interchangeably and links to the canonical GDBOP / Bulgarian cybercrime police page.

결과 및 영향Results and Impact

  • 3 U.S.-registered domains seized (zamunda.net, arenabg.com, zelka.org) — all three placed in U.S. government custody with seizure-banner notification.
  • 30 addresses searched across Bulgaria (per BTA 2026-01-30).
  • 4 individuals detained in Bulgaria (per BTA 2026-01-30).
  • 3 individuals charged with online piracy and money laundering under the new Bulgarian piracy-platform-operator statute (per DOJ ICHIP April 2026 program summary).
  • The three domains were assessed as receiving "tens of millions of visits a year" with "millions of downloads" and "retail value totaling millions of dollars" of infringed works — the operation thus disrupted what the DOJ ICHIP summary describes as "three of the largest internet piracy platforms in the European Union."
  • Structural impact: established a precedent for the use of the new Bulgarian piracy-platform-operator statute (the first such law in Europe) — the legal framework itself is a co-product of U.S.–Bulgaria cooperation.

활용된 공조 메커니즘Cooperation Mechanisms Used

The release explicitly names a multi-channel cooperation stack:

  1. Domain seizure (U.S.) — federal seizure warrants issued in the Southern District of Mississippi against U.S.-registered .net / .com / .org domains.
  2. Search and seizure (Bulgaria) — 30 addresses; supervised by Sofia City and Sofia District Prosecutor's Offices; executed by GDCOC/GDBOP, NIS, and the State Agency for National Security.
  3. ICHIP Bucharest — DOJ in-region IP/cybercrime advisor providing "significant assistance"; cross-border capacity-building turned into operational assist.
  4. OPDAT + CCIPS legislative-drafting cooperation — the new Bulgarian piracy-platform-operator statute was co-drafted with these DOJ components prior to the takedown.
  5. Europol coordination — explicitly named with "critical role" in coordination and technical assistance.
  6. HSI Athens + CBP Sofia — DHS in-region attachés providing technical assistance during the action.
  7. IPR Center (U.S. interagency clearinghouse) — coordinated U.S.-side investigative effort.

과제 및 마찰 요인Challenges and Friction Points

  • The DOJ release does not enumerate the specific Bulgarian Criminal Code article number under which the three individuals were charged — only that it is "a new criminal statute that criminalizes operating piracy platforms" and is "the first law of its kind in Europe." Wiki records this as a missing field.
  • The U.S. side does not yet enumerate U.S. criminal charges against any defendant (the domain seizures and Bulgarian charging are publicly described, but a U.S. indictment, if any, was not referenced in the press release).
  • The operation took more than six years from the initial Bulgarian request for U.S. assistance to the action day — driven in significant part by the need to first co-draft an enabling Bulgarian statute. This is a documented data point on the legal-framework-precondition timeline for trans-Atlantic IP enforcement cooperation.

교훈Lessons Learned

  • Statute-as-cooperation-product: an unusually visible case where the foreign legal instrument used to charge defendants was itself co-drafted by the cooperating partner. This complements the more common pattern of cooperation operating within an existing legal framework.
  • DHS in-region attaché triangulation: HSI Athens (Greece) + CBP Sofia (Bulgaria) + ICHIP Bucharest (Romania) operating as a coordinated trio for southern-EU IP/cybercrime enforcement, alongside Europol.
  • Long-arm domain seizure as anchor mechanism: U.S. seizure of the .net / .com / .org domains gave the operation a public-facing takedown effect that was independent of the underlying server location.

후속 조치Follow-Up Actions

  • Public prosecution under the new Bulgarian piracy-platform-operator statute is in progress. Three individuals have been charged with online piracy and money laundering as of the DOJ ICHIP April 2026 program summary.
  • Open question: whether U.S. charging follows (the DOJ press release describes only seizure warrants, not indictments).
  • Open question: status of any extradition requests, MLA traffic, or asset-tracing follow-up.

Korean Involvement (한국의 참여)

South Korea is not named in the DOJ press release 26-82 or in the corroborating BTA same-day report. The operation is recorded in the wiki for the statute-as-cooperation-product pattern, the U.S.–Bulgaria + ICHIP-region triangulation pattern, and the DHS in-region attaché triangulation — none of which directly inform the Korean cybercrime IC posture. Korean structural relevance is therefore indirect:

  • Korean rightsholders (entertainment, software, gaming) are not enumerated as victims in the cited tier-1 sources, but the broader pattern of U.S.-led domain-seizure-plus-foreign-search action against torrent indexes is a template that has occasionally been applied to platforms hosting Korean copyrighted content in unrelated operations (see operation-404-digital-piracy-2019 for a comparable Brazilian-led IPTV/piracy template).
  • The Bulgarian piracy-platform-operator statute, as a "first law of its kind in Europe," may inform comparative analysis of how Korea's Copyright Act enforcement against platform operators compares with the new Bulgarian model.
