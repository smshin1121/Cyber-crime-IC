Operation Eclipse (CSAM Americas)

개요Summary

Operation Eclipse was an INTERPOL-coordinated operation against child sexual abuse and exploitation material across nine countries in Central America, North America, and the Caribbean. The operational window ran from February 2025 to January 2026, with public results announced on 5 March 2026.

The operation focused on identifying victims, reopening older cases, locating suspects, and synchronizing cross-border evidence. INTERPOL reported support from UNODC and the Child Rescue Coalition, with national authorities conducting arrests, searches, victim-protection measures, and extradition follow-up.

배경Background

Modus Operandi

Operation Eclipse targeted offenders engaged in the production, possession, distribution, and online facilitation of child sexual abuse material (CSAM) across the Americas. The underlying offending patterns documented by INTERPOL and partner national police releases fall into three classes: (1) contact offenders who produced original CSAM of children in their own households or social circles and circulated it to other offenders, (2) distributors moving pre-existing CSAM through Tor-based forums, encrypted messaging channels (Telegram, Signal), peer-to-peer networks, and paywalled clearnet sites, and (3) possessors and collectors identified via hash-matching, ICSE-database referrals, or seized devices recovered from arrested distributors. INTERPOL's reassessment of 57 existing Notices (Red and Blue) indicates that a substantial sub-component of the operation was the re-attribution of older Notice subjects to current locations across the nine participating jurisdictions, leveraging victim-identification specialists to re-examine archived imagery for new locational, biometric, or contextual cues.

Victim Profile and Impact

65 child victims were identified and rescued or safeguarded across the nine countries. Per INTERPOL and the national-police releases (Costa Rica, Dominican Republic, Panama), victims included previously unidentified children appearing in older abuse material (Panama, Costa Rica), as well as current contact-abuse victims identified through suspect-device seizures during the operation (Dominican Republic — two child victims in one case). Victim ages and exact circumstances are deliberately not disclosed in the tier-1 source. Beyond the 65 directly safeguarded children, the cumulative downstream protective effect on potential further victims of the 60 arrested offenders is highly likely substantial but not separately quantified.

Financial Flow

The tier-1 INTERPOL release does not attribute Operation Eclipse to a financially-motivated CSAM commercialisation scheme (e.g., paywalled subscription site, crypto-priced bespoke imagery), and no cryptocurrency seizure, payment-processor takedown, or commercial-platform takedown is reported. This is consistent with the operation's emphasis on victim identification and notice review rather than infrastructure disruption. A specific financial-flow gap note is recorded in Contradictions & Open Questions below.

Criminal Organisation Structure

Unlike RaaS, scam-call-centre, or commercial CSAM platform operations, Operation Eclipse appears to target a loosely-networked offender population rather than a hierarchical criminal organisation. Suspect-to-suspect linkages where they exist are typically forum-based or chat-group-based affinity ties (sharing of imagery and grooming techniques) rather than employment or revenue-share relationships. The Panama-Dominican Republic bilateral follow-up, which located two suspects wanted by Panama in Dominican territory and triggered extradition proceedings, is the only explicitly documented cross-border offender linkage in the public record; further inter-country linkages are roughly even chance to exist but are not disclosed for victim-protection and ongoing-investigation reasons.

Boundary and Non-Duplication Notes

This record is the 2026 INTERPOL-coordinated CSAM operation across nine countries. It is distinct from unrelated domestic operations that may also use the name "Eclipse", including a 2024 Panama domestic operation reported by Panama's public prosecutor.

The page includes only high-level, victim-protective details. It does not reproduce identifying details, images, or granular abuse descriptions.

Evidence Notes

INTERPOL is the anchor source for the operation name, timeframe, country list, 60 arrests, 65 victims, UNODC/Child Rescue Coalition support, notice review, and cross-border evidence model. La Nacion adds Costa Rica-specific arrest detail; Diario Libre adds Dominican Republic and Panama-Dominican extradition context; Peters & Peters provides an independent INTERPOL-focused legal summary of the notice review and ongoing extradition follow-up.

모순점 및 미해결 문제Contradictions & Open Questions

  • Financial-flow gap. The tier-1 INTERPOL release does not address whether any subset of the arrested offenders monetised CSAM commercially (paywalled site, crypto-priced bespoke imagery, subscription forum). The 60-arrest cohort roughly even chance includes some commercial offenders, but the public record does not distinguish them from non-commercial possessors/distributors.
  • Victim-suspect ratio. 60 arrests and 65 identified victims suggests a near-1:1 ratio, which is unusual for CSAM enforcement where possessors typically outnumber identified victims. This is highly likely an artefact of victim-identification being the operation's primary metric rather than possession arrests.
  • Per-country arrest distribution. INTERPOL does not break the 60 arrests down per country; La Nacion reports 18 in Costa Rica and Diario Libre reports 2 in Dominican Republic, leaving ~40 arrests unattributed in public sources.
  • Suspect-network topology. Whether the 60 arrested offenders form a single multi-country forum/chat network, several separate networks, or a fully fragmented set of unconnected offenders is not disclosed. Only the Panama-Dominican Republic pair is explicitly cross-jurisdictionally linked in the public record.
  • Extradition outcomes. The two Panama-issued, Dominican-located suspects were said to trigger extradition proceedings as of the 2026-03-05 announcement; final extradition outcomes are not yet in the public record.
